# Philosophy: Depth over Breadth + the CRAFT Method

← [[00 Education Framework|Back to Index]]

---

## The Core Argument

Standard educational exposure operates on breadth: the more things a child has encountered, the better prepared they are. This is wrong as a primary strategy — and specifically wrong for the targets in [[02 The Ten Targets|this framework]].

**What breadth actually produces:** recognition without understanding. A child who has "done" fifty books, twenty films, and ten museums has a large vocabulary of references and a thin layer of comprehension over all of them. Under pressure — in an argument, in a creative problem, in a moment requiring genuine reasoning — the references don't activate because they were never internalized as functional tools.

**What depth produces:** transferable analytical frameworks. A child who has studied one great film deeply — who understands who directed it, why they made specific casting decisions, what the camera is doing in a given scene and why, which moments are structurally load-bearing and which are texture — has built a mental model for *reading any film*. Every subsequent film becomes faster to understand and richer to experience because the analytical apparatus already exists.

This is the Feynman principle applied to culture: you don't understand something until you can derive it. The derivation — the reconstruction of the decisions that produced the thing — is the education.

**On content specifically:** the question is not "has my child seen Spirited Away?" but "does my child understand what Miyazaki was doing, and why, and how he did it?" The first takes two hours. The second takes years of revisiting and deepens with each return.

## The CRAFT Method

A repeatable framework for going deep on any content — film, book, game, painting, piece of music, building. Use it as a lens, not a checklist. Apply it in conversation, not as homework.

### C — Creation
*Who made this? Under what constraints? With what tools and technology?*

- Who are the authors/directors/designers? What is their background and history?
- What technology was available? What was new? What was impossible before?
- What were the production constraints — budget, time, censorship, audience expectation?
- Why this form? Why a film and not a book? Why three volumes and not one?

**Example (Avatar: The Last Airbender):** Bryan Konietzko and Michael DiMartino pitched it to Nickelodeon as a single unified story — unusual for Western animation at the time. They hired martial arts consultants to design each bending style as a distinct discipline (Tai Chi for Waterbending, Hung Gar for Earthbending, Northern Shaolin for Firebending, Ba Gua for Airbending). The constraint of a children's network forced them to handle war and genocide obliquely — which made the result more powerful, not weaker.

### R — Reasoning
*What key decisions were made? What alternatives existed? Why this and not that?*

- Every significant element is a choice. What are the alternatives the creator rejected?
- Why was this character cast this way? Why does the story end here and not earlier?
- What problems was the creator solving? What constraints were they working within?
- Where did the creator change their mind mid-creation, and why?

**Example (Star Wars, 1977):** Lucas shot in Tunisia and Guatemala for location, not stage, because he wanted texture that couldn't be faked. He hired John Williams for an orchestral score when synthesizers were standard — because he wanted the emotional gravity of a classic epic. He re-edited the Death Star battle sequence fourteen times. Each of these is a reasoning question: why? What would the alternative have produced?

### A — Architecture
*What is the structural skeleton? How is it built? What are the load-bearing elements?*

- What is the three-act structure? Where are the act breaks?
- Which scenes are structurally necessary? Which are texture?
- How does the information flow? What does the audience know and when?
- In visual works: what does the camera do? How does light work? What does color mean?
- In books: how does sentence rhythm vary? Why short chapters vs. long?

**Example (Calvin & Hobbes):** Bill Watterson used the Sunday strip format as an entirely different structural unit from dailies — he fought syndicate pressure to lock panel layouts, preserving compositional freedom. The strips operate on two levels simultaneously: literal (what Calvin and Hobbes are doing) and philosophical (what it means). The architecture is deceptively simple — single-page, four-panel dailies — but the compression required is extreme. Every word chosen is the only word that works.

### F — Function
*What is this trying to do to the audience? How does it achieve it? Where does it succeed or fail?*

- What emotional state is the creator trying to produce?
- What ideas are being transmitted, and how — directly or through character, metaphor, structure?
- What does the audience leave with that they didn't arrive with?
- Where does it fail? Why? What would have fixed it?

**Example (Richard Scarry):** The function is cognitive mapping of social systems. A child encountering *What Do People Do All Day?* is being introduced to division of labor, interdependence, and systemic cause-and-effect — through visual density rather than explanation. The function is to make visible what is normally invisible: that bread requires a baker who requires a miller who requires a farmer who requires tools made by a blacksmith. Scarry achieves this through illustration density and humor rather than narrative.

### T — Trace
*What influenced this? What did it influence? Where do you see its DNA in other works?*

- What was the creator responding to or building on?
- What came after this that wouldn't exist without it?
- How has it been misread, misused, or distorted in its legacy?
- What other works share this structure/approach/philosophy?

**Example (Dune, Herbert 1965):** Herbert was responding to the "competent hero" archetype in science fiction — his explicit goal was to create a story about the dangers of charismatic leadership and messiah narratives. The ecology of Arrakis was built from his study of Oregon sand dunes and the interconnection of moisture, plants, and terrain. Its trace: Star Wars borrowed heavily (desert planet, chosen one, mystical force) while stripping out the political critique — making the Trace conversation interesting because the successor inverted the original's argument.

## Breadth vs. Depth: The Practical Decision Rule

**The rule:** go deep on 2–3 anchor universes per developmental phase. Use the CRAFT method on each. Allow unlimited adjacent exploration from within those anchors — but don't mistake adjacency for depth.

**Why not more anchors:** cognitive load, and diminishing returns. A child who deeply understands the narrative architecture of Avatar has more tools for understanding any subsequent story than a child who has watched twenty shows at surface level. The frameworks transfer. The references don't.

**Why not fewer:** developmental range matters. A single anchor per phase risks misalignment with the child's actual interests at that moment. Two to three provides optionality without dilution.

**On the Engagement Progression Atlas:** the Atlas is a menu of options — valuable as a resource for what exists and what's age-appropriate. This framework selects from it rather than delivering it wholesale. See [[04 Content Curriculum]] for the curated selections and the reasoning for why specific alternatives were rejected.

## How the Three Layers Connect

```
VIRTUES & SKILLS          MENTAL MODELS             CONTENT
(who they become)         (how they reason)         (what they engage with)

Self-Efficacy      ←→    Cause & Effect       ←→   Richard Scarry (1-3)
First-Principles   ←→    Map vs Territory     ←→   Feynman Lectures (14+)
Obsessive Focus    ←→    Circle of Competence ←→   Warhammer (11-13)
Grit               ←→    Feedback Loops       ←→   Chess / Go (8+)
Verbal Intelligence←→    Inversion            ←→   Debate / Improv (11+)
```

Content is not decoration. When chosen well and engaged with deeply via CRAFT, each universe simultaneously builds virtues and installs mental models. The three layers are not parallel programs — they are one program viewed from three angles.

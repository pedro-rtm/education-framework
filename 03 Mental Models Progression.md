## What Mental Models Are

Mental models are cognitive frameworks — simplified representations of how something works — that allow you to reason about reality accurately without having to process everything from scratch. Munger's key insight: a *latticework* of models from multiple disciplines (physics, biology, psychology, economics, history) produces judgment that no single model can. The models reinforce each other and reveal patterns across domains.

**This is distinct from first-principles reasoning** (Target 5), though they're related. First-principles reasoning is the *method* — going down to axioms and rebuilding. Mental models are the *accumulated results* of doing that across many domains. A child who reasons from first principles over years builds a model latticework. You can also deliberately accelerate this by naming and introducing models explicitly.

**The developmental constraint is real.** Abstract models require abstract reasoning, which requires prefrontal development that isn't complete until the mid-20s. The progression below respects this: early models are concrete and experiential; abstract and probabilistic models come later. Introducing Bayesian updating to a 6-year-old is not developmental acceleration — it's noise.

## Ages 1–3: Foundational Cognition
*Models at this stage are pre-linguistic and experiential. They are built through action and consequence, not words.*

| Model | What it is | How it's built |
|---|---|---|
| **Cause and Effect** | "When I do X, Y happens. Reliably." | Dominoes, stacking towers, water pouring, any toy with direct physical consequence |
| **Object Permanence** | Things exist even when you can't see them | Peek-a-boo, hide objects under blankets, find the hidden toy |
| **Iteration** | "That didn't work. Try differently." | Don't rescue immediately. Narrate: "That didn't fit. What else could we try?" |
| **Categorization** | Things have properties and can be grouped | Shape sorters, color sorting, "find all the round ones" |
| **Scale / Size** | Some things are bigger than others; relationships exist | Grimm's Rainbow, nesting cups, Duplo |

**Content that installs these models:** Richard Scarry (systems, categorization, scale), Where's Wally (search strategies, visual categorization), Duplo and stacking toys (cause-effect, iteration).

## Ages 4–7: Early Causal and Social Models
*Language is now a tool for reasoning. Models become discussable.*

| Model | What it is | How to introduce |
|---|---|---|
| **Inversion** | "What would happen if the opposite were true?" | Fairy tale analysis: "What if the wolf was trying to help?" "What would happen if nobody followed any rules?" Bedtime "what if?" questions. |
| **Incentives** | "Why does the character want that? What does the villain actually want?" | Any story analysis. "Why did they make that choice?" Not rhetorical — actually reason it out together. |
| **Systems / Interdependence** | A town is a system. Each part depends on others. | Richard Scarry field trips (post office, bakery, fire station). "What would happen if the baker didn't bake? Then what?" |
| **Circularity / Feedback** | Some effects loop back to causes | Plant growth cycles, weather, "if you water it every day, it grows, and then what?" |
| **Simple Probability** | Some things are more likely than others | Games with dice. "Which number do you think will come up most?" Don't over-explain; plant the concept. |

**Content that installs these models:** Avatar: The Last Airbender (incentives, systems — why does the Fire Nation want to conquer?), Calvin & Hobbes (inversion — Calvin constantly inverts social norms and reasons about the consequences), Studio Ghibli (interdependence with nature).

**Specific activity:** At dinner, pick one character from whatever they're watching and ask: "What does [character] actually want? Why? What would you do if you wanted that?" This builds incentive analysis as a reflex.

## Ages 8–10: Structural and Consequential Models
*Abstract reasoning begins. Models can now be named and discussed explicitly.*

| Model | What it is | How to introduce |
|---|---|---|
| **First and Second-Order Consequences** | "What happens next? And what happens *because of that*?" | Chess (every move has cascading consequences). "We cut down all the trees. Then what? And then what?" |
| **Feedback Loops** | Positive (amplifying) and negative (stabilizing) loops | Minecraft fire spreading (positive loop). Thermostat (negative loop). Predator-prey populations. |
| **Opportunity Cost** | Choosing this closes the door on that | "You chose soccer this afternoon, so we couldn't do the museum. Was that the right trade?" Then generalize. |
| **Map vs. Territory** | The model is not the thing. All models are wrong; some are useful. | "The word 'dog' doesn't bite." History books are maps, not reality. Scientific models are tools, not truth. |
| **Occam's Razor** | Among competing explanations, prefer the simpler — but don't oversimplify | Why do we believe the Earth orbits the Sun? What's the simplest explanation that fits all the evidence? |
| **Inversion (advanced)** | Solve by removing the problem, not adding a solution | "Instead of trying to make the project better, what would make it worse? Now don't do those things." |

**Content that installs these models:** Star Wars (second-order consequences — what happens after the Death Star is destroyed?), Minecraft (feedback loops, systems), chess (opportunity cost, second-order thinking).

**Named introduction:** At this age you can start naming models explicitly. "That's called opportunity cost — every choice closes other doors. Economists think about everything this way."

## Ages 11–13: Probabilistic and Epistemic Models
*Abstract reasoning fully available. Can engage with models as models — tools with limits.*

| Model | What it is | How to introduce |
|---|---|---|
| **Probabilistic Thinking** | Not certain/uncertain binary — degrees of likelihood. Almost nothing is 100%. | Poker, weather forecasting, sports statistics. "What's the probability of that? Not 'will it happen' — what's your actual estimate?" |
| **Survivorship Bias** | We see successes; we don't see failures. Our sample is not representative. | "Why do we think startups are glamorous? We only hear about the ones that worked." The Abraham Wald airplane armor story — essential. |
| **Regression to the Mean** | Extreme events are followed by less extreme ones — not because of any intervention, but because extremity is rare | Sports performance spikes and drops. Test score outliers. "When a player has a great season and then declines, is it because they 'lost it' or because the great season was unusual?" |
| **Circle of Competence** | Know where your competence ends. The edge matters as much as the center. | "What do you actually understand vs. what are you pretending to understand? It's not shameful to not know — it's shameful to pretend." |
| **Hanlon's Razor** | Don't attribute to malice what can be explained by incompetence or ignorance | Conflict resolution, history analysis. "Before assuming they were evil, could they have just been wrong? Stupid? Misinformed?" |
| **Confirmation Bias** | We seek evidence that confirms what we already believe and discard contradicting evidence | Science history: how long did people believe bloodletting worked? "What would you have to see to change your mind about this?" |
| **Compounding** | Small consistent advantages accumulate into massive differences over time | Investing as the clearest example. But also: reading 30 minutes a day vs. not. Practicing an instrument. "What does this look like in 10 years?" |

**Content that installs these models:** Lord of the Rings (the survivorship bias of history — who writes it, whose stories survive), Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood (equivalent exchange as a model with limits, regression to the mean in power dynamics), Warhammer as a deep system with probability built in.

**Key conversation trigger:** History class is a survivorship bias machine. "Who wrote this? Why did their version survive? What were the other versions?" This turns any history assignment into an epistemic model exercise.

## Ages 14–16: The Munger Lattice Begins
*First-principles reasoning has been practiced long enough to produce real models. Time to name the framework explicitly.*

| Model | What it is | How to introduce |
|---|---|---|
| **Bayesian Updating** | Prior belief + new evidence = updated belief. Formally, not just intuitively. | "Before you saw that evidence, what did you believe and how strongly? After seeing it, what do you believe now and how strongly? Did your update match the strength of the evidence?" |
| **Via Negativa / Inversion** | Add value by removing what's wrong, not only by adding what's right. The power of not doing things. | Nassim Taleb. Munger: "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there." Medical: most interventions cause harm; sometimes the right move is nothing. |
| **Principal-Agent Problem** | When the agent's incentives diverge from the principal's, the agent serves themselves. | Politics, business, medicine, law — it's everywhere. "Who is the agent here? What are their actual incentives? Are those the same as the principal's?" |
| **Comparative Advantage** | Do what you're *relatively* better at, not absolutely better at. | Ricardo's trade theory. Personal application: "You don't have to be the best at this in the world — you have to be the best at the combination of this and something else that makes you unique." |
| **Emergence** | System behavior is not predictable from component behavior alone. | Ant colonies, markets, language, cities. "You can know every ant perfectly and still not predict the colony." |
| **Lindy Effect** | Things that have survived a long time tend to survive longer. | "Why read Seneca instead of a modern self-help book? Because Seneca has survived 2,000 years of scrutiny. The modern book hasn't survived 2 years." |
| **Asymmetric Risk** | The cost of being wrong is not symmetric in both directions. Know which direction is catastrophic. | "What's the worst case if you're wrong? Is that recoverable? Never risk ruin for a marginal gain." |

**Resource:** Charlie Munger's "Poor Charlie's Almanack" — read together and discuss. Not as a book report; as a live conversation about which models they already use and which are new.

## Ages 17–18: Synthesis and Meta-Models
*Models are now tools to be combined, stress-tested, and extended. The goal is building original frameworks.*

| Model | What it is | How to engage |
|---|---|---|
| **Falsifiability (Popper)** | A theory is only scientific if evidence could, in principle, prove it wrong. | "What would have to be true for this to be false? If nothing could disprove it, it's not a theory — it's a belief." |
| **Paradigm Shifts (Kuhn)** | Science doesn't progress by accumulation — it progresses through revolutionary restructuring of frameworks. | "What did scientists believe before Copernicus? Were they stupid? No — they had a coherent model that fit the evidence they had. What made it shift?" |
| **Gödel's Incompleteness** | Every sufficiently powerful formal system contains truths it cannot prove within itself. | Introduce as a limit on reason itself, not just mathematics. "Even the best model has truths it can't reach from inside." |
| **Model Failure Analysis** | Every model has edges where it fails. Knowing the failure modes is as important as knowing the model. | "In what conditions does this model give the wrong answer? What does it assume that might not be true?" |
| **Original Model Building** | Create your own frameworks for recurring problems you face. | "You've seen this pattern before. What's the simplest model that explains it? What are its assumptions? Where would it fail?" |

**The meta-skill:** By 17–18, the goal is not that they know Munger's models — it's that they have internalized the *habit* of asking "what model am I using here, is it the right one, and where does it fail?" That habit, applied consistently, is more valuable than any specific model.

## The Munger Lattice — Quick Reference

From Charlie Munger's framework, the models every educated person should have:

**From Mathematics:** Compounding, probability, permutations, decision trees
**From Physics:** Critical mass, feedback systems, inertia, thermodynamics metaphors
**From Biology:** Evolution, natural selection, niche, ecosystem, adaptation
**From Psychology:** Confirmation bias, social proof, incentive-caused bias, loss aversion, availability heuristic
**From Economics:** Opportunity cost, comparative advantage, supply/demand, incentives, tragedy of the commons
**From History:** Survivorship bias, great man vs. systems causation, unintended consequences
**From Philosophy:** Inversion, Occam's Razor, falsifiability, map vs. territory

The goal is not to memorize this list. The goal is to have *functional access* to each — to actually use them when encountering a new problem.

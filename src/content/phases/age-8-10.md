<h1>Age 8–10: Structural Thinking</h1>
<p class="subtitle">Abstract reasoning begins in earnest. Models can be named and discussed explicitly. The niche mastery commitment should now be deep.</p>

<div class="note-box">
<h3>Sub-Phases</h3>
<p><strong>8–9:</strong> Reading fluency is established. Can sustain chapter books (Harry Potter). Chess and strategy games become genuinely engaging. First real code (Scratch). The niche mastery activity should now be 3+ years in — the plateau arrives and tests commitment. First competition experiences.</p>
<p><strong>9–10:</strong> Abstract reasoning unlocks model naming. Can discuss "opportunity cost" and "feedback loops" explicitly. Python transition from Scratch. First build projects that work. Pre-adolescent identity begins. Independence in daily routine management increases significantly.</p>
</div>

<div class="priority-box">
<h3>Priorities — Your 20 Hours/Week</h3>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">1</span><strong>Niche mastery — deep investment.</strong> By now the domain should be clear. Resource it: better teacher, competitions, exposure to people who are excellent at it.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">2</span><strong>Reading together → reading alongside.</strong> Transition from reading aloud to shared reading. Same book, discussed at dinner. Start Harry Potter, then Star Wars extended universe.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">3</span><strong>Mental model naming.</strong> Start using the terms: "That's opportunity cost." "That's a feedback loop." Name the thinking tools explicitly.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">4</span><strong>First build project.</strong> Scratch → Python. Or physical: woodworking, electronics. Something that WORKS and does a thing.</div>
</div>

<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">2</span><h4>Agency & Moral Reasoning</h4><p>Expand decision space. <div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">3</span><h4>Self-Awareness & Self-Regulation</h4><p>Journaling shifts from emotions to patterns: "When do I do my best work? What drains me?" Introduce different cognitive styles. Competition losses are the curriculum — debrief analytically. Reflective journal and temperament self-assessment conversations.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">4</span><h4>Deep Curiosity</h4><p>The niche should be producing flow states regularly. Shift from parent-led to child-led investigations. Minecraft, KSP, Scratch → Pygame, electronics kits, woodworking. <div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">5</span><h4>First-Principles Reasoning</h4><p>Science experiments with prediction → test → explain. "What do you think will happen? Why? Were you right? Why not?" AoPS Intro courses, Thames & Kosmos experiment kits, Scratch programming.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">6</span><h4>Communication Mastery</h4><p>Debate practice — "Argue the other side." Short essays on topics they care about: real arguments, not school essays. Harry Potter discussion for ethical dilemmas and character motivation. Debate club, family "argue the opposite" game at dinner.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">7</span><h4>Relational Intelligence</h4><p>Team sport is now serious. Experience being captain AND being benched. Group dynamics awareness: "Who was the leader? How did they become the leader?" Team sport competition, study groups, practice partners.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">8</span><h4>Compassion & Kindness</h4><p>Sustained local service — same place repeatedly, so they see the system. "Why are these people here? What would fix this?" Recurring volunteer commitment, <a href="#s-resources">Horrible Histories</a> for empathy through historical perspective.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">9</span><h4>Physical System Mastery</h4><p>Multi-day hikes or camping. Cold water swimming (voluntary, modeled). Martial arts intensity increases. <a href="#s-resources">Backpacking gear</a> (child-sized), multi-day family camping trips, martial arts competition if ready.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">10</span><h4>Adaptability</h4><p>History: "Who wrote this? Why did their version survive? What were the other versions?" <a href="#s-resources">Horrible Histories</a> and "Why?" discussions about historical narratives.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">11</span><h4>Creative Production</h4><p>Scratch projects, physical builds, writing, and selling — first shipped work. "I made that" is the standard.</p></div>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Competitions may be draining even when successful. Protect recovery time after tournaments/performances. Solo mastery activities (coding, chess puzzles, instrument practice) should be the majority of their skill-building time.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Team-based competitions (debate, robotics, team chess). Study groups and practice partners. The social dimension of mastery becomes a motivator — use it.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Competition exposure should be gradual. Start with low-stakes events. Cold exposure remains voluntary — never coerced, never publicly compared to siblings. The analytical failure debrief must be genuinely curious, not interrogative.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>Increase challenge intensity faster — they can handle high-stakes competition and blunt feedback without lasting distress. Channel the appetite for stimulation into structured risk: harder opponents, tighter deadlines, physical challenges that push limits.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>The niche mastery commitment is natural territory. Let them go as deep as they want — extra practice, advanced material, self-directed study. Guard against adults who try to "broaden" a child who is thriving in depth.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Rotation is fine, but introduce the concept of commitment: "You can try new things AND keep one thing going." Require at least one activity sustained across the full year. The breadth instinct is valuable — it just needs an anchor.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>This child is ready for age 11-13 communication work: formal essay structure (thesis, evidence, conclusion), structured debate with research, and presentation to audiences outside the family. Push L2 Spanish toward academic register — book reports, simple essays, structured arguments in Spanish. Introduce persuasive writing: "Write something that changes my mind." Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's rhetoric and argumentation curriculum. Praise the strength of the argument's structure, not the vocabulary deployed.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-dev"><div class="t-label">Communication: Developing</div>Scaffold essay writing with templates: topic sentence, three supporting points, conclusion. Accept shorter output with clearer structure over longer, disorganised text. Oral presentation can substitute for written work where the goal is understanding, not writing skill. Debate exercises should start with topics they care about deeply before moving to assigned positions. Reading aloud together (shared book at dinner) builds the language patterns that writing eventually draws on.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>Accelerate into the age 11-13 problem-solving toolkit: independent science experiments with hypothesis-test-document cycles, Scratch projects with self-defined requirements, and Kerbal Space Program missions with specific orbital objectives. Multi-step mathematical reasoning (AoPS problems requiring 4-5 logical steps) is accessible. Praise the elegance of the solution path, not just getting the right answer.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>Break complex problems into visible sub-problems. In Scratch, build one sprite's behaviour before combining two. In science experiments, isolate one variable at a time. Provide worked examples they can modify rather than blank-slate creation. Kerbal Space Program in sandbox mode (unlimited fuel, no failure consequences) lets them experiment without the cognitive load of resource management. No time pressure on problem-solving tasks — rushing penalises the methodical thinker.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-gm-adv"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Advanced</div>Sport specialisation is viable — the child can begin position-specific training, competition-level martial arts, and longer endurance challenges (multi-day hikes, open-water swimming). Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's strength training introduction and competitive sport escalation. Physical challenge should now include strategic elements: bouldering route-reading, martial arts sparring tactics, team sport positioning. Praise the strategic body use, not just the physical output.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-dev"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Developing</div>Continue fundamental movement work alongside sport participation. A child who hasn't fully mastered running mechanics, throwing accuracy, or balance shouldn't be pushed into competitive sport — it compounds frustration. Swimming instruction with technique focus, non-competitive martial arts with emphasis on form, and hiking at moderate distances build the base. Physical confidence precedes physical performance; protect the former while building the latter.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>Precision tasks escalate: miniature painting (Citadel starter kits), detailed model-making, electronics soldering (supervised), and musical instrument technique at Grade 3+ level. Technical drawing and sketch-noting become accessible. Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's calligraphy, detailed illustration, and lab notebook work. Praise the precision and patience invested, not the natural dexterity.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Handwriting may still be effortful — consider keyboard proficiency as a parallel track so that fine motor challenges don't gate written expression. Touch typing (Typing Club, Keybr) is a fine motor exercise itself. Art and building projects should use materials with forgiving tolerances: clay over origami, large LEGO over small Technic pins. The goal is consistent practice with increasing precision, not perfection.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-social-adv"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Advanced</div>This child navigates peer dynamics and reads social cues ahead of age. Push toward leadership development: team captain roles, mentoring younger children, organising group activities. Introduce explicit social observation: "Notice how the group changed when that person spoke up — what did they do differently?" Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's networking and social capital concepts. Peer pressure resistance is worth discussing explicitly — they'll encounter it before less socially advanced peers. Praise the social awareness, not the social status.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-dev"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Developing</div>Peer pressure dynamics are harder to navigate. Provide explicit frameworks: "If someone asks you to do something and your stomach feels wrong, that's a signal." Role-play peer pressure scenarios at home. Team sport participation should be in supportive environments with coaches who manage group dynamics actively. Smaller group sizes (futsal over 11-a-side, quartet over orchestra) reduce the social complexity to a manageable level.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>Sustained focus in classroom settings comes naturally. Challenge with longer independent work periods, multi-session projects requiring delayed gratification, and strategic games where the best move requires ignoring the obvious one (chess, Go). Introduce the metacognitive concept: "Your impulse was X — what would happen if you followed it vs. waited?" This makes inhibition a conscious, nameable tool. Praise the discipline of sustained attention, not just the output it produces.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-dev"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Developing</div>Classroom focus is a challenge — work with teachers on environmental supports (preferential seating, movement breaks, fidget tools). At home, break study sessions into 15-minute blocks with physical movement between them. Reduce distractions in the study environment to the minimum: one task, one surface, one timer. Games that train sustained attention (jigsaw puzzles, model building with instructions) build the muscle without the pressure of academic performance.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>Subject-switching is smooth — they can move from math to writing to music practice without extended transition friction. Challenge with tasks requiring rapid strategy shifts: debate where they must argue the opposite side, coding projects where requirements change mid-build, and multi-rule games with evolving conditions. Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's interdisciplinary thinking. Praise the cognitive flexibility itself, not just task completion.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-dev"><div class="t-label">Shift: Developing</div>Transitions between subjects and activities need explicit support. Build a consistent homework sequence so the order is predictable (e.g., always math first, then reading, then creative work). Use a physical signal for transitions (timer, bell, change of location). When switching between tasks is difficult, allow a 2-minute "bridge" activity (stretching, getting water) rather than demanding an instant gear change.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-adv"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Advanced</div>This child self-regulates under pressure — competition losses, performance anxiety, and social friction are processed without prolonged disruption. Introduce more sophisticated regulation strategies: cognitive reappraisal ("What's another way to think about this situation?"), pre-performance routines, and stress inoculation ("Let's practice being nervous and performing anyway"). Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's mindfulness and advanced emotional metacognition. Praise the regulation strategy used, not the absence of the emotion.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-dev"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Developing</div>Competition and performance contexts may trigger emotional overwhelm. Build a pre-event routine (breathing, visualisation, familiar music) that becomes an automatic calming sequence. After difficult events, debrief the emotion first ("That was really frustrating") before analysing the performance. Journaling about emotions — even three sentences — begins to create distance between feeling and reacting. The amygdala hijack explanation is especially useful: "Your alarm went off before your thinking brain caught up."</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>Multi-step math problems, multi-source research projects, and complex building plans from memory are all accessible. Introduce note-taking as a working memory extension tool — not because they need it yet, but because the habit compounds. Challenge with mental math (2-digit multiplication without paper), chess blindfold exercises (visualise 2-3 moves ahead), and cooking from a recipe read once. Praise holding complexity in mind across time, not just immediate recall.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>Externalise aggressively: written task lists, graphic organisers for research projects, step-by-step checklists for multi-part assignments. Teach note-taking as a survival skill, not an academic exercise — "Write down what you need to remember so your brain can think about other things." Spaced repetition (Anki) is especially valuable here, offloading memorisation to a system. Math problems benefit from showing all work on paper, using the page as external working memory.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-plan-adv"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Advanced</div>This child can manage their own project timelines. Introduce backwards planning: "The science fair is in four weeks — what needs to be done by when?" Weekly planning conversations shift from parent-led to child-led: they propose the schedule, you refine. Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's time management systems and long-term goal setting. Praise the quality and realism of the plan, not just plan compliance.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-dev"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Developing</div>Provide planning templates: a weekly planner with time blocks, a project checklist with deadlines pre-set, a daily homework sequence posted at their desk. Co-plan rather than expecting independent planning — sit together and map out the week. Break long-term projects into daily micro-tasks with visible progress tracking (sticker chart, checklist). Planning is a teachable skill that develops through scaffolded practice, not natural maturation.</div>

<h2 id="s-communication" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">record_voice_over</span>Communication & Expression</h2>

<h3>Language — Written & Oral</h3>
<div class="music-track language-track">
<div>
<h4>Written Track</h4>
<p>Weekly writing practice: one short essay or story. Start with a nature journal — observe, describe, reflect. Book reports evolve into book arguments: "I think X because..." rather than "This book is about..." Apply the engagement cycle to their own writing: "Who is your audience? What are you trying to do to them? How is your piece structured?"</p>
<p>The standard: if they can't write it clearly, they don't understand it yet. Writing is not a school subject — it's the mechanism by which thinking becomes precise.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h4>Oral &amp; Performance Track</h4>
<p><strong>Presentations.</strong> One 3-5 minute presentation per month on something they've learned or care about. Structure it: opening hook, three points, conclusion. Deliver to the family. Feedback on clarity and delivery, not just content. By 10, comfortable speaking to a group for five minutes without notes.</p>
<p><strong>Reading aloud.</strong> Read from novels with expression and character voices. Take turns with a parent. The goal shifts from comprehension to performance — can they make the text come alive for a listener?</p>
<p><strong>Debate basics.</strong> Family "argue the opposite" game formalizes: pick a topic, assign sides, 2 minutes each, then switch. This trains oral argumentation under time pressure — a fundamentally different skill from writing an essay.</p>
<p><strong>First improv games.</strong> "Yes, and..." exercises. One Word Story. Conducted Story (parent points, child speaks; point away, child stops). These build comfort with verbal spontaneity and the skill of not knowing what you'll say next.</p>
</div>
</div>

<h3>Negotiation & Sales</h3>
<p><strong>Family negotiation practice.</strong> Bedtime, screen time, allowance, activity choices — all negotiable within principles. "State your position. State theirs. Find the overlap. What can you offer?" Every family conflict is a negotiation exercise.</p>
<p><strong>Selling their work.</strong> Lemonade stand, selling art, tutoring younger kids. The lesson: money comes from solving someone else's problem. "Convince me to buy this. Why should I care?"</p>
<p><strong>Persuasion awareness.</strong> After any compelling ad, trailer, or pitch: "Why did that work on you? What technique did they use?" Cialdini's principles (reciprocity, scarcity, social proof) at age-appropriate level.</p>

<h3>Digital Creation</h3>
<p><strong>Photography as composition.</strong> Framing, light, what to include/exclude. Weekly photo challenge: "capture something that shows [concept]."</p>
<p><strong>Video basics.</strong> Short films with phone/tablet. Edit in CapCut or iMovie. Storyboard → shoot → edit → share. A shipped 2-minute video teaches more about communication than a month of essays.</p>
<p><strong>Presentation design.</strong> When they present at home (monthly 3-5 min presentations), they design slides. Visual communication starts here — "Does this slide help the audience understand, or does it just have words on it?"</p>

<h3>Visual & Spatial</h3>
<p>Data visualization literacy: read and create simple charts (bar, line, pie). "What story does this graph tell? What does it hide?" Photography as observation tool — composition, framing, what you choose to include/exclude. Mind mapping for projects. Sketch-noting: taking notes as a mix of words and drawings — this is often the unlock that makes academic material accessible. Push toward visual communication as a stretch alongside essay writing and debate: infographics, sketch-noting, presentation design.</p>

<h3>Language Milestones</h3>
<p><strong>L1+L2:</strong> Both languages at academic level — can write essays, argue positions, and read grade-level texts in both. The minority language (English) may lag the dominant (Spanish) by 1-2 years in academic register — this is normal and closes with continued reading. By 10: comfortable switching between languages mid-conversation without effort.</p>
<p><strong>L3:</strong> Basic conversational ability after 2-3 years of exposure. Can introduce themselves, order food, describe their day. Not fluent — functional.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Written expression is often stronger than oral. Let writing be the primary mode — essays, journals, stories. Presentations should start with family-only audiences before expanding. Improv games may need a warm-up period; don't force participation before they're ready.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Oral performance is a natural strength — lean into debate, improv, and presentations. The challenge is written precision: they'll want to talk instead of write. Pair writing assignments with oral discussion first, then require the written version to match the clarity of what they said aloud.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>Sketch-noting and mind mapping are the unlock for focused, detail-oriented children. Let them draw their arguments before writing them. Video creation and photography will be stronger expression channels than essays. Storyboarding before writing a story converts spatial thinking into narrative structure.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Public speaking anxiety is common. Build exposure gradually: present to one parent, then both parents, then extended family, then a small group. Never surprise them with an audience. The "argue the opposite" game should avoid topics they feel morally strong about until they're comfortable with the format.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>Push into age 11-13 territory: formal debate with researched evidence, essay writing with cited sources, and presentations to non-family audiences (class, club, community group). L2 Spanish writing can include opinion essays and structured book arguments. Introduction to rhetoric: "What makes this speech convincing? What technique is the author using?" Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's rhetoric curriculum. Praise the persuasive architecture, not the verbal fluency.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-dev"><div class="t-label">Communication: Developing</div>Oral expression may outpace written expression significantly. Let them dictate essays and then edit the transcript — this separates idea generation from the motor task of writing. Presentation structure should be provided as a template (hook, three points, conclusion) rather than discovered independently. Improv games should be low-stakes and playful; the goal is comfort with verbal spontaneity, not performance quality. Pair every written assignment with an oral warm-up discussion.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>In debate and presentation contexts, this child can resist the impulse to speak over others, wait for the right moment to make a point, and hold back their strongest argument for maximum effect. Introduce strategic communication timing: "When in the conversation would this point land hardest?" This is inhibitory control applied to rhetoric. Praise the restraint that makes the communication more effective.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-dev"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Developing</div>Speaking over others in group discussions and blurting answers before questions are finished are inhibition challenges, not rudeness. Use structured turn-taking formats: a physical token passed between speakers, a timer per person, or a raised-hand rule. Written expression may be a better channel for complex arguments because the pace is self-controlled. Keep group discussion sizes small (3-4 people) to reduce the inhibitory demand.</div>

<h2 id="s-music" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">music_note</span>Music</h2>
<div class="music-track">
<div>
<h4>Classical Track</h4>
<p>Progressing through graded repertoire. Sight-reading developing. Theory understanding: keys, scales, intervals, basic harmony.</p>
<p>ABRSM or equivalent exams — external milestones. Target: Grade 3–4 by age 10.</p>
<p>First ensemble: school orchestra, recital participation, chamber group if available.</p>
<p>Practice: 30–45 min daily.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h4>Jazz / Improv Track</h4>
<p>Introduction to jazz vocabulary: blues scale, 12-bar blues structure, basic chord progressions (I-IV-V).</p>
<p>Transcription begins: listen to a simple melody, play it back by ear.</p>
<p>Improvisation exercises: play over a backing track, any notes at first, then constrained to a scale.</p>
<p>Exposure: listen to Miles Davis (Kind of Blue), Bill Evans, Chet Baker — accessible entry points.</p>

<p><strong>The dual-track payoff:</strong> By 10, they should be able to both read a piece from notation AND make up a melody on the spot. Most musicians their age can do one or the other. This combination is rare and compounds.</p>
<p><strong>⚡ Window narrowing:</strong> If formal music instruction hasn't started by 8, the ear training window is narrowing. Start now with the best private instructor available. It's harder but not impossible — deliberate ear training exercises can partially compensate.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Solo practice and private lessons are the core. Ensemble participation is valuable but draining — limit to one ensemble commitment. Recitals may trigger performance anxiety; prepare with low-stakes living-room concerts first.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Ensemble playing is the motivator. Prioritize group music-making — school orchestra, chamber group, jazz combo. Solo practice discipline may need external structure (practice partner, timed sessions with a parent nearby) to compensate for the social energy they crave.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Music is often a deep emotional channel for these children. They may connect intensely with pieces and resist moving on. ABRSM exams can feel high-pressure — frame them as milestones, not judgments. Performance anxiety requires specific desensitization: record at home, play for one person, then gradually expand the audience.</div>

<h2 id="s-mind-body" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">fitness_center</span>Mind & Body</h2>

<h3>Physical System</h3>
<p>"Your brain and body are one system." Introduce the connection explicitly. Breathing exercises before tests. Physical warm-up before study sessions — even 10 minutes of movement measurably improves subsequent focus. Sleep is introduced as a cognitive tool: "You don't just rest when you sleep — your brain practices what you learned today."</p>
<p><strong>Sleep architecture — teach it.</strong> By 10, they should understand WHY sleep matters: "Your brain replays and consolidates everything you learned today. Skimp on sleep and yesterday's practice is wasted." Make it their own responsibility, backed by understanding.</p>
<p><strong>Exercise as cognitive tool.</strong> "Run before you study. Your brain works measurably better after 30 minutes of cardio." Make this a self-managed habit, not a parental rule.</p>

<h3>Self-Awareness & Emotional Intelligence</h3>
<p>Explain the amygdala hijack simply: "Your brain's alarm system goes off before your thinking brain catches up. That's why you say things in anger you don't mean." Journaling as regulation — writing about emotions reduces their intensity (Pennebaker's research). Breathing techniques as a learnable, practicable skill: box breathing (4-4-4-4) before tests, performances, difficult conversations. Identify personal triggers: "What situations reliably make you angry/anxious? Knowing the pattern is half the battle."</p>
<p><strong>Self-knowledge deepens.</strong> Journaling shifts from emotions to patterns: "When do I do my best work? What drains me? What energizes me?" Introduce the concept that people have different temperaments — not just emotions but cognitive styles. "Your sister needs quiet to think. You need to talk it through. Neither is wrong — they're different operating systems." This is the seed of the self-profiling that becomes explicit at 11-13.</p>
<p><strong>Process joy — name it.</strong> "You just spent 3 hours on that and didn't notice. That's flow. That's the feeling you're optimizing for in life." Begin archetype conversations: "When do you do your best work? Morning or evening? Alone or with people? Talking or writing? There's no wrong answer — knowing yourself is the skill."</p>

<h3>Substance Awareness</h3>
<p><strong>Name that substances exist.</strong> "Some adults drink alcohol. Some people use other substances that affect how the brain works. These are called drugs." No detail, no scare tactics — just awareness that the topic exists and can be discussed openly. The goal: when they encounter substances at 11-13, they already have a parent they can talk to about it.</p>
<p><strong>The developing brain.</strong> "Your brain is still being built. It won't be finished until you're about 25. Substances affect a developing brain differently than an adult brain — like pouring paint on wet concrete vs. dry concrete." This is not a scare tactic; it is neurobiological fact.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Emotional regulation techniques are especially critical. These children feel everything at higher volume — the amygdala hijack hits harder. Box breathing and journaling aren't optional extras; they're core infrastructure. Overstimulation manifests as meltdowns that look like misbehavior but are sensory overload. Teach them to recognize the precursors and self-extract before the crash.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>Emotional awareness may need deliberate cultivation. These children can miss their own stress signals until they're exhausted or sick. Teach body-scanning: "Where in your body do you feel tension right now?" The journaling habit is harder to establish — they may not see the point. Start with short, concrete prompts rather than open-ended reflection.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Solitary reflection and journaling come naturally. Protect unstructured alone time as a non-negotiable — it's how they process. Exercise should include solo options (running, swimming, cycling) alongside any team activity. The "when do I do my best work?" conversation will reliably point to quiet, solo conditions.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Self-awareness may need external scaffolding — they process by talking, not writing. Let verbal reflection count: dinner conversation debriefs, walking-and-talking, voice memos instead of journals. Exercise motivation is highest in social settings — team sport, group classes, running with a parent.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-gm-adv"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Advanced</div>Sport specialisation and competitive intensity can increase. Multi-day backpacking with real distance (10+ km/day), martial arts sparring with strategy, and swimming in open water are all appropriate. Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's strength training introduction and endurance benchmarks. Physical challenge should now include measurable goals: "Run 2 km in under 12 minutes," "Hold a handstand for 10 seconds." Praise the training discipline, not the natural talent.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-dev"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Developing</div>Maintain the emphasis on movement confidence and enjoyment over competitive performance. Non-competitive physical activities — climbing, swimming, cycling, hiking — build capacity without the social comparison that team sport introduces. If participating in team sport, choose positions with lower coordination demands initially. Physical self-consciousness peaks in this age range; protect their relationship with movement by keeping challenge within reach.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>Precision activities escalate: miniature painting with detail brushes (eyes, highlights), electronics soldering under supervision, musical instrument technique at intermediate level (vibrato, dynamic control), and technical drawing with rulers and compasses. Touch typing should target 50+ WPM. Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's lab notebook standards and detailed illustration. Praise the patience invested in precision, not the natural steadiness.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Touch typing is both a fine motor exercise and an essential bypass — if handwriting is effortful, keyboard proficiency ensures that written expression isn't bottlenecked by motor skill. Art projects should use materials that forgive imprecision (clay, large-format drawing, collage) alongside precision-building exercises. Musical instrument practice may need technique-focused warm-ups to maintain engagement when fine motor demands create frustration.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-adv"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Advanced</div>Introduce cognitive reappraisal as a named technique: "When you feel anxious before a test, what's another way to interpret that feeling? Maybe it's excitement." This child can begin to separate the emotion from the reaction and choose their response deliberately. Pre-performance routines (breathing sequence, positive self-talk, physical warm-up) become self-managed. Praise the use of regulation strategies, not the absence of difficult emotions.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-dev"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Developing</div>Academic pressure and social comparison create new emotional triggers at this age. The amygdala hijack framework is essential: "Your alarm brain fired before your thinking brain could catch up. That's normal — here's what we do about it." Build a reliable calming sequence (box breathing 4-4-4-4, safe space visualisation, physical movement) and practise it when calm so it's available during crisis. Journaling three sentences about a difficult emotion begins to create the observer-self that regulation requires.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>Complex multi-step projects are manageable: research projects requiring synthesis from multiple sources, multi-movement music pieces, and extended coding projects. Mental math with 2-digit multiplication, remembering multi-part classroom instructions, and chess analysis (3+ moves ahead) are all accessible. Introduce note-taking methods (Cornell, mind-mapping) as working memory extensions. Praise holding complexity across sessions, not just within a single sitting.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>Multi-step classroom instructions will be missed. Teach them to write down instructions immediately — this is a survival skill, not a crutch. For research projects, break the process into one-step-at-a-time cards: "Step 1: Find three sources. Step 2: Write one sentence from each." Math benefits from showing all intermediate steps on paper. Spaced repetition (Anki) is especially high-value here — it offloads memorisation entirely to an external system.</div>

<h3>Dance & Movement</h3>
<p><strong>First structured dance style, 1-2x/week (instructor-required).</strong> A private instructor or quality studio — not a school PE teacher. Choose based on the child's pull:</p>
<p>• <strong>Flamenco</strong> — rhythm mastery, expression, cultural depth. Madrid gives direct access to serious flamenco instruction. Zapateado (footwork) at this age develops percussion-through-movement.</p>
<p>• <strong>Hip-hop</strong> — groove, body isolations, style, peer currency. The most socially relevant dance form for their generation.</p>
<p>• <strong>Latin basics</strong> (salsa, merengue) — foundational social dance in their cultural world. Start partner dance awareness.</p>
<p><strong>Body isolations begin.</strong> Chest, hips, shoulders moving independently. This is what separates "doing steps" from "dancing." Can be trained in hip-hop class (instructor) or at home with isolation drills (parent-guided — video tutorials are adequate for basic isolations).</p>
<p><strong>Ballet continues (instructor-required)</strong> if started at 6-7. Barre work, centre floor, across-the-floor combinations. RAD or Vaganova syllabus through Grade 2-3. The discipline compound is visible by now: ballet-trained children move qualitatively differently from non-ballet-trained children in any physical context.</p>
<p><strong>Freestyle dance at home continues (parent-guided).</strong> Dance to diverse music as a family. The habit must survive the transition from "little kid who dances freely" to "older kid who might become self-conscious." Regular family dance sessions protect this window.</p>

<h3>Gymnastics & Acrobatics</h3>
<p><strong>Tumbling progression with a coach (instructor-required):</strong> cartwheels → round-offs → handsprings. This is the progression that leads to backflips by 12-14. A qualified coach is non-negotiable for handsprings and beyond — the injury risk of self-taught aerial skills is high. Same logic as martial arts: technique before power, always with qualified instruction.</p>
<p><strong>Handstands (parent-guided):</strong> wall → freestanding → walking. 5 minutes daily practice at home. This is the single best bodyweight exercise for upper-body strength, balance, and spatial awareness.</p>
<p><strong>Flexibility maintenance — the puberty window is approaching.</strong> Maintain splits, bridges, shoulder mobility. Stretching can be parent-guided but a gymnastics coach ensures proper form. If they lose flexibility now, regaining it after puberty is 10x harder. 15 minutes of stretching, 3x/week minimum.</p>
<p><strong>Competitive or recreational gymnastics (instructor-required).</strong> If interested and talented: competitive gymnastics or acrobatic gymnastics (AcroGym). If not competitive: continued recreational gymnastics class for tumbling progression and aerial awareness. Either way, the instructor relationship continues.</p>
<p><strong>Trampoline (parent-guided supplementary):</strong> aerial awareness, spatial orientation while inverted, and core control. A garden trampoline with safety net builds the air sense that later back tucks require.</p>

<h2 id="s-social" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">people</span>Social & Relational</h2>

<h3>Team Sport</h3>
<p>Team sport becomes serious at this age. The child needs to experience the full range: being captain and being benched, winning unfairly and losing despite merit. The emotional processing happens after, not during — debrief gently, don't over-analyze.</p>
<p><strong>Competition begins.</strong> Tournaments, leagues, structured play with stakes. Frame outcomes as feedback: "What did the other team do better?" not "You should have..."</p>
<p><strong>What to watch for:</strong> Do they encourage struggling teammates? Can they follow instructions from a coach they disagree with? How do they handle a teammate's mistake in a crucial moment? These micro-moments build Targets 11-12 more than any curriculum.</p>

<h3>Social Intelligence</h3>
<p>Group dynamics awareness: "Who was the leader in that game? How did they become the leader? Did anyone else want to lead?" Active listening practice — repeat back what someone said before responding. Reading subtext: "She said she's fine. Do you think she's actually fine? What clues did you notice?" This is social pattern recognition, the same skill as reading a system.</p>
<p><strong>Negotiation as life skill.</strong> Every interaction where people want different things is a negotiation. Family negotiations with real stakes: bedtime, screen time, allowance, activity choices. The framework: state your position, state their position, find the overlap. "What can you offer?" The child who can negotiate bedtime by offering to read for 30 extra minutes is learning value creation.</p>

<h3>Service & Kindness</h3>
<p>Local, supervised, sustained. Not a one-off charity drive — a recurring commitment. Food bank, animal shelter, elderly visits, park cleanup. The same place, repeatedly, so they see the system, not just the moment. "Why are these people here? What would fix this? Is our help actually helping?" These questions are more valuable than the service itself. Service without reflection is sentimentality; service with reflection is applied ethics.</p>
<p><strong>Luck introduction.</strong> "Why did that good thing happen? Was it all skill, or was some of it being in the right place?" Introduce the idea that luck is partly controllable: preparation (be ready), exposure (be in more rooms), and kindness (people help people who helped them). Plant the seed — formalize later.</p>

<h3>Social World & Network</h3>
<p><strong>Mentors become possible.</strong> The chess coach who was a titled player. The music teacher who performs professionally. The martial arts instructor with competition experience. Not enrichment instructors — practitioners. The child should see adult mastery up close.</p>
<p><strong>Selective peer groups:</strong> Math competitions, chess tournaments, martial arts competitions, music festivals. These self-select for children who take something seriously.</p>
<p><strong>Madrid:</strong> Real Sociedad Matemática Española youth competitions. Spanish Chess Federation youth program. Conservatory preparation programs. Science fairs.</p>

<h3>Social Independence</h3>
<p><strong>History examples of being right against the consensus.</strong> Galileo: the Earth moves around the Sun — mocked, tried, confined. Ignaz Semmelweis: hand-washing prevents infection — mocked, fired, died in an asylum. Later proven right. Rosa Parks: refusal as moral act — arrested, but catalyzed a movement. The pattern: being right is sometimes deeply unpopular, and popularity is not evidence of truth.</p>
<p><strong>"Why did everyone go along?"</strong> Apply this analytical question to any historical failure or atrocity studied. Salem witch trials. Any familiar story where the group was wrong. The question is not rhetorical — it demands understanding the social mechanisms (fear, conformity, diffusion of responsibility) that produce collective failure.</p>
<p><strong>Asch conformity experiments.</strong> Describe in child-friendly terms: "Scientists showed people lines of different lengths and asked which was longest. When everyone else pointed to the wrong one, 75% of people agreed with the wrong answer. Even though they could see it was wrong." Ask: "Would you?" Discuss what makes it hard to disagree with a group.</p>
<p><strong>Niche mastery as social armor.</strong> By now they have 3+ years in a domain. Being genuinely excellent at something provides an identity anchor that peer opinion cannot easily dislodge. Name this explicitly: "You know more about chess/music/coding than almost any kid your age. That's yours. Nobody's opinion can take that from you."</p>
<p><strong>Comfort challenges escalate.</strong> Share an unpopular opinion in a safe family setting and defend it. Bring a non-mainstream interest to show-and-tell. Wear something unusual to school for one day. Write about what happened afterward: "What did people say? How did it feel? Would you do it again?"</p>
<p><strong>Reading:</strong> "Wonder" (R.J. Palacio) — the social cost of being different, and the social courage of befriending someone different. "Hatchet" (Gary Paulsen) — self-sufficiency under extreme conditions. "My Side of the Mountain" (Jean Craighead George) — a child who chose to live differently from everyone.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>One or two deep friendships are worth more than a wide social circle. Don't push social breadth — focus on relationship depth. The niche mastery community (chess club, music peers, coding group) provides natural social connection without forced mingling. Service commitments should be small-group or one-on-one, not large events.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Social energy is abundant — the challenge is selectivity. Help them notice WHO they spend time with, not just how much. Team sport and group activities are natural fits; use them to teach leadership observation: "Who did the group follow? Why?" Multiple friend groups are fine, but at least one should share a mastery interest.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Social dynamics hit harder. Being benched, a teammate's criticism, or a coach's blunt feedback can linger for days. Debrief gently and privately — never in front of the group. Service work with animals or nature may be easier entry points than direct human-focused service, where others' suffering can be overwhelming.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-social-adv"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Advanced</div>This child reads team dynamics, navigates peer pressure, and builds alliances naturally. Challenge with explicit leadership: captain roles, mentoring younger children, organising group projects. Introduce the concept of social capital: "Who do people go to when they need help? Why?" Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's networking and mentorship frameworks. Discussion of peer pressure should be proactive, not reactive — "What would you do if someone you respect asked you to do something you thought was wrong?" Praise the social intelligence deployed, not the social popularity achieved.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-dev"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Developing</div>Peer pressure and team dynamics are the primary challenges. Provide explicit decision frameworks: "If your stomach feels wrong, that's a signal — trust it." Role-play specific scenarios at home: being asked to exclude someone, being mocked for a genuine interest, being pressured to break a rule. Choose team environments with active adult supervision and coaches who manage group dynamics. Smaller teams and structured roles reduce the social navigation complexity.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>Sustained focus during classroom instruction is strong. Push into demanding focus contexts: chess tournament concentration (2+ hour games), extended music practice without breaks, and sustained reading for 45+ minutes. In social contexts, they can resist peer pressure to act impulsively. Introduce the meta-skill: "Notice when you choose not to follow an impulse — that's a superpower most adults haven't developed." Praise the conscious choice to hold back when holding back was the harder option.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-dev"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Developing</div>Sustained classroom focus requires environmental support. Work with teachers on seating position, movement breaks, and task chunking. At home, study sessions should be short (15-20 min) with physical movement between them. Social inhibition challenges (impulsive comments, difficulty waiting for turns in conversations) benefit from structured formats rather than open-ended social situations. Games with clear turn-taking rules (board games, card games) practise inhibition in a low-stakes context.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>This child adapts to changing group dynamics, new social environments, and shifting rules quickly. Challenge with social situations requiring rapid recalibration: new activity with unfamiliar peers, leadership of a group with mixed skill levels, or resolving a conflict between two friends. The "argue the opposite" dinner game at this age should require genuine perspective-taking under time pressure. Praise the flexibility of response when the social situation shifted unexpectedly.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-dev"><div class="t-label">Shift: Developing</div>Changes in social context — new team member, different coach, friend group reshuffling — are disruptive. Prepare them for known changes in advance and debrief after unexpected ones. Maintain stable social anchors (one consistent best friend, one familiar activity) while gradually introducing novel social elements. Rigid social rules ("I always sit next to Carlos") are a flexibility signal; gently expand the range rather than forcing abrupt changes.</div>

<h2 id="s-building" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">construction</span>Building & Making</h2>

<h3>Coding</h3>
<p>Scratch (MIT). One project per month — a game, an animation, a quiz. The goal is thinking in algorithms, not learning syntax. Also: spreadsheet basics. Formulas, sorting, simple data analysis. A child who understands =SUM() and =IF() has grasped the essence of computation: input → rule → output. These are more transferable than any specific programming language.</p>

<h3>Entrepreneurship & Projects</h3>
<p><strong>Build:</strong> Scratch → Python transition. Make something that works: a calculator, a simple game, a quiz app. Physical projects: electronics (Arduino starter kit at 10), woodworking (birdhouse, shelf). The deliverable matters — something they can point to and say "I made that."</p>
<p><strong>Monetize:</strong> First real exchange of value for money. Lemonade stand, selling something they made (art, baked goods), a small service (dog walking, car washing for neighbors). Lesson: money comes from solving someone else's problem.</p>
<p><strong>Meta-learning begins:</strong> Introduce spaced repetition (Anki or physical flashcards) for anything they're memorizing. Teach the testing effect: "Quiz yourself instead of re-reading." Touch typing (target: 40+ WPM by age 10).</p>
<p><strong>Money as system.</strong> The Moonjar teaches saving, spending, sharing. Now add investing: "Money can work for you while you sleep." Show a compound interest calculator — "If you put €10/month away starting now..." Distinguish saving (preservation) from investing (growth). Price vs. value: "Why does this toy cost €30? Is it worth €30? Those are different questions." Price is what the market says today. Value is what something is actually worth to you. These diverge constantly.</p>
<p><strong>Compound interest concept.</strong> Show a compound interest calculator — "If you put €10/month away starting now, by age 60 it's €X. If you start at 20 it's half." The Compounding mental model (#6) comes alive with real numbers.</p>
<p><strong>LEGO Technic → Mindstorms/Spike.</strong> The building pipeline formalizes: System (still active) → Technic mechanisms (gears, pulleys, pneumatics) → Mindstorms/Spike robotics + programming. Leads to FIRST LEGO League competition at 10-11.</p>
<p><strong>Miniature painting basics.</strong> Citadel starter kits or Army Painter. Fine motor, patience, colour theory, aesthetics. Entry point for the <a href="#universe-warhammer">Warhammer</a> pipeline at 11-13. Even without the game, painting minis teaches focus and craftsmanship.</p>

<p><strong>Protocol:</strong> Budgeted time (e.g., 45 min weekdays, 90 min weekends). Discussed analytically — "What's your strategy in Civ?" "What did you learn from that Kerbal failure?" Games are a tool, not a pacifier.</p>
<p>Coding through narrative is powerful at this age: text-based games, interactive fiction, chatbots. Writing documentation for projects builds both coding and communication skills simultaneously. Physical building (Arduino, woodworking, electronics) is equally valuable as a making activity. Minecraft modding and Kerbal Space Program are optimal coding entry points. Let them build the thing before they write about it — the physical artifact is their thinking tool.</p>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>One project, carried far. Let them spend three months on a single Scratch game rather than completing ten tutorials. <div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>Self-defined project requirements are the next level: "What game do you want to build in Scratch? Write down the features before you start coding." Science experiments should follow the full hypothesis-test-document cycle independently. Kerbal Space Program missions with specific orbital targets (reach Mun, achieve stable orbit) require iterative problem-solving at genuine difficulty. Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's Python projects and competition math. Praise the problem decomposition approach, not just the working solution.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>Provide worked examples before expecting novel solutions. In Scratch, modify an existing game before building one from scratch. In science, replicate a known experiment before designing an original one. Kerbal Space Program's sandbox mode removes resource constraints so they can focus on the physics problem alone. Step-by-step project guides with checkpoints ("Does this part work before you move on?") prevent the compounding errors that make complex projects fail catastrophically.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>Precision building reaches a new tier: miniature painting with layered techniques (base coat, wash, drybrush, highlight), electronics with soldered connections, LEGO Technic mechanisms with gear trains and pneumatics. Typing speed should target 50+ WPM. Musical instrument technique enters the intermediate range where finger independence and dynamic control separate competent from advancing players. Praise the hours of patient detail work, not the final visual result.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Typing proficiency (Typing Club, Keybr) remains the highest-priority fine motor investment because it removes the handwriting bottleneck from all written work. Building projects should use materials with larger tolerances — woodworking with pre-drilled holes, snap circuits over soldered connections, large-format art over miniatures. Progress at their pace; the goal is consistent engagement with precision tasks, not matching a particular benchmark age.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-plan-adv"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Advanced</div>Project planning becomes self-directed: "Write down your plan before starting, estimate how long each step takes, and compare your estimate to reality afterward." Introduce backwards planning for school projects and competition preparation. Entrepreneurial ventures (lemonade stand, selling crafts) require planning inventory, pricing, and logistics — real project management at small scale. Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's time management systems. Praise the accuracy of the plan, not just successful completion.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-dev"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Developing</div>Provide project planning templates: a checklist with deadlines, a materials list, a daily task card. Co-plan major projects together — sit down and map out the timeline before they start building or coding. Break multi-week projects into daily micro-tasks with visible progress markers. The weekly planning conversation should be parent-led with gradually increasing child input: "What should we do first? Why?" Planning is a skill that develops through structured repetition, not instruction.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>Complex multi-session projects are manageable without re-reading previous notes. Challenge with coding projects requiring variables that interact (game score tracking, multi-sprite interactions in Scratch), building projects from verbal descriptions without visual reference, and multi-step money calculations (compound interest examples, budgeting across categories). Praise the ability to resume complex work after interruption without losing the thread.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>Every multi-session project needs a "where I left off" note — teach them to write one sentence summarising current status before stopping work. Coding projects benefit from extensive in-line comments ("this sprite does X because Y") so the logic is readable when they return. Physical builds should photograph progress at each session. Reduce the number of active projects to one at a time so working memory isn't split across contexts.</div>

<h2 id="s-material-culture" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">handyman</span>Material Culture & Craft</h2>

<h3>Deconstruct & Begin to Repair</h3>
<p><strong>Monthly object autopsy.</strong> Buy cheap items from second-hand shops specifically for this purpose. Take apart a broken appliance, an old shoe, a worn-out bag. Examine: What materials? How was it assembled? Why did it fail? What could be repaired? The autopsy teaches more about manufacturing than any textbook.</p>
<p><strong>Sneaker anatomy.</strong> Buy a cheap sneaker and an expensive one. Cut them both open. Compare: sole thickness, cushioning material, stitching vs. glue, insole construction, upper material quality. "What are you paying for when you pay 3x more?" The answer is genuinely complex — materials, durability, marketing, brand premium. Not "expensive is better" or "cheap is smart."</p>
<p><strong>Textile basics.</strong> Learn to identify fabrics by touch: cotton vs. polyester vs. wool vs. silk vs. linen. Understand weaving vs. knitting at a basic level. Visit a textile workshop or watch a weaver at work if accessible.</p>
<p><strong>Cooking escalation.</strong> By age 10, cook 5 meals without a recipe. Understand the Maillard reaction — why does browning taste good? Make bread from scratch: flour, water, yeast, salt, time — nothing else. "This costs 30 cents. The bakery charges 3 euros. What are you paying for?" (Labour, equipment, rent, expertise, convenience. All legitimate.)</p>
<p><strong>Basic repair skills.</strong> Sew a torn seam. Glue a broken ceramic — introduce kintsugi: the Japanese art of repairing with gold, making the repair visible and beautiful. "A repaired bowl with a visible gold seam is more interesting than an unbroken one because it has a history." Re-attach a loose sole with shoe goo. Sharpen a kitchen knife (supervised).</p>
<p><strong>Quarterly artisan visits.</strong> Cobbler, glassblower, leatherworker, potter, luthier, blacksmith. Madrid and surrounding areas have all of these. The child observes, asks questions, and writes or draws what they learned. Seeing adult professionals who make things with their hands expands the model of what "work" looks like.</p>

<h3>Electricity & Energy Fundamentals</h3>
<p><strong>Simple circuits.</strong> Build a flashlight from batteries, LEDs, switches, and wire. Understand: what IS electricity? Electrons flowing through a conductor, driven by voltage, resisted by resistance. Physical analogy: water through a pipe — voltage is pressure, current is flow rate, resistance is pipe narrowness. "When you flip a light switch, what actually happens?" Trace the path from power plant to wall socket.</p>
<p><strong>Energy literacy.</strong> Where does the energy in our house come from? Follow the chain: sun → coal/gas/wind/solar panel → power station → grid → transformer → wall socket → light. What is a kilowatt-hour? Read the electricity meter together. Compare: how much energy does a light bulb use vs. a fridge vs. a car? The concept of energy conversion: chemical → thermal → mechanical → electrical. Energy is never created or destroyed — only converted.</p>
<p><strong>House systems awareness.</strong> Where does water come from and go? Trace the plumbing: reservoir → treatment plant → pipes → tap → drain → sewer → treatment → river. Where does heat come from? Trace the heating: boiler → radiators → thermostat. "The house is a machine. These are its systems." Walk through and identify every system: electrical, plumbing, heating, ventilation.</p>

<h3>Vehicle Curiosity</h3>
<p>Name the major parts of a car engine: pistons, crankshaft, combustion chamber. How does fuel become motion? The four-stroke cycle in simple terms: suck in air and fuel, squeeze it, ignite it, push out exhaust. Visit a mechanic's workshop — watch a car on a lift, see the underside. "Every part you see was designed by an engineer and made in a factory. Nothing here is magic."</p>

<h3>Price vs. Value</h3>
<p><strong>"What is this actually worth?"</strong> Apply to everything: meals, clothes, toys, furniture. "This IKEA chair costs 20 euros. This handmade chair costs 200 euros. The materials cost is 15 and 80 respectively. What explains the 10x price difference? Is the expensive one 'worth it'?" The answer depends on what you value — durability, aesthetics, the story, the craft. Not a simple answer. That's the point.</p>
<p><strong>William Morris, simplified.</strong> "Have nothing in your house that you do not know to be useful, or believe to be beautiful." Apply to their own room: what here is useful? What is beautiful? What is neither? This is not minimalism — it is intentionality.</p>

<h2 id="s-knowledge" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">lightbulb</span>Knowledge & Thinking</h2>

<h3>Ethics & Moral Reasoning</h3>
<p>Consequences thinking: "Who gets hurt? Who benefits? Are they the same people?" Introduction to empathy as a reasoning tool, not just a feeling — "What would this feel like from their perspective?" Rules vs. principles: "Why does this rule exist? If the rule didn't exist, would the action still be wrong?" This is the seed of deontological vs. consequentialist thinking, unnamed.</p>

<h3>History</h3>
<p>History as story, but now with causal reasoning. Usborne encyclopedias give way to narrative histories. "Why did Rome fall?" — not one answer but many, interacting. Visits to historical sites (Madrid is dense with them) with discussion, not just observation. Timeline of Spain: Romans → Visigoths → Moors → Reconquista → Golden Age → decline. Not patriotism — case study in how civilizations rise, peak, and fall.</p>

<h3>Nature & Ecology</h3>
<p>Formal ecology concepts: food webs, habitats, adaptation, natural selection as a mechanism. Climate basics — what is happening, why, what the evidence is. "Every system you study in this framework — economics, politics, culture — exists within the biosphere. The biosphere is the system that contains all other systems."</p>

<h3>Teaching & Explanation</h3>
<p>"Explain what you learned to a younger sibling or friend. If they don't understand, the problem is your explanation, not their intelligence." This is the core Feynman insight: teaching reveals the gaps in your understanding. One explanation per week of something they learned — at dinner, casually.</p>

<h3>Philosophy of Enough</h3>
<p><strong>The "what is this actually worth?" reflex.</strong> Begin applying to everyday objects: "Your lunch cost 3 euros at the canteen. What would it cost to make at home? What are you paying for?" "This toy costs 25 euros. What is it made of? How long did it take someone to make? What would a fair price be?" Not to produce guilt — to produce analytical understanding. Price and worth are different concepts.</p>
<p>This connects to the Incentives model (#3): "What incentives produce a 5-euro t-shirt? What had to be true about the labour, materials, and environmental costs for that price to be possible?" The answer is analytical, not moralistic.</p>

<h3>Digital Literacy</h3>
<p><strong>How computers think.</strong> Input → processing → output. Every app, game, and website follows this pattern. A search engine doesn't "know" things — it matches patterns. A recommendation algorithm doesn't "like" things — it predicts what you'll click. Demystify the machine: it is a tool that follows rules humans wrote.</p>
<p><strong>Algorithms as invisible curators.</strong> "Why does YouTube recommend that video? Because you watched something similar, and people who watched that also watched this. The algorithm is optimising for your attention, not your wellbeing." This is the Incentives model (#3) applied to technology.</p>

<h3>Canon Knowledge</h3>
<p><em>Canon knowledge is not a checklist. It's the minimum factual scaffolding needed to reason about the world. Deep immersions build factual knowledge organically — these benchmarks confirm the substrate is forming, not prescribe what to drill.</em></p>
<table>
<tr><th>Domain</th><th>They Should Know</th></tr>
<tr><td>Math</td><td>Mental arithmetic: multiply 2-digit numbers, percentages, basic fractions. Estimation as reflex.</td></tr>
<tr><td>Geography</td><td>Every continent, major countries on a map. All European capitals. Major rivers, mountain ranges, oceans.</td></tr>
<tr><td>History</td><td>Basic timeline: ancient civilizations → Greece → Rome → medieval → Renaissance → modern. Rough dates, correct sequence, basic causality.</td></tr>
<tr><td>Science</td><td>How plants grow. Water cycle. Basic astronomy (planets, sun, moon, seasons). Evolution concept (variation + selection).</td></tr>
<tr><td>Practical</td><td>Cook 2 simple meals. Swim competently. Ride a bike on roads. Touch-type at 40+ WPM. Read a map.</td></tr>
</table>

<h3>Cognitive Models</h3>
<p>Introduce System 1 / System 2 explicitly: "Your brain has two speeds — fast and automatic, or slow and deliberate. Fast is great for catching a ball. Slow is better for deciding whether something is fair." Name common biases using their own experience: anchoring ("the first price you see changes what you think is reasonable"), confirmation bias ("you only noticed evidence for what you already believed"), sunk cost ("you want to finish the film because you already watched an hour, not because it's good"). Kahneman's <em>Thinking, Fast and Slow</em> is the parent's reference; the child builds understanding through conversation and worked examples at this stage.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>Timeline walls, map-based history, and ecological diagrams are powerful access points for focused, detail-oriented children. Let them draw the food web, build a 3D model of a historical site, or map trade routes physically. History through narrative and argument is a natural entry — push for precision: "Can you state that more exactly?" The Feynman technique (teach to explain) is especially effective. Let them disappear into a domain. If it's Roman history, let them spend three months on it — reading, building models, visiting sites. The canon knowledge benchmarks will fill in naturally through obsession. Don't interrupt a deep dive to cover breadth.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Cross-domain connections are their superpower. "How is a food web like an economy?" comes naturally to these children. Use the canon knowledge table as a checklist — breadth-seekers risk knowing a little about everything and mastering nothing. Periodically check depth in each domain.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>Ethics and history questions can demand multi-step causal reasoning: "Rome fell because of X, but what caused X?" Science experiments should include failed predictions — the interesting learning happens when the hypothesis is wrong. Math through AoPS should include problems with no obvious entry point, requiring strategy selection before execution. Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's formal logic introduction. Praise the quality of the reasoning process, not the correctness of the conclusion.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>Break complex historical and ethical questions into guided sequences: "First, what happened? Then, why do you think it happened? Finally, what could have been different?" Science experiments should start with clear predictions and simple variables before introducing ambiguity. Canon knowledge benchmarks may need more time — focus on depth in 2-3 domains rather than breadth across all. Demonstrate the thinking process aloud before asking them to replicate it independently.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>Cross-domain thinking is a strength: "How is what happened to Rome like what's happening in this Civilization game?" Switching between ethical frameworks (is it fair? is it kind? is it useful?) in the same conversation trains cognitive flexibility at a high level. History through multiple perspectives (Roman soldier vs. conquered Gaul, coloniser vs. indigenous) requires rapid viewpoint switching. Praise the capacity to hold contradictory perspectives simultaneously.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-dev"><div class="t-label">Shift: Developing</div>Stick with one domain per study session rather than switching between history, science, and math. Within a domain, stay with one perspective before introducing alternatives — "Let's understand the Roman perspective first, then next time we'll look at it from the other side." The canon knowledge table should be approached as a rotation (one domain per week at depth) rather than a parallel curriculum.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>Historical timelines held in memory, multi-source synthesis for arguments, and canon knowledge that interconnects across domains are all accessible. Challenge with "connect the dots" exercises: "How does what you learned about Roman aqueducts relate to the water cycle you studied in science?" This cross-referencing requires holding information from different contexts simultaneously. Praise the connections made across domains, not just the facts recalled within one.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>Externalise knowledge connections: timeline walls, mind maps linking topics, and colour-coded notes that group related facts. The canon knowledge benchmarks are best checked through conversation ("Tell me about...") rather than written tests, reducing working memory load. Spaced repetition (Anki) for geographical facts, historical dates, and science vocabulary ensures the foundational knowledge is available without taxing working memory during reasoning tasks.</div>

<h2 id="s-culture" data-tier="recommended"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">travel_explore</span>Culture & Inner Life</h2>

<h3>Cultural Travel</h3>
<p>Travel with purpose, not tourism. Historical site visits tied to what they're learning (Rome after studying Rome, Athens after Greek mythology, Morocco after studying the Moors in Spain). One trip annually to a non-Western context: East Asia, the Americas, North Africa. The goal: calibrate their sense of "normal." A child who has eaten dinner with a family in Fez and a family in Tokyo has a fundamentally different model of human possibility than one who has only seen Madrid and beach resorts.</p>
<p>Domestic deep-dives: Andalucía (Alhambra, Córdoba mosque — the Moorish legacy they're studying), Basque Country, Catalonia. Spain's internal diversity is a curriculum in itself.</p>

<h3>Meaning-Making & Spirituality</h3>
<p>Basic architecture of the five major world religions: what Christians, Muslims, Jews, Buddhists, and Hindus actually believe, and why. Not comparative religion as academic exercise — genuine curiosity about how billions of people make meaning.</p>
<p><strong>In Madrid:</strong> Visit a mosque (Islamic Centre of Madrid), a synagogue (Synagogue of Madrid), a Buddhist temple. They already know Catholic churches from the environment. Make it a world tour of meaning-making.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Travel to non-Western contexts may be overwhelming — poverty, unfamiliar sensory environments, and cultural dislocation hit harder. Prepare them in advance: show photos, discuss what they'll see, name the discomfort before it arrives. Meaning-making conversations about religion and spirituality may go unusually deep; don't rush or simplify when they're genuinely grappling.</div>

<h2 id="s-planning" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">calendar_month</span>Planning & Milestones</h2>

<h3>Institution Decisions</h3>
<p><strong>⚡ CRITICAL: Secondary school (age 10-12).</strong> THE decision. After 12, peer influence dominates parental influence (Harris, 1998). The peer group IS the curriculum. Look for: peers who take things seriously, a culture where depth is respected not mocked. Everything else — facilities, curriculum, brand — is secondary to WHO ARE THE OTHER KIDS.</p>
<p><strong>⚡ Summer immersive programs begin (age 10+).</strong> 1-2 week domain-specific programs with serious peers. Residential if possible. This is a network accelerant and identity-forming experience. Start researching now for ages 12-14.</p>
<p><strong>Private instruction continues.</strong> By now the niche teacher should be a genuine expert. If the current teacher is holding them back, upgrade. The child's growth rate should not be limited by instructor quality.</p>

<h3>Portfolio & Visible Excellence</h3>
<p><strong>First competition entry.</strong> Chess tournament, math competition (AMC 8), music recital, martial arts tournament, science fair. The result doesn't matter — the act of competing externally is the milestone. Frame it: "You put your work in front of people who don't know you. That takes courage."</p>
<p><strong>First shipped project with outside users.</strong> Scratch game shared on the Scratch community. Handmade goods sold at a school fair. A blog post that someone outside the family read. The criterion: someone who doesn't love you used or valued what you made.</p>
<p><strong>Community involvement begins.</strong> Sustained commitment to one organization — not a one-off event. They return, they're known, they see the system.</p>

<h3>Summer Intensives</h3>
<p>Summer is for deep dives in their niche domain plus exploration. 1–2 week immersions that produce real skill development.</p>
<div class="card-grid">
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Coding Bootcamp (1 week)</div><p>Scratch to Python transition, or game modding, or web development intro. Real projects that work. Not a summer school — a focused deep dive.</p></div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Music Intensive/Masterclass</div><p>2 weeks with an advanced teacher or summer program. If they're showing real aptitude, this is where serious progress happens. Concert preparation.</p></div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">History Deep-Dive</div><p>2-week immersion around a domain they're fascinated by. Visit museums, read age-appropriate sources, watch documentaries. Example: ancient Rome — visit ruins if possible, read adaptations of Caesar's Commentaries, see films.</p></div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Wilderness Camp</div><p>Multi-day backpacking or expedition camp. Learn navigation, camping skills, wilderness first aid. Build self-reliance and physical hardship tolerance.</p></div>
</div>

<h3>Weekly Cadence</h3>
<div class="card-grid">
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Parent Evenings</div>
<p>Homework check (brief). Instrument practice (30 min, parent present for part). Dinner conversation (mental model naming 2–3x/week). Reading — now shared: same book, discuss at dinner. Coding/building time 2x/week (30 min, parent alongside).</p>
</div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Weekends</div>
<p>Saturday: Niche mastery activity (competition, lesson, or deep practice). Physical activity (hike, swim, martial arts). Gaming time (budgeted).</p>
<p>Sunday: Build project time. Cultural outing (museum, concert, or exploration). Extended unstructured time. Family game night.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Summer intensives with residential components are high-value but high-drain. Choose programs with built-in solo time and don't stack two intensive weeks back-to-back. Secondary school selection should weight small class sizes and a culture that respects quiet depth over social performance.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Summer is ideal for sampling — one week coding, one week wilderness, one week music. But the school-year anchor activity should remain constant. Use summer breadth to inform which domains deserve deeper commitment during the year.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-plan-adv"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Advanced</div>This child can co-own the weekly cadence. Let them propose the weekend schedule, estimate activity durations, and manage their own homework sequence. Introduce calendar management as a life skill: "Block your practice time on the calendar like a meeting." Cross-reference the age 11-13 phase's time management systems and long-term goal setting. Praise the independence in planning, not just plan adherence.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-dev"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Developing</div>The weekly cadence should remain parent-managed with the child's input increasing gradually. Use a visual weekly planner posted where they can see it. Summer intensives need careful spacing — two intensive weeks back-to-back overwhelm a child still developing planning stamina. Pre-trip checklists ("What do you need to pack for camp?") are concrete planning exercises with immediate feedback — they'll know if they forgot something.</div>

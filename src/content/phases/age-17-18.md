<h1>Age 17–18: Synthesis & Launch</h1>
<p class="subtitle">Models are tools to be combined, stress-tested, and extended. The goal: original frameworks, intellectual independence, and readiness to leave.</p>

<div class="priority-box">
<h3>Priorities — Your Remaining Time</h3>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">1</span><strong>Release control.</strong> They make their own decisions — including bad ones. Your role: available, honest, non-directive unless asked.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">2</span><strong>Synthesis conversations.</strong> "What's your model for X?" "Where does your model fail?" The meta-skill: using models as models, not as beliefs.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">3</span><strong>Launch preparation.</strong> University applications, gap year planning, career exploration — they drive, you advise.</div>
</div>

<h3>Full Onion Review</h3>
<p>By 18, every one of the twelve universal models has been encountered across all five layers. The goal is not that they know the models — it's that they've internalized the habit of asking "what model am I using, is it the right one, and where does it fail?" That habit, applied consistently, is more valuable than any specific model.</p>
<p><strong>Critical assessment:</strong> Can they deploy 10-12 models automatically in conversation, analysis, and decision-making? Can they identify which model applies to a novel situation? Can they build original models from observed patterns? If yes, the onion structure has worked.</p>

<h2 id="s-communication" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">record_voice_over</span>Communication & Expression</h2>

<h3>Language — Written & Oral</h3>
<div class="music-track language-track">
<div>
<h4>Written Track</h4>
<p>Independent writing voice. Portfolio of published or shared work — essays, blog posts, project documentation, application essays. Can draft a 2,000-word argument in a single sitting without structural collapse. Writing IS thinking — they should no longer distinguish between "figuring out what I think" and "writing." These are the same activity.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h4>Oral &amp; Performance Track</h4>
<p>By 18, they can give a compelling 10-minute talk on any subject they know well — structured, clear, delivered with authority and without notes. They can handle Q&amp;A: hostile questions, tangential questions, questions they don't know the answer to ("I don't know, but here's how I'd think about it"). They can improvise a coherent 3-minute argument on an unfamiliar topic with 60 seconds of preparation.</p>
<p><strong>Interview command.</strong> Genuine conversational command with adults in professional contexts. They can hold their own with a university admissions interviewer, a potential employer, or a domain expert. The mechanism is oral skill: the ability to think, structure, and speak simultaneously.</p>
<p><strong>Performance range.</strong> They have delivered: a formal speech to an audience of 20+, a debate round against a prepared opponent, an improvised presentation, and either a theatrical performance or a spoken-word delivery. These are evidence that the voice has been trained as seriously as the pen.</p>
</div>
</div>

<h3>Digital Presence</h3>
<p><strong>Portfolio is the resume.</strong> Personal website, GitHub profile (if technical), published writing, video portfolio — whatever medium fits their niche. This exists and is discoverable. Professional quality.</p>
<p><strong>AI literacy.</strong> By 2044 graduation, AI fluency is as fundamental as computer literacy was in 2000. They should understand: what AI can and can't do, how to use AI tools to amplify their work, the ethical implications, and how to stay valuable in an AI-augmented world.</p>

<h3>Visual & Spatial</h3>
<p>Visual communication as professional competence. Can create a clear diagram, chart, or presentation that communicates complex ideas to an audience unfamiliar with the domain. Data literacy: can read and critique any visualization. Design sense: their projects, presentations, and documents look professional — not because aesthetics matter intrinsically, but because visual clarity reflects clarity of thought.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Their written voice is likely their strongest channel — the portfolio, blog, and application essays will be genuinely compelling. Interview command requires deliberate practice: mock interviews weekly in the months before applications. Frame it as performance skill, not social skill. One-on-one conversations with professors and mentors are their natural networking mode.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Interview command and Q&A handling come naturally. The gap to close: written precision. Their essays may read as they speak — conversational but structurally loose. Insist on tight revision cycles. The 2,000-word argument in a single sitting should be followed by ruthless editing, not submission of the first draft.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>Portfolio should lead with visual and interactive work: video demos, designed presentations, interactive projects. Application essays may need more scaffolding — use outlining and diagramming as pre-writing tools. Their digital presence should be design-forward; a beautiful portfolio site IS the argument for their competence.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Interview anxiety can undermine genuinely strong candidates. Systematic desensitization: start with low-stakes informational interviews, build to mock admissions interviews with strangers, then real ones. Their emotional authenticity in essays is a competitive advantage — help them channel it without oversharing. Performance range (speech, debate, theatre) may need extra support if stage anxiety persists.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>An advanced communicator at 17-18 operates at a professional level: writing that requires no adult editing, networking conversations with domain professionals that produce genuine mutual value, and a personal brand (portfolio, social presence, published work) that precedes them into rooms. Push toward professional communication contexts — conference presentations, published articles, podcast appearances, or content creation with a genuine audience. Praise the strategic use of communication as a career tool, not just the skill itself.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-dev"><div class="t-label">Communication: Developing</div>A developing communicator at 17-18 needs intensive, structured support for the highest-stakes communication of their life to date: application essays, university interviews, and professional networking. Work through multiple revision cycles for every written piece. Mock interviews should be weekly for months before applications, starting with warm audiences and building to strangers. Provide explicit frameworks for networking conversations: how to introduce yourself, how to ask a good question, how to follow up.</div>

<h2 id="s-music" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">music_note</span>Music</h2>
<p><strong>If pursuing music seriously:</strong> Conservatory preparation. Audition repertoire. Pre-professional level in both performance and theory.</p>
<p><strong>If not:</strong> Lifelong amateur competence — can play at a gathering, sit in with a band, sight-read a hymn, improvise over changes, discuss music with the analytical rigor they apply to literature. Music production skills: can record, mix, and publish their own work.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>If pursuing music seriously, audition anxiety is the primary obstacle — their musical sensitivity is likely exceptional, but performance pressure can undermine it. Build audition simulation into regular practice: record yourself, perform for small audiences, then scale. If music is recreational, it remains a powerful emotional regulation tool; protect it from being crowded out by application pressure.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>Music production, sound design, and audio engineering are professional paths that combine perceptual detail with musical knowledge. A portfolio of produced tracks, mixed recordings, or scored short films is as valid as a performance portfolio. If they've moved into electronic music or production, this IS their instrument.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>Advanced fine motor mastery at 17-18 is a professional asset: pre-professional instrument technique, portfolio-quality physical craft (model-making, prop fabrication, precision electronics), or surgical-grade hand-eye coordination for specialized fields. This is no longer a developmental category — it is a competitive differentiator in their niche. The portfolio should showcase physical craft alongside digital work.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>At 17-18, developing fine motor skills are accommodated rather than remediated. Career and university choices should account for this honestly — fields requiring extreme manual precision may not be the right fit, but digital tools increasingly bypass manual dexterity requirements. Focus on domains where cognitive and creative strengths compensate, and maintain physical dexterity practice as a secondary discipline through activities they enjoy.</div>

<h2 id="s-mind-body" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">fitness_center</span>Mind & Body</h2>

<h3>Physical System</h3>
<p>Autonomous management of the body-mind system. They have their own exercise routine designed partly for cognitive benefit. They manage sleep, nutrition, and recovery as a system, not as parental rules. They understand that physical state directly determines cognitive capacity and make daily choices accordingly.</p>

<h3>Self-Awareness</h3>
<p>Mastery. They regulate automatically in most situations. They recognize when they can't and have strategies. They read emotional dynamics in groups and respond appropriately. The gap between "feeling an emotion" and "acting on it" is wide enough to drive a truck through. This is not suppression — it's the ability to choose a response rather than be hijacked by a reaction.</p>
<p><strong>Self-Awareness (Target 3) — integrated.</strong> Can articulate their own strengths, weaknesses, values, cognitive style, communication patterns, and failure modes — not as fixed identity but as current operating parameters subject to revision. "I work best when... I fail when... I need... I contribute..." This connects to Ethics (values arrived through reasoning) and Social Intelligence (self-awareness as foundation of social effectiveness). The child who was profiled by parents at age 3 now profiles themselves — and updates the profile based on evidence.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Their autonomous physical-cognitive routine likely favors solo exercise — running, swimming, weightlifting, yoga. This is correct; don't push group fitness. Self-awareness profiling is a natural strength: they've been observing their own inner life for years. Help them formalize it: written self-assessment, periodic revision, evidence-based updates to their operating parameters.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>By 18, they should have a robust toolkit for emotional regulation — and know which tools work for which states. The risk is that launch pressure (applications, transitions, leaving home) overwhelms systems that worked in calmer conditions. Stress-test their regulation strategies before the pressure peaks: simulated high-stakes scenarios, deliberate overload weeks with built-in recovery. If their system breaks under pressure, better to discover that now than at university.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>Self-awareness is the persistent gap. They may over-index on physical system mastery (exercise, nutrition, sleep optimization) while under-indexing on emotional self-knowledge. Push the harder questions: "What are your failure modes in relationships? When have you missed someone's emotional signal? What cost did that have?" If they can't articulate emotional patterns with the same precision they apply to physical routines, the self-awareness target isn't met.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-adv"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Advanced</div>Advanced gross motor capacity at 17-18 is either a competitive athletic identity (varsity, club, martial arts) or a sophisticated personal fitness system designed for lifelong health and cognitive performance. Either path, the key is ownership: they design, execute, and adjust their physical program independently. The identity is "I am someone who trains" — not as obligation but as self-knowledge. This survives the transition to university.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-dev"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Developing</div>At 17-18, the goal is establishing a lifelong fitness identity that survives leaving home. Find the physical activity with the lowest friction and highest enjoyment — gym membership, regular hiking, recreational swimming, yoga — and make it habitual before they leave. The standard is consistency, not excellence. A young adult who exercises three times per week for life outperforms a former athlete who stops at 18.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-adv"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Advanced</div>Advanced emotional regulation at 17-18 is professional-grade composure: maintaining equanimity during application rejections, handling interpersonal conflict without escalation, and performing under pressure (interviews, auditions, exams) with their full capacity available. The frontier is teaching others — they should be able to articulate their regulation strategies and coach peers through emotional difficulty. This is leadership infrastructure.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-dev"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Developing</div>Developing emotional regulation at 17-18, when stakes are at their adolescent peak (university decisions, leaving home, adult relationships), requires a proven toolkit — not a toolkit still being built. If regulation strategies are still fragile, invest in them now as the highest-priority development target. Consider professional support (therapist, coach) as a strength-building measure, not a crisis response. The transition to university without reliable emotional regulation is genuinely risky.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>Strong inhibitory control at 17-18 manifests as professional restraint — knowing when NOT to speak in a meeting, when NOT to send the email, when to let an insult pass without response, and when patience is the strategically dominant move. This is executive function applied to adult life. Challenge them to identify situations where restraint was the highest-leverage action, and to articulate the difference between passivity and strategic patience.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-dev"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Developing</div>Developing inhibitory control at 17-18 means higher risk in contexts where impulsive decisions carry permanent consequences — substance use, sexual decisions, financial commitments, digital reputation. Explicit frameworks help: the "10-10-10" rule (how will I feel about this in 10 minutes, 10 months, 10 years?), no-send zones for emotionally charged messages, and pre-commitment devices for high-risk situations. Build the external constraints before they leave home.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>Advanced cognitive flexibility at 17-18 means handling rejection gracefully (adapting plans when the first-choice university says no), pivoting career plans based on new information, and managing the emotional shift of leaving home without identity collapse. These are adult adaptive skills. Encourage them to see every plan as a hypothesis subject to revision — not weakness, but intellectual maturity.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-dev"><div class="t-label">Shift: Developing</div>Developing shifting capacity at 17-18 — when major life transitions demand flexibility — requires advance preparation. Plan B should be built alongside Plan A for every major decision (university, gap year, career direction). Practice the emotional component of shifting: "If this doesn't work, how will I feel, and what will I do next?" Rigidity under stress is the primary risk; make the alternative plans concrete enough to feel real, not just theoretical.</div>

<h3>Dance — Social Identity</h3>
<p>They move well, lead or follow confidently, dance to unfamiliar music, and are never the person standing against the wall. Like cooking and material literacy — the capability is simply present because it was developed over 15 years.</p>
<p><strong>University social life.</strong> Dance is one of the most valuable social skills at university. Latin dance clubs, social dance events, parties, formal balls — the person who dances confidently makes friends faster and navigates social settings more naturally than the person who cannot. This is social independence made physical: they dance because they enjoy it, not because they are performing for approval.</p>
<p><strong>For the interested (instructor-required):</strong> advanced technique, travel to learn. Cuba for salsa, Seville for flamenco, Buenos Aires for tango, Seoul for urban choreography. The dance becomes a cultural passport.</p>

<h3>Acrobatics — Maintenance</h3>
<p>Physical vocabulary is complete. They can do a handstand, a cartwheel, and a <strong>standing backflip</strong> — a non-negotiable milestone if the gymnastics progression was followed from age 4. Their body is a trained instrument. This is maintenance territory, not development.</p>
<p><strong>Flexibility and tumbling are use-it-or-lose-it.</strong> 15 minutes, 3x/week keeps a lifetime of physical capability intact. Yoga, movement practice, or recreational gymnastics are all adequate vehicles. The investment of 14 years of physical training is protected by 45 minutes per week of maintenance. Do not let it atrophy.</p>

<h2 id="s-social" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">people</span>Social & Relational</h2>

<h3>Collaborative Work</h3>
<p>By 18, team sport may have become recreational or may be a serious pursuit. Either way, the targets it served (Relational Intelligence, Compassion & Kindness) should now be self-sustaining. Observable: they collaborate with people across age, status, and culture. They maintain deep relationships. They act ethically when it costs them something.</p>
<p>If these aren't observable, the earlier social development was insufficient. The fix at this stage is real-world collaborative projects with real stakes: co-founding something, organizing events, leading teams in internships or service work.</p>

<h3>Social Intelligence & Negotiation</h3>
<p>Professional social skills. Interview presence — can they hold a conversation with a 40-year-old professional in their field without shrinking? Negotiation (connects to BATNA model). The ability to connect with people across age, status, and culture. They understand that their network is not a collection of contacts but a web of genuine relationships maintained through mutual value. This is Target 7 (Relational Intelligence) and Target 8 (Compassion & Kindness) at maturity: sustaining deep relationships across distance and difference, acting ethically when it costs something, reading group dynamics with precision.</p>
<p><strong>Negotiation mastery.</strong> Real-world application: internship/job negotiation, university interviews, professional relationship building. Can negotiate salary, project scope, and deadlines with adults. Understands principled negotiation vs. positional bargaining. Second professional intensive if possible — different school than first (if Voss at 14-16, then Harvard PON or Karrass at 17-18). Maintain practice: negotiate something real every month. The skill atrophies without use.</p>

<h3>Adult Safety, Consent & Reputation</h3>
<p><strong>Leaving home requires adult-grade boundary competence.</strong> Consent under alcohol, status imbalance, dependency, or emotional coercion is not reliable consent. They need practical standards, not slogans: what to do when a situation turns ambiguous, how to leave, how to intervene for a friend, and how to ask for help without delay.</p>
<p><strong>Digital and legal reality.</strong> Private images are leverage. DMs become evidence. Employers, universities, partners, and enemies all inherit the internet. Add scams, fake contracts, predatory landlords, abusive bosses, extortion, and "easy money" offers to the threat model. The child should know what not to sign, what not to send, and when to escalate to adults, lawyers, school, or police.</p>
<p><strong>Health and harm reduction.</strong> Sexual health, STI testing, contraception, substance safety, emergency protocols, and transport plans belong here. The standard is not purity. The standard is staying uncoerced, unblackmailed, physically safe, and capable of protecting others.</p>

<h3>Substances — Adult Operating Manual</h3>
<p><strong>University reality.</strong> Alcohol is ubiquitous. Cannabis is common. MDMA, cocaine, and psychedelics are accessible. Vaping is everywhere. Prescription stimulant misuse (Adderall, Ritalin) is normalised as "study drugs." They will encounter all of this. The question is not whether but whether they have the knowledge to make informed decisions.</p>
<p><strong>Monitor your own relationship.</strong> "Am I using this to enhance an experience or to avoid a feeling? Am I using more than I planned? Am I using alone? Am I hiding use from people I trust?" These four questions, asked honestly, are the earliest warning system for problematic use.</p>
<p><strong>Drug interactions.</strong> Alcohol + benzodiazepines = respiratory depression (lethal). Alcohol + cocaine = cocaethylene (cardiotoxic). MDMA + SSRIs = serotonin syndrome (dangerous). Stimulants + sleep deprivation = psychosis risk. "Never combine substances without understanding the interaction. 'My friend did it and was fine' is not pharmacology."</p>
<p><strong>Know how to help.</strong> Recovery position. When to call emergency services (always err on the side of calling). How to talk to a friend about their substance use without moralising. Where professional help is available. "You are not responsible for other people's choices, but you are responsible for not walking past someone who is in danger."</p>

<h3>Service & Kindness</h3>
<p>Systemic engagement. Volunteer work should now connect to their emerging professional identity — medical volunteering for the aspiring doctor, legal aid for the aspiring lawyer, teaching for anyone. International service with real responsibility, not photo-op voluntourism.</p>
<p>The synthesis: by 18, they should understand that service is not charity but participation in the systems that sustain civilization. They should have a cause they care about, a track record of action, and a critical eye for what actually works.</p>
<p><strong>Luck as designed outcome.</strong> By 18, they should understand: "The opportunities that came to me happened because I was prepared (framework), exposed (institutions, network, programs), and kind (social capital). All three had to be true. I can control all three." This is the antidote to both entitlement ("I deserve this") and helplessness ("it's all luck").</p>

<h3>Network</h3>
<p><strong>Professional network seeds.</strong> At least one relationship with someone in a field they might pursue — not a career advisor, but someone who does the work. Internships, research assistantships, apprenticeships.</p>
<p><strong>International:</strong> Their peer network should include people outside Spain. Exchange, competitions, online intellectual communities, university pre-programs.</p>

<h3>Social Independence — Integration</h3>
<p>By 18, they have their own value system, arrived at through reasoning AND experience. They can articulate: "I believe X because of Y, and I've tested this belief by doing Z." They distinguish kindness from compliance. They are generous without being dependent on gratitude. They disagree without hostility. They hold unpopular positions without defensiveness.</p>
<p>They have experienced: public criticism, social rejection for a genuine choice, the approval of people they don't respect, and the disapproval of people they do respect. They have survived all four and know that none defined them.</p>
<p><strong>The internal locus of evaluation is operational.</strong> "I evaluate my work by my own standards. External feedback is data, not identity." This connects to the existing amor fati synthesis: if there is no given meaning, you must create it. A person who creates meaning from their own values, sustained by their own competence, is genuinely free from social control — not because they are antisocial, but because their identity has an internal anchor.</p>
<p>The causal chain is complete: material competence → reduced need → freedom from consumption pressure → identity anchored in capability → genuine social independence. The person who can make, repair, and understand needs less approval because they need less — period.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Professional networking happens through depth, not breadth. One genuine mentor relationship in their field outweighs twenty LinkedIn connections. Prepare for interview contexts explicitly: mock conversations with adults they don't know well. The negotiation skill atrophies faster without practice — build in monthly real-world negotiation exercises even if they resist the social energy cost.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Their social fluency is now a genuine professional asset. The maturity test: can they listen as well as they talk? Can they maintain a relationship across distance when there's no immediate social payoff? Push toward quality over quantity in their network — five deep relationships that survive university separation are worth more than fifty acquaintances.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Real-world negotiation — salary, scope, deadlines — may feel confrontational. Reframe: principled negotiation is collaboration, not conflict. Their ability to read emotional dynamics in rooms is a professional superpower; help them trust it and use it deliberately rather than being overwhelmed by it. Service work in emotionally intense contexts (healthcare, poverty) requires structured debriefing.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>Their network will be narrow but deep — a few people who know them extremely well. This is the right shape for recommendation letters and mentor relationships. Push them to maintain at least one relationship outside their primary domain: cross-pollination prevents intellectual insularity. Service work should connect directly to their niche — depth applied to impact.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-adv"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Advanced</div>Socially advanced young adults at 17-18 navigate adult relationships — professional networking, romantic partnerships, cross-generational mentorship, and financial independence conversations — with genuine competence. Push toward contexts that test adult social skills: internship negotiations, professional relationship building, living independently (even briefly), and managing their own finances. The goal is readiness to leave home as a socially functional adult, not just an intellectually prepared student.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-dev"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Developing</div>Developing social skills at 17-18, on the threshold of independence, requires focused investment. Practice the specific social skills university and work demand: introducing yourself to strangers, making small talk at professional events, navigating shared living situations, and handling conflict with people you can't avoid. Structured social exposure (internships, pre-programs, group travel) provides practice with a safety net. Don't send them to university without these skills rehearsed.</div>

<h2 id="s-building" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">construction</span>Building & Making</h2>

<h3>Coding</h3>
<p>If this is their niche: pre-professional. Strong in at least one language, comfortable in a second. Systems design thinking. Contributing meaningfully to real projects. If it's not their niche: functional literacy. Can automate a task, read code, evaluate whether a technical claim is plausible, and understand what software does and doesn't do well. Either way, they understand that code is a lever — it trades human time for machine time.</p>

<h3>Entrepreneurship & Projects</h3>
<p><strong>Portfolio of shipped projects.</strong> Multiple things they've built that exist in the world. This portfolio matters more than grades for most career paths.</p>
<p><strong>Business understanding:</strong> Can evaluate a business model. Understands equity, debt, revenue, margin, cash flow. Can read an annual report. Understands the principal-agent problem in employment (selling time vs. owning equity).</p>
<p><strong>Real investing.</strong> Open a brokerage account (with parental oversight), invest real money (small amounts). Execute at least one trade in each: equities, an ETF/index fund, and if legal in jurisdiction, a simple derivative (e.g., covered call on a stock they own). Understand index funds vs. active management — the evidence overwhelmingly favors index. Paper-trade futures and options to understand leverage and margin calls. <strong>Personal financial planning:</strong> income → expenses → savings rate → investment → compounding, as a system they design and maintain. <strong>Evaluating financial advice:</strong> fee structures (AUM vs. flat fee vs. commission), conflicts of interest, fiduciary vs. suitability standard. "Your banker is not your friend — understand their incentives before following their recommendations." <strong>Global tax literacy:</strong> how different jurisdictions tax income, capital gains, dividends, inheritance. Double taxation treaties. Holding structures (why companies incorporate in specific places). Wealth tax vs. income tax jurisdictions. Not to evade — to understand the system they will operate in and evaluate professional advice intelligently.</p>

<h3>Games</h3>
<p>At their full discretion. If gaming is their niche, it's now a potential career vector (game design, esports, content creation, game programming). If it's recreation, the framework trusts their judgment by now — they've been trained to budget attention.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>Shipped projects should be visually and physically impressive — design quality IS the differentiator for focused, detail-oriented builders. If coding is their niche, frontend, game development, or creative coding (generative art, data visualization) leverages their perceptual strength. Financial concepts enter better through visual models: build a spreadsheet dashboard, visualize portfolio allocation, chart compounding curves. Pre-professional depth in one stack or domain. Their portfolio tells a story of mastery: fewer projects, each more impressive. Open-source contribution to a major project, a single app with thousands of users, or a research contribution that required sustained technical investment.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>The portfolio should showcase range as a deliberate strength: a technical project, a creative project, a business venture, a community initiative. The connecting thread is taste and execution across domains. The risk at 17-18 is still non-completion; each portfolio piece must be genuinely shipped. Financial and business understanding comes naturally to generalists — lean into the full-stack entrepreneur identity.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>Advanced problem solvers at 17-18 have shipped real products, contributed to real research, or solved problems at a level that impresses domain professionals. The standard is pre-professional depth: code that production teams would accept, research that supervisors would co-author, or products with genuine market traction. Push toward the frontier of their domain — the unsolved problem, the novel approach, the contribution that didn't exist before them. Praise the intellectual courage to attempt hard problems, not just the solutions found.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>Developing problem solvers at 17-18 can still build impressive portfolios by choosing problems at the right difficulty level and using frameworks that compensate for raw problem-solving speed. Well-documented projects with clear methodology demonstrate thinking even when the technical depth is moderate. Collaborative projects where they contribute alongside stronger technical partners build skills while producing real output. The goal is evidence of systematic thinking, not genius-level solutions.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>Strong working memory at 17-18 enables managing the full complexity of adult life: academic workload, application processes, job/internship responsibilities, personal relationships, physical health, and financial management — simultaneously, without dropping threads. This is the executive function that makes independence viable. Challenge them to manage a complexity level that simulates the first year of university or work life, where no one reminds you what's due.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>Developing working memory at 17-18, on the threshold of independence, means the external systems must be robust enough to survive without parental oversight. Before they leave home, ensure they have: a trusted digital task manager they actually use, a calendar system that captures every commitment, and a weekly review habit that catches dropped threads. These systems ARE their working memory — the tool is as valid as the trait.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-adv"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Advanced</div>Advanced planners at 17-18 are designing their own life: career strategy with multiple contingency paths, financial planning with real numbers, university selection based on systematic evaluation criteria, and gap year or first-job planning with clear deliverables. This is life design as an engineering problem — and they're the lead engineer. Push toward articulating a five-year vision with annual milestones and quarterly actions. The plan will change; the planning habit will not.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-dev"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Developing</div>Developing planning skills at 17-18 requires converting anxiety about the future into structured action. Build the university/career plan together as a project with explicit phases, deadlines, and decision criteria. Introduce simple life-design frameworks: "What do you want your typical Tuesday to look like in three years? What has to be true for that to happen?" Transform vague aspiration into concrete planning steps. The planning system should be simple enough to maintain independently — complexity is the enemy of consistency.</div>

<h2 id="s-material-culture" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">handyman</span>Material Culture & Craft</h2>

<h3>Self-Sufficiency as Identity</h3>
<p>By 18, the child can: cook 20+ meals from memory, sew a button and hem a garment, repair a broken object before replacing it, evaluate the quality of a manufactured good by examining its construction, articulate why something costs what it costs, and furnish a room with quality and taste on a constrained budget. This is not a checklist to drill. It is the natural outcome of the craft progression from age 4. If they have been cooking since 4, sewing since 7, repairing since 10, and constructing since 11, these capabilities are simply present.</p>

<h3>Technical Self-Sufficiency</h3>
<p>Can build and upgrade a PC. Can wire a plug and understand residential circuits. Can fix a leaky tap and unclog a drain. Can change a tire and check oil. Can read and understand an electricity bill. Can perform a household energy audit. Can operate basic fabrication tools — 3D printer, and potentially laser cutter or CNC. Understands how a car engine works (both ICE and EV), how the electrical grid works, and how plumbing works. Can troubleshoot before calling a professional — and knows when to call the professional.</p>

<h3>Industrial Design Literacy</h3>
<p>Can evaluate product design: materials, ergonomics, manufacturing method, repairability. Has designed and fabricated at least one functional object through the full pipeline: sketch → CAD → prototype → iterate → finished product. Understands the difference between engineering for durability, engineering for cost, and engineering for planned obsolescence — and can identify which one they're looking at.</p>

<h3>Launch Project</h3>
<p><strong>Furnish the first independent space.</strong> University room, apartment, shared flat — whatever comes first. Explicit budget. The William Morris test applied to every object: is it useful? Is it beautiful? Is it neither? Set up its systems: internet, basic tool kit (screwdrivers, pliers, adjustable wrench, tape measure, drill, sewing kit, multimeter), repair supplies. The space they create is the physical expression of their material philosophy.</p>
<p><strong>Craft identity integration.</strong> "I am someone who understands how the physical world works — from the shirt on my back to the electricity in the wall to the engine in the car. This means I am not dependent on buying my way to comfort, status, or capability. I can make, repair, and understand. That competence is mine regardless of income, location, or circumstance."</p>

<h2 id="s-knowledge" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">lightbulb</span>Knowledge & Thinking</h2>

<h3>Ethics</h3>
<p>Their own ethical framework — arrived at through reasoning, not inherited. Not relativism ("everything is equally valid") but a defensible position: "I believe X because Y, and my framework fails in conditions Z." They can articulate where utilitarianism, deontology, and virtue ethics each provide the best answers and where each breaks down. They've encountered real ethical dilemmas in their projects and relationships and navigated them imperfectly but consciously.</p>

<h3>History</h3>
<p>Primary source history. Gibbon selections (Rome's fall as a lens for civilizational fragility). The 20th century as one continuous arc: WWI → Versailles → WWII → Cold War → decolonization → globalization → present fractures. Spain's specific role in this arc. They can answer "why did this happen?" for any major historical event with a multi-causal model, not a single explanation.</p>

<h3>Nature & Ecology</h3>
<p>Ecological literacy is now integrated into their systems thinking. They understand that every economic model, every political system, every technology operates within biophysical constraints. Climate change is not a "topic" — it's the context in which their generation will make every major decision. They should have a position on it arrived at through evidence, not tribal affiliation.</p>

<h3>Teaching</h3>
<p>"Can you teach this to a room? To a camera? To a skeptic?" Teaching as oral performance. By 18, they should be able to take any domain they know well and make it accessible to someone who doesn't. This is where written mastery and oral mastery converge: you need the structural precision of writing and the improvisational responsiveness of speaking. It is the ultimate test of understanding and the foundation of leadership, communication, and influence.</p>

<h3>Canon Knowledge</h3>
<p><em>Canon knowledge is the synthesis, not a checklist. A child who has gone through the onion layers arrives here with most of this naturally. These benchmarks confirm the substrate, not prescribe what to drill. High expectations are non-negotiable; rigid timelines are not.</em></p>

<h3>Cognitive Models</h3>
<p>By 18, cognitive models are not a topic — they're an operating system. The child who has traversed all five onion layers for each of the twelve universal models now thinks in models automatically. The final test: can they identify which model applies to a novel situation, deploy it, find its failure mode, and switch to a better one — all in real time?</p>
<p><strong>The Sequences — Eliezer Yudkowsky:</strong> Comprehensive treatment of rationality and decision theory. Builds on Kahneman with philosophical depth. Optional but valuable for the analytically inclined. Push for original synthesis: not just reading Popper and Kuhn but writing their own critique. The ethics position paper should be genuinely defensible in an undergraduate seminar. The risk: pure theory without application — insist every philosophical position connects to a decision they've actually made.</p>

<h3>AI Fluency</h3>
<p><strong>AI fluency is fundamental.</strong> By the time they graduate (~2044), AI will be as embedded in professional life as email is today. They should understand: what AI can and cannot do, how to use AI tools to amplify their work without replacing their thinking, the ethical implications of AI deployment, and how to remain valuable in an AI-augmented world.</p>
<p><strong>The human advantage.</strong> AI excels at pattern matching, synthesis, and scale. Humans excel at: genuine understanding (knowing WHY, not just WHAT), moral reasoning (should we, not just can we), novel creative vision (what has never existed), physical craft (embodied skill), and relational depth (trust, empathy, leadership). The framework's eleven targets are, not coincidentally, the capabilities that AI cannot replicate. A person who has developed all eleven is AI-proof — not because they avoid AI, but because they do what AI cannot.</p>
<p><strong>Evaluate any AI claim.</strong> "What training data produced this output? What biases might it contain? What would I need to verify before acting on this? Is this AI output genuinely useful or just fluent?" These questions, applied consistently, are the digital equivalent of the Baloney Detection Kit.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>Canon knowledge enters through making and visualizing for focused, detail-oriented children. The philosophical arc from Plato to existentialism can be mapped as a visual timeline with branching connections. Teaching through video, diagrams, and interactive explainers plays to their strengths. Ecological literacy is natural if framed as systems modeling rather than text-heavy analysis. Let them go deep on one philosophical tradition or one historical period rather than surveying all of canon knowledge at equal depth. A teenager who has genuinely internalized Stoicism or existentialism or a specific historical case study has more intellectual substance than one who has surveyed everything superficially. The canon knowledge table is a confirmation checklist, not an assignment list.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Existential philosophy hits differently for the sensitive mind. Dostoevsky's Grand Inquisitor and Nietzsche's "God is dead" are not just intellectual exercises — they can trigger genuine existential anxiety. Guide the engagement: these are questions to sit with, not problems to solve. Channel the intensity into their own ethical framework rather than letting it become free-floating dread.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>The canon knowledge table is their natural territory — they've been building cross-domain connections for years. The synthesis test is whether they can articulate how philosophy connects to economics connects to ecology connects to ethics. Push for original model-building: "You've seen patterns across all these domains. What's your model?" The Munger lattice approach was designed for minds like theirs.</div>

<h2 id="s-culture" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">travel_explore</span>Culture & Inner Life</h2>
<p>Travel with genuine purpose. Gap year component if appropriate — not backpacking as consumption but immersion as education. Work or volunteer abroad. Live in another language for an extended period. By 18, they should have experienced at least 3 genuinely different cultures (not just "visited" — experienced: eaten in homes, navigated alone, made local friends).</p>
<p>The goal: a young adult for whom "the world" is not an abstraction but a lived map of specific places, people, and systems they've engaged with directly.</p>
<p>Nietzsche: "God is dead" and its consequences — what happens when meaning-making systems fail? Dostoevsky: The Grand Inquisitor — faith, freedom, authority, the burden of choice. Existentialism: if there's no given meaning, you must create it. What do you create?</p>
<p>By now they should understand every major religious tradition as an intellectual system — its axioms, its strengths, its failure modes — and have a personal position (even if that position is uncertainty) arrived at through genuine reasoning.</p>
<p><strong>Amor fati — full synthesis.</strong> "If there's no given meaning, you must create it. What do you create?" Nietzsche, Dostoevsky, existentialism — but not as literature. As lived experience. The 18-year-old who has internalized process joy, archetype alignment, and love of fate has an inexhaustible fuel source that doesn't depend on external validation.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Gap year or international immersion works best with structure and solitude built in: a research position abroad, a solo language immersion, a residency program. Group backpacking is draining; purposeful solo travel with a clear project is energizing. Their inner life is likely rich — the existential questions (meaning, faith, purpose) may already be deeply engaged. Don't interrupt the process; deepen it through reading and journaling.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>The existential dimension hits hardest for sensitive adolescents. "If there's no given meaning, you must create it" is not just a philosophical proposition — it can feel like vertigo. Provide anchors: amor fati as practice (not just concept), process joy as daily reality, genuine relationships as meaning-generators. Living abroad in a genuinely different culture will be intense; prepare for culture shock as a feature, with structured reflection and trusted people to debrief with.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>They'll want to experience everything — every culture, every religious tradition, every philosophical school. This breadth is an asset for the comparative analysis the section requires ("understand every major religious tradition as an intellectual system"). Push for genuine depth in at least one tradition: read the primary sources fully, not just summaries across five traditions. The gap year, if taken, should have a core purpose, not a scattershot itinerary.</div>

<h2 id="s-planning" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">calendar_month</span>Planning & Milestones</h2>

<h3>Institution Decisions</h3>
<p><strong>Route choice is now concrete.</strong> University, conservatory, elite apprenticeship, FP superior, founder path, or a structured gap year. Pick intentionally. The question is no longer "what sounds impressive?" but "which arena actually rewards this child's demonstrated strengths?"</p>
<p><strong>University pre-programs (age 16-17).</strong> Real immersion, not tourism. Research assistantships, selective academic programs, and advanced seminars should test whether university life is what they think it is.</p>
<p><strong>Gap year planning.</strong> Not backpacking as consumption — immersion as education. Work abroad, research, language immersion, apprenticeship. Purposeful international exposure.</p>
<p><strong>The application is the portfolio.</strong> By 18, they have: a niche with demonstrable excellence, shipped projects, a professional network seed, a published/public body of work, and the communication skills to present all of it compellingly in any medium.</p>

<h3>Portfolio & Visible Excellence</h3>
<p><strong>The portfolio speaks for itself.</strong> Demonstrable spike, external validation, impact at scale, coherent narrative, route-appropriate gatekeeping cleared. By 18, the child has: a niche with competition results or equivalent public proof, shipped projects with real users, published or presented research or artistic work, a professional network seed, and the communication skill to present all of it in any medium.</p>
<p><strong>Application essays</strong> tell the story the life has already written. If the narrative arc was shaped from 14, the essays write themselves — they're just the articulation of a life already lived with intention.</p>
<p><strong>Portfolio website.</strong> Professional quality. Shipped work, published research, competition results, community impact. This is discoverable and represents them when they're not in the room.</p>
<p><strong>Recommendation letters</strong> from mentors who know them deeply. Specific examples, compelling stories, genuine advocacy. The 2+ years of relationship investment pays off here.</p>
<p><strong>Interview command.</strong> Communication Mastery (Target 6) pays off. Can hold a substantive conversation with a 40-year-old admissions officer or professor. Thinks on their feet. Asks good questions back. This is the culmination of years of Socratic dinner, debate, extemporaneous speaking, and negotiation practice.</p>
<p><strong>Route-specific packaging:</strong> US-selective route emphasizes essays, scores where relevant, recommendations, and spike. Spain / EU route emphasizes expediente, PAU/EvAU or equivalent, language credentials, olympiads, and interviews. Conservatory / arts route emphasizes audition or portfolio. Founder / apprenticeship route emphasizes product, revenue, references, and execution under real constraints.</p>

<h3>Summer Intensives</h3>
<p>Summer is preparation and execution. Real consequences. Real people.</p>
<div class="card-grid">
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Full Internship</div><p>8–12 weeks in a field they're seriously considering. Not generic "internship experience" — real contribution to a real organization. Determines if this is actually what they want.</p></div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">University Pre-Program</div><p>Summer at university doing real research or study. Advanced classes, lab work, seminar participation. Tests whether university life is what they think it is.</p></div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Independent Research Project</div><p>Original research or creative work with external outcome. Written thesis, curated exhibition, scientific paper, published work. Portfolio-building.</p></div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Travel with Purpose</div><p>Not tourism. Work abroad, service mission, language/cultural immersion, apprenticeship. Something that changes their understanding of the world.</p></div>
</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Application essays are their strength — the introspective narrative comes naturally. Interview prep requires more investment: weekly mock interviews starting 3-4 months before applications. Independent research projects and solo internships suit them better than group pre-programs. Gap year should be structured around a solitary project with clear deliverables, not open-ended social travel.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>University pre-programs and full internships are their accelerant — the social learning compounds fast. The application risk: essays that are charming but lack intellectual depth. Push for multiple revision cycles with honest feedback. Interview command is likely strong; the gap may be in the written portfolio. Recommendation letters will be warm and enthusiastic; ensure they're also specific and substantive.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Application season is emotionally brutal. Rejections feel personal; waitlists feel like limbo. Build the identity before the outcome: "You are not your admission result. The work exists regardless." Prepare them for multiple rejections as a statistical reality, not a personal verdict. Celebrate the portfolio itself — the shipped work, the relationships, the growth — independent of where it gets them admitted.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>Their application narrative is their strongest asset: sustained commitment to one domain, genuine depth, visible mastery. Target schools that reward this — research universities, specialized programs, departments known for deep work. The portfolio should be curated, not comprehensive: three exceptional pieces that demonstrate mastery, not ten adequate ones. Independent research project for summer is the natural fit.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>The application narrative requires synthesis: "Here is the thread connecting my diverse interests, and here is why that combination creates unique value." Target schools that reward interdisciplinary thinking: liberal arts colleges, programs with flexible curricula, universities with open minors and cross-department opportunities. Portfolio website should explicitly frame breadth as a design choice, not a lack of focus.</div>

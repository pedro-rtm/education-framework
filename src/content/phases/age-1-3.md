<h1>Age 1–3: Language Explosion & Motor Mastery</h1>
<p class="subtitle">Language is arriving. Motor control is developing rapidly. The world becomes manipulable.</p>

<div class="note-box">
<h3>Sub-Phases Within This Range</h3>
<p><strong>12–18 months:</strong> Walking arrives. First words. Primary job: safe exploration of physical space. Minimal structured activity — environment IS the curriculum. Balance bike introduction at 18 months.</p>
<p><strong>18–24 months:</strong> Language explosion (50 → 300+ words). Parallel play with peers. Practical life activities begin (pouring, dressing attempts, "helping" with cooking). Tantrums arrive — emotional regulation is pre-cognitive at this stage; stay calm and present.</p>
<p><strong>24–36 months:</strong> Sentences form. Imagination ignites. "Why?" begins. Real choices become possible. This is the transition to structured learning — shape sorters, categorization games, first puzzle books. Where's Wally becomes genuinely engaging. Reading sessions can extend to 15–20 minutes.</p>
</div>

<div class="priority-box">
<h3>Priorities — Your 20 Hours/Week</h3>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">1</span><strong>Language density.</strong> Talk, read, narrate constantly in English. This is the critical window for bilingual vocabulary building. Lean into conversation, storytelling, and word games. They may talk early and voluminously — feed it.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">2</span><strong>Autonomy scaffolding.</strong> Let them do things badly. Dressing, pouring, choosing. Resist the urge to help.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">3</span><strong>Outdoor physical challenge.</strong> Climbing, running, falling, getting up. Build the physical confidence substrate. Emphasize construction toys and physical manipulation: Duplo, sand play, water play. Books with dense visual information (Scarry, Wally) complement text-heavy narratives.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">4</span><strong>Reading ritual.</strong> Minimum 3 books at bedtime. English from you. Make it the best part of the day.</div>
</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Respect the need for solitary play. Don't force sharing or group play at this age — parallel play is normal and healthy until ~3. Quiet reading time is not isolation; it's recharging.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Maximize social exposure. Playdates, park groups, music classes with other children. They learn through social interaction more than solitary exploration. The interna's social schedule matters more.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Transitions are hard. Give warnings: "In 5 minutes we'll leave the park." Avoid overstimulating environments (loud, crowded). Smaller playgroups. Permission to leave when overwhelmed, without drama.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>May need more intense stimulation to engage with activities. Bigger physical challenges, louder music, messier sensory play. Don't mistake placidity for satisfaction — actively offer novel, high-intensity inputs and watch for the spark.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>When they obsess over one toy, one book, one activity — let them. Reading the same book 15 times is not repetition; it's mastery. Fewer toys available, more time with each. Resist the urge to introduce novelty when they're deep in something.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Rotate toys and books more frequently. Offer variety in the daily schedule — multiple short activities rather than extended single-focus sessions. Their breadth of interest is a strength; feed it with range, not depth pressure.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>If vocabulary is ahead of schedule (100+ words by 18 months, sentences by 24 months), increase language complexity — use subordinate clauses, introduce "because" and "but," ask open-ended questions ("What do you think will happen?"). Pull narration and storytelling activities from the 3-6 phase. Begin simple dictation: they tell a story, you write it down. Praise the effort: "You used a new word — you've been listening carefully."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-dev"><div class="t-label">Communication: Developing</div>If speech is limited or unclear, increase modelling without correction — when they say "ba" for ball, respond "Yes, the BALL! The big red BALL." Expand every utterance they attempt. More singing, more rhythmic repetition, more pointing-and-naming with physical objects. Reduce background noise during conversation time. If fewer than 50 words by 24 months, discuss with paediatrician — early intervention for speech is highly effective and time-sensitive.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>If they solve simple puzzles quickly (4-piece before 18 months, 8-piece before 24 months) and demonstrate systematic search strategies in Wally, introduce more complex challenges: puzzles with 12+ pieces, sorting by two attributes simultaneously (big red vs. small blue), simple matching games. Pull shape-sorting with rotation from the 3-6 phase. Praise the strategy: "You tried different pieces until you found the right one — that's how solving works."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>Simplify puzzles — use knobbed puzzles with obvious shape cues before moving to interlocking pieces. Demonstrate the solution, then let them try. Hand-over-hand guidance is fine for the first few attempts. Stay with the same puzzle until mastery before introducing new ones. Success builds the confidence to attempt harder problems. More cause-and-effect toys: water wheels, ball drops, ramps.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-adv"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Advanced</div>If walking is confident before 13 months, climbing is fearless, and balance is strong, push the challenge higher — balance bike earlier (15-16 months), playground climbing structures for older children (supervised), running on uneven terrain, jumping from low heights. Introduce ball-kicking and throwing. Praise effort and persistence: "You climbed higher than last time — you kept trying."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-dev"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Developing</div>If walking is late (after 18 months) or unsteady, increase supported walking practice — push toys, walking while holding both hands, then one hand. More time on varied surfaces (grass, sand, slight inclines). Avoid carrying when they could be practising walking. For late crawlers-turned-walkers, ensure they still get floor play for core strength. Consult paediatrician if not walking by 18 months.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>If pincer grip is precise and they manipulate small objects confidently, introduce threading beads on a lace, play dough tools (rolling pins, cutters), and drawing with thinner crayons. Practical life fine motor: buttoning large buttons, using a child-sized fork, pouring from a small pitcher. Praise precision: "You threaded the bead — your fingers did exactly what you wanted."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Stick with large-grip tools: chunky crayons, large knobbed puzzle pieces, big Duplo blocks. More scooping, squeezing, and tearing activities (tear paper, squeeze sponges, scoop sand). Play dough for hand strength. Avoid small beads or threading until pincer grip is reliable. Build up from whole-hand grasp to three-finger grasp gradually. Patience — fine motor lags behind gross motor and that is normal.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-adv"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Advanced</div>If they show early empathy (comforting a crying child), self-help skills (attempting to dress, feed themselves with utensils), and social initiation (approaching other children to play), increase social complexity — slightly older playmates, simple cooperative games (rolling a ball back and forth), more practical life independence (choosing clothes, setting their plate). Praise the social awareness: "You saw she was sad and went to her — that was kind."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-dev"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Developing</div>If self-help skills lag (not attempting to use a spoon, no interest in dressing) or social engagement is limited, simplify and scaffold. Hand-over-hand spoon use, dressing with only the last step left for them to complete (pull shirt over head after you've put it on). For social hesitancy, start with one familiar child in a familiar setting. Model social approaches explicitly: "Watch — I say hello, then I share the toy." Don't push independence faster than their confidence allows.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>If they can wait briefly for a snack, resist grabbing a forbidden object with a verbal reminder, or stop an action when asked, build on this — play simple "freeze" games (dance then freeze when music stops), practice "wait... now!" before meals, introduce "Simon Says" at a basic level by 30+ months. Praise the restraint: "You waited even though you wanted it right now — that was hard and you did it."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-dev"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Developing</div>If impulse control is minimal (grabs everything, cannot wait, frequent hitting or throwing), this is within normal range for under-3s — the circuitry is simply not mature. Reduce demands on inhibition: fewer "don't touch" scenarios, more redirection ("Let's do this instead"), physical barriers rather than verbal prohibitions. Consistent, calm limits. Do not punish failure to inhibit — it is neurological, not behavioural defiance.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>If they adapt well to changes in routine, accept substitutions ("We can't go to the park — let's go to the garden instead"), and show flexible categorisation (sorting the same objects by colour, then by size), increase cognitive flexibility demands. Change rules mid-game: "Now we stack only the blue ones." Vary routines slightly on purpose. Praise the adaptation: "We changed the plan and you went with it — that's flexible thinking."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-dev"><div class="t-label">Shift: Developing</div>If any change triggers meltdowns — different cup, new route to the park, unexpected visitor — keep routines maximally consistent and introduce changes gradually with advance warning. Use visual schedules (pictures of the day's activities in order). When a shift is necessary, validate: "I know this is different. That's hard. We're going to X instead." Forced flexibility at this age backfires. Build the secure base first; flexibility follows trust.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>If they follow two-step instructions reliably ("Get the ball AND bring it to Daddy"), increase to three steps. Play memory games: "Where did we put the red car?" after a 30-second delay. Sing songs with sequences they must remember. During Wally sessions, ask them to hold a target in mind: "We're looking for the dog — can you remember that while we search?" Praise remembering: "You held three things in your head — that's impressive work."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>If they struggle with even single instructions ("Go get your shoes" and they wander off), simplify to one step at a time, with visual or gestural support — point at the shoes while saying it. Repeat instructions with the same words. Use routines as memory scaffolds: the bedtime sequence is the same every night, so they don't need to "remember" — the structure remembers for them. Gradually extend from 1-step to 2-step instructions only after the 1-step is reliable.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-adv"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Advanced</div>If they show early capacity to recover from frustration without extended meltdowns, name the regulation strategy: "You took a deep breath — that helped you calm down." Introduce simple emotional vocabulary beyond basic (frustrated, disappointed, proud, nervous) and begin connecting emotions to causes: "You're frustrated because the tower fell." Praise the recovery: "You were upset and then you calmed yourself — that's a big skill."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-dev"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Developing</div>If tantrums are frequent, intense, and recovery is slow, prioritise co-regulation consistency. Same calming routine every time — deep pressure, rocking, low voice. Do not reason during the storm; process after calm returns. Create a physical "calm spot" (cushion corner, tent, soft blanket) they can retreat to. Tantrums at this age are normal — frequency and intensity vary by temperament, but the intervention is always calm, consistent presence. Avoid punishing emotional expression.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-adv"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Advanced</div>Plan/Organize is only lightly assessable before age 3. If they show early signs — lining up objects deliberately, attempting to put toys back in specific spots, or narrating a sequence before doing it ("First we put the blocks, then the roof") — reinforce the planning behaviour. Ask "What should we do first?" before activities. Praise the plan: "You thought about it before you started — that's planning."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-dev"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Developing</div>If there is no evidence of planning or sequencing (entirely impulsive play, no cleanup attempts, no anticipation of next steps), this is normal before age 3. Support with external structure: "First shoes, then outside" with consistent pairing. Cleanup routines with a specific song that signals the sequence. The routine IS the plan at this age — you are the external organiser until their prefrontal cortex comes online.</div>

<h2 id="s-communication" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">record_voice_over</span>Communication & Expression</h2>
<h3>Language — Written & Oral</h3>
<p>Critical OPOL window. Your English input is competing with Spanish from every other source. Quantity matters: the more English words they hear from you, the stronger the bilingual foundation. Read 3+ books per session. Narrate your actions. Sing English songs. Don't mix languages mid-sentence — clean separation helps the developing brain categorize.</p>
<p><strong>Interna:</strong> Should be reading Spanish picture books daily. Provide a list: Maisy (Lucy Cousins), Peppa Pig books in Spanish, Spanish nursery rhymes.</p>
<p><strong>L3 note:</strong> L3 exposure begins at age 6-8. At this stage, focus on deep L1+L2 bilingual foundation. See Philosophy tab for multilingual architecture.</p>
<p><strong>⚡ Window alert:</strong> This is the critical window for bilingual vocabulary building. English input from father competes with Spanish from every other source. Quantity of English exposure now determines bilingual depth for life.</p>
<p>Scribbling is pre-writing. Provide thick crayons, finger paints, chalk on pavement. Narrate their drawings: "Tell me about your picture." This builds the connection between marks and meaning. Dictation begins here too: they tell you a story, you write it down, they see their words become text. This is the seed of authorship.</p>
<p><strong>Oral expression begins.</strong> "Tell me about your picture" is already in play — now extend it: "Tell Mama what we did at the park today." They narrate events to a third party, organizing experience into spoken sequence. This is oral composition. Nursery rhymes and simple poems recited together build rhythm, memory, and the pleasure of spoken language as performance. Finger plays (Itsy Bitsy Spider, Pat-a-Cake) are the first oral performances — learned text delivered with gesture and expression. Expand every utterance: "Truck!" → "Yes, a big red truck carrying logs." Introduce rhyming games, silly words, and early wordplay. Dictation is especially powerful — their words becoming text on paper is electrifying. Language also develops through action: label objects during physical play ("You're stacking the tall tower"), use picture-heavy books (Scarry, Wally) where they can point before they can name, and narrate their drawings and scribbles.</p>
<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>They may not perform language socially but process it deeply in private. Don't mistake quiet in groups for language delay. One-on-one reading and conversation will yield more output than group settings. Let them warm up — rushing verbal performance in front of others backfires.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>If they produce sentences early, have large vocabulary, or name colours and objects ahead of peers, push complexity — introduce "why" and "how" questions, encourage retelling of events ("What happened at the park?"), begin simple categorisation games in language ("What animals can fly?"). Pull early literacy activities from the 3-6 phase: letter recognition through play, not instruction. Dictation is especially powerful — they narrate, you write. Praise the elaboration: "You told the whole story — you remembered every part."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-dev"><div class="t-label">Communication: Developing</div>If language production lags (fewer than 50 words at 24 months, no two-word combinations by 30 months), increase input density without pressure to perform. More singing, more repetitive books, more narration of their actions. Reduce questions ("What's this?") which can feel like tests, and increase modelling ("That's a dog! A big brown dog"). Expand every utterance: "Car" → "Yes! A fast red car." Early intervention referral for speech therapy is recommended if benchmarks are missed — the earlier the better.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>If they can recall characters and events from yesterday's reading, use books as memory exercise — "What happened to the caterpillar yesterday? What did he eat first?" Retelling strengthens both language and working memory simultaneously. Begin simple "what comes next?" prediction games with familiar stories. Praise remembering: "You remembered from yesterday — your memory held onto it."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>If they don't recall previous readings or struggle to follow a two-sentence narrative, re-read the same books more frequently. Repetition builds the memory trace. Use the same phrases each time — predictability reduces memory load. Point to pictures while narrating to anchor words in visual memory. Don't test recall; just model it: "Remember the caterpillar? He ate through the apple — look, there it is."</div>

<h2 id="s-music" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">music_note</span>Music</h2>
<p><strong>Active instruments:</strong> Xylophone, small drum, egg shakers, tambourine. Let them make noise with intention — rhythm emerges from chaos.</p>
<p><strong>Exposure:</strong> Continue diverse genre rotation. Add: children's music with real musical quality — Raffi, Ella Jenkins. Avoid the algorithmically-generated nursery rhyme content on YouTube (it's designed for engagement, not musicality).</p>
<p><strong>Classes:</strong> Consider Suzuki Early Childhood Education or a local music-and-movement class. The point is not instruction — it's rhythmic social experience. Movement is a primary music engagement channel at this age — dance, bounce, march, stomp. Xylophone and drums reward physical action with sound. Singing is equally powerful: nursery rhymes with actions, call-and-response songs, filling in the last word of familiar lyrics. Prioritize instruments they can hit, shake, or move with.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Music classes may overwhelm — the combination of noise, other children, and unfamiliar space compounds. Try one-on-one instrument exploration at home first. When attending classes, sit near the exit and leave without guilt if they shut down.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>If they manipulate instruments with control — holding a drumstick properly, pressing xylophone keys individually rather than sweeping — introduce more precise instruments: a triangle with striker, finger cymbals, or a simple recorder (just blowing). Praise the precision: "You hit just that one note — your fingers knew exactly where to go."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Stick with instruments that reward any contact: drums (any hit works), egg shakers (any shake works), tambourines. Avoid instruments requiring precise finger placement. The goal is musical participation and joy, not accuracy. Strap-on wrist bells let them make music through movement without requiring grip control.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>If they handle transitions well, play musical games with rule changes: "Now we march! Now we tiptoe! Now we freeze!" Tempo shifts (fast drumming → slow drumming on command) build cognitive flexibility through physical action. This is one of the best vehicles for practising shift at this age. Praise the switch: "You changed from fast to slow — you listened and switched."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>Musical freeze games are the ideal inhibition training vehicle at this age. Play music → dance → music stops → FREEZE. Start with obvious pauses and long freeze windows. Gradually shorten the warning. This is genuinely fun inhibition practice disguised as play. Praise the stop: "The music stopped and your body stopped too — that takes control."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-dev"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Developing</div>If they cannot stop when the music stops, play the game with longer pauses and physical support — gently hold them still during freeze, then release. Make it silly, not serious. The point is repeated practice of the stop-start pattern, not compliance. Over weeks, they will begin to freeze independently. No correction needed — just model and repeat.</div>

<h2 id="s-mind-body" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">fitness_center</span>Mind & Body</h2>
<h3>Physical System</h3>
<p>Outdoor daily regardless of weather. Climbing, jumping, running on uneven surfaces. Barefoot when safe. Balance bike from 18 months. Playground with challenge — not the safe toddler section.</p>
<h3>Self-Awareness & Emotional Intelligence</h3>
<p>Emotion vocabulary is the primary tool. "You look angry. Are you angry?" "That was scary." "You're so excited!" Name their emotions before they can — this gives them handles for experiences that would otherwise be overwhelming. Simple regulation techniques: deep breaths together (blow out the birthday candles), counting to five, hugging a specific comfort object. Post-tantrum, once calm: "What happened? You were angry because..." This builds the narrative-self connection.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Tantrums hit harder and last longer. They are not being dramatic — their nervous system genuinely amplifies emotional signals. Regulation takes more time and more repetitions. Prioritize physical co-regulation (holding, rocking, deep pressure) over verbal processing during the storm. Words come after calm.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>Emotions may be underexpressed, not absent. Ask more, assume less. "You seem quiet — are you feeling something?" They may need explicit permission and modeling to identify and express internal states. Don't let their composure fool you into skipping emotional check-ins.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-adv"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Advanced</div>If they run confidently, climb without hesitation, and balance well, increase physical challenge — balance beam (tape on floor → raised plank), jumping from heights (step, then low wall), kicking a ball with direction, catching a large ball. Consider balance bike progression to a pedal bike earlier (Woom 2 by 2.5 years if ready). Praise persistence over performance: "You fell and got right back up — that's how you learn."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-dev"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Developing</div>If walking is unsteady, running is hesitant, or climbing is avoided, increase supported physical play — hand-holding on uneven terrain, low steps to practice up-and-down, gentle rough-and-tumble play to build confidence in movement. More outdoor time on varied surfaces. Avoid over-protecting from falls — supervised minor tumbles are how they calibrate their body. Consult a physiotherapist if motor milestones are significantly delayed.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>If they use utensils well, draw with intention (circles, lines), and manipulate small objects confidently, introduce scissors (child-safety), play dough tools, and lacing cards. Practical life fine motor: spreading butter, opening containers, zipping jackets. These are the direct precursors to writing. Praise the detail work: "You cut along the line — your hands are getting very precise."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>If utensil use is clumsy, drawing is undifferentiated scribble, or they avoid fine manipulation, increase hand-strengthening play — squeezing sponges in the bath, tearing paper, play dough (just squeezing and pulling). Use adapted utensils with thicker handles. Drawing with finger paints before crayons. The progression is full-hand → three-finger → pincer. Don't skip to precision tools before grip strength and control are established.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-adv"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Advanced</div>If they can name their emotions and show early self-regulation ("I'm angry!"), build on this — introduce the concept of emotional strategies: "When you're angry, you can take three breaths, squeeze your bear, or tell me about it." Begin connecting emotion to cause and to solution. Praise the awareness: "You said you were frustrated — knowing what you feel is the first step to handling it."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-dev"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Developing</div>If emotional storms are frequent, intense, and they have no language for feelings, focus on co-regulation first and naming second. During calm moments (not mid-tantrum), practise emotion identification with books, picture cards, or facial expressions in the mirror. Build a minimal vocabulary: happy, sad, angry, scared. Use these consistently. The neural pathway is: co-regulation → emotion recognition → emotion naming → self-regulation. You cannot skip steps.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>If they can stop a physical action on request ("Wait before you pour"), extend this to more challenging contexts — waiting for a treat while it sits in front of them, not touching a new toy until told "okay." This is the toddler version of the marshmallow test. Keep it brief and game-like, not punitive. Praise the control: "You waited even though it was hard — that's self-control and it's a superpower."</div>

<h2 id="s-social" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">people</span>Social & Relational</h2>
<p>Parallel play (playing alongside, not with) is developmentally correct at this age. Don't force sharing or cooperation — it's neurologically premature. Instead, model turn-taking: "My turn... your turn." Narrate social situations: "She's crying because she wanted the ball. She's sad." This builds the social-emotional vocabulary that precedes actual social skills.</p>
<p><strong>Kindness seeds.</strong> Model turn-taking, sharing, and gentleness. Narrate kind acts: "You gave her the toy. That was kind." Frame kindness as something they DID (agency), not something they ARE (identity).</p>
<p>Start building the family network deliberately. In Madrid: international school parent communities (even before enrollment), English-speaking playgroups, your own social circle's children. The 3–4 families you befriend now will likely be your children's first peer group for years.</p>
<p><strong>Team sport note:</strong> Team sport begins at age 4. These early years build the social substrate through playgroups and parallel play — the relational foundation that makes team dynamics possible later.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Social situations drain faster. Limit playdates to one other child initially. Avoid birthday parties with 15 toddlers and a bouncy castle — that's sensory hell, not socialization. Build relationships through repeated, calm, familiar settings with the same few children.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-adv"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Advanced</div>If they show early cooperative play (before 3), offer toys to others spontaneously, or demonstrate empathy (comforting a crying peer), increase social complexity — introduce simple collaborative tasks ("Let's build a tower together"), very basic board games (colour-matching games by 2.5), and structured playdates with slightly older children who model more advanced social behaviour. Praise the social act, not the trait: "You shared the blocks with her — she was happy" rather than "You're such a good sharer."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-dev"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Developing</div>If they avoid other children, do not share or take turns even with modelling, or show significant separation anxiety beyond 18 months, keep social settings small and highly familiar. One other child, same location, same routine. Model social scripts explicitly and repeatedly: "Say 'my turn' — now give it to him — now he gives it back." Avoid forcing social contact. Proximity without pressure — being near other children without interaction requirements — is a valid intermediate step.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>In social settings, if they can resist grabbing a toy from another child (even briefly), reinforce that restraint heavily. "You wanted the truck but you waited — that's really hard to do." Practice "asking first" as a social-inhibition script: "Can I have a turn?" requires pausing the grab impulse and substituting a verbal request. This is the intersection of inhibition and social skill.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>If they adapt to different social rules in different settings (gentle at the library, louder at the park, patient with a baby), name the flexibility: "You knew to be quiet here — you changed your behaviour for the place." Social flexibility is cognitive flexibility made visible. Introduce role-play: "You be the doctor, I'll be the patient" requires holding a new perspective while maintaining social interaction.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-dev"><div class="t-label">Shift: Developing</div>If they rigidly apply the same behaviour everywhere (shouting in the library, demanding the same toy at every house), provide explicit contextual cues before entering each setting: "At Maria's house, we play gently. At the park, we can run and shout." Use visual cues or a consistent verbal script for each environment. Don't expect them to infer social rules from context — spell them out every time until the pattern internalises.</div>

<h2 id="s-building" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">construction</span>Building & Making</h2>
<p>None. No screens before 3.</p>
<p>Shape sorters, nesting cups, simple puzzles (4-12 pieces). Brio World wooden train layouts — the track-building IS spatial reasoning (which piece connects? does the loop close? what happens if I add a bridge?). Block towers with intentional collapse ("how tall before it falls?"). These are not toys — they are the physical instantiation of spatial logic. Narrate the building process: "What are you making? Tell me about it." They may build stories more than structures — a Duplo tower becomes a castle with characters and a narrative. That's valid building. Encourage verbal planning: "What should we build?" before starting. Provide increasingly complex construction challenges: Magna-Tiles for 3D, Brio track with bridges and tunnels, Grimm's Rainbow for freeform sculpture. Silence during construction is concentration, not disengagement.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>If they stack precisely, connect Duplo with intention, and place Magna-Tiles deliberately, introduce more demanding construction — Brio track layouts requiring alignment, Grimm's Rainbow balance structures, and Magna-Tiles enclosures (walls, roofs). Provide smaller building components alongside larger ones. Praise the precision: "You balanced that piece on top — you placed it so carefully."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>If stacking beyond 3-4 blocks is difficult or Duplo connection is unreliable, stay with large, forgiving materials — mega blocks, nesting cups, soft stacking rings. Demonstrate, then let them try. Celebrate every tower, regardless of height. The act of placing one block on another IS the fine motor workout. Don't jump to Magna-Tiles or Brio until Duplo is comfortable.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>If they plan structures before building ("I'm making a house"), introduce design challenges: "Can you build a bridge the car can go under?" "Can you make a fence around the animals?" These are the first engineering problems — constraints that require thinking before acting. Pull simple KAPLA-style challenges from the 3-6 phase. Praise the planning: "You thought about what you wanted to build before you started — that's how real builders work."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>If building is random stacking with no apparent goal, model intentional construction alongside them — narrate your process: "I'm going to put this big one at the bottom so it's stable." Don't direct their building, but let them observe yours. Offer problems with obvious solutions: "This ball needs to go in the cup — which cup fits?" Keep the gap between challenge and current ability small.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-adv"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Advanced</div>If they show early signs of sequencing in construction (gathering materials first, then building), reinforce the planning behaviour. Before building: "What do we need?" "What should we build first — the walls or the roof?" After building: "What did you make? Tell me about it." This verbalisation of the building plan is the earliest executive planning visible in play. Praise: "You got everything ready before you started — that's planning."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>If they can hold a building goal in mind while working ("I'm making a tower for the giraffe"), increase complexity by adding constraints mid-build: "Can you make it taller? Now can you add a door?" Holding the original goal while incorporating new requirements stretches working memory. Brio track layouts are excellent — remembering the destination while building the route. Praise: "You remembered you were building a road to the farm — even when it got tricky."</div>

<h2 id="s-knowledge" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">lightbulb</span>Knowledge & Thinking</h2>
<p><strong>Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman:</strong> Essential reading for cognitive biases and heuristics. System 1 vs. System 2 thinking. Understanding your own mental errors is foundational to better decision-making.</p>
<p>Name everything outdoors. Plants, insects, weather, soil, puddles. "What does the ant carry? Where is it going?" Seasonal awareness: leaves falling, flowers blooming, rain patterns. Outdoor time minimum: 1.5 hours daily regardless of weather (the interna brief specifies this). Nature is the default environment; indoors is the exception.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>When they ask "why?" about ants for the fifteenth time, go deeper each time. Get a magnifying glass. Watch an ant trail for 30 minutes. Find a book about ants. The depth child doesn't want breadth of nature — they want to understand ONE thing completely before moving on.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Cover more ground — literally. Different parks, different trails, different ecosystems if accessible. Name broadly: birds, trees, rocks, clouds, puddles, flowers. They are building a wide catalog of the natural world. Don't linger when their attention moves on; follow their scanning pace.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>If they ask "why" and "how" questions early, answer at a level above what you think they understand — they will stretch to meet it. "Why does rain fall?" gets a real answer: "Water goes up into the clouds and when there's too much, it falls back down." Introduce simple experiments: "What happens if we leave water in the sun?" Pull structured investigation from the 3-6 phase. Praise the question: "That's such a good question — let's find out."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>If they don't yet ask investigative questions, model them yourself — pick up a leaf and say "I wonder why this one is yellow and that one is green?" Demonstrate wonder as a behaviour. Simple observation narration: "Look — the puddle is getting bigger because it's raining." Connect cause to effect explicitly and repeatedly. They will absorb the questioning pattern before they produce it.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>If they can name many objects and are building sentences, use outdoor time as intensive vocabulary expansion — introduce specific terms (sparrow not just bird, oak not just tree, drizzle not just rain). Ask them to describe what they observe: "Tell me about that bug — what's it doing?" Verbal description of the natural world fuses language with scientific observation. Praise the specificity: "You called it a snail, not just a bug — you're learning exact names."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>During nature walks, play "remember and find" — show them a leaf, put it away, then ask them to find another one that looks the same. Or set a scavenger hunt: "Find something round, something soft, and something red." Holding multiple search targets in mind exercises working memory in a natural context. Praise remembering: "You found all three things — you held them all in your mind."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>If they can't hold nature observations from one walk to the next, collect physical tokens — bring leaves, stones, or feathers home and look at them again. Physical objects are external memory aids. Use the same walking route repeatedly so the environment itself becomes familiar and they can notice what's changed: "Look — there's a new flower where there wasn't one last week." Repetition builds the memory scaffold.</div>

<h2 id="s-culture" data-tier="recommended"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">travel_explore</span>Culture & Inner Life</h2>
<p>Weekend trips and short holidays within Spain. Museums (Prado, Reina Sofía — even briefly, at their pace). The rhythm of travel: packing, airports/trains, new beds, new foods. Normalize the unfamiliar. Europe by car or train: Portugal, France, Italy — close enough for short trips. No educational agenda. Just exposure.</p>
<p>Not explicit yet. But the seeds: wonder at nature, questions about existence ("where was I before I was born?"), awe. Don't shut these down with rational explanations — sit with the mystery alongside them. "That's a really big question. What do you think?" This is Layer 0 of the meaning-making progression (see Philosophy tab).</p>
<p><strong>Joy signal.</strong> Watch what produces genuine delight — not compliance, not performance for adults. The activities that make them lose track of time are the signal. Protect those.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Travel disrupts routine, and routine is their regulation infrastructure. Keep trips short (2-3 nights max). Bring familiar objects — same blanket, same bedtime book. Museums and cultural sites in very short doses with an exit plan. The goal is positive association with new environments, not endurance.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>If they adapt well to new environments — different hotels, unfamiliar restaurants, changed schedules — push the range of cultural exposure. New foods in new settings, bilingual interactions with strangers, unfamiliar cultural contexts (a flamenco show, a local market in a new city). Praise the adaptation: "Everything was new today and you explored it all — that's brave and flexible thinking."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-dev"><div class="t-label">Shift: Developing</div>If travel and novelty cause significant distress, keep cultural exposure close to home — new restaurants in your own neighbourhood, a different park in your city, a short museum visit with the same snack and comfort object as always. Familiarity anchors make novelty tolerable. Gradually extend: same museum, new gallery; same city, new restaurant. Don't abandon cultural exposure — just scaffold it with consistency.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-adv"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Advanced</div>If they handle the frustrations of travel (waiting, disrupted naps, unfamiliar food) without extended meltdowns, use travel as emotional vocabulary expansion: "You were disappointed the pool was closed. You handled it really well — you felt disappointed and then you moved on." Travel is a natural emotional regulation laboratory. Praise the coping: "That wasn't what you expected, and you were okay — that's resilience."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-dev"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Developing</div>If travel triggers emotional dysregulation, build in more downtime and predictability anchors. The bedtime routine should be identical regardless of location. Carry a "travel calm kit" — familiar blanket, comfort toy, favourite book, a familiar snack. Preview changes: "Tonight we sleep in a different bed, but we still read our books and cuddle." The goal is to make the emotional environment consistent even when the physical environment changes.</div>

<h2 id="s-planning" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">calendar_month</span>Planning & Milestones</h2>
<div class="card-grid">
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Parent Evenings (2 hours)</div>
<p>Arrive → 20 min active play (their choice) → dinner together (conversation, not screens) → bath → 3+ books in English → bedtime routine. Same sequence. Predictability is still the infrastructure.</p>
</div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Weekends (Full days)</div>
<p>Morning: outdoor adventure (park, nature walk, playground with challenge). Afternoon: one structured activity (museum, music class, cooking together) + free play. Evening: family reading time, extended bedtime ritual. One weekend day can be more unstructured — follow their lead entirely.</p>
</div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Interna Weekdays</div>
<p>Morning outdoor time (1.5+ hours). Practical life activities (helping with food prep, cleaning, dressing). Spanish reading (2–3 books). Nap. Afternoon: one outing or activity + free play. Key instruction: "Don't rush them. Don't rescue them. Let them struggle. Go outside."</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Build solo play into the daily structure as non-negotiable recharge time — not filler between social activities. After playgroups or outings, schedule quiet at-home time. Weekend days should alternate between social and solitary. The interna should not fill every gap with a social outing.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Maximize social time in the weekly plan. More playdates, more park time with other children, more music classes. Quiet solo play will happen naturally at nap boundaries — don't over-engineer it. Weekends should be socially rich. Brief the interna to prioritize group activities.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Build decompression buffers after every transition and every social event. Never schedule two stimulating activities back to back. The interna brief should include specific overstimulation signals and a clear protocol: when you see X, do Y. Bedtime routine may need to be longer and quieter.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-adv"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Advanced</div>If they show early signs of planning (gathering items for an activity, narrating what they'll do before doing it), use the weekly cadence to reinforce: "What should we do at the park today?" before leaving. Visual schedules (pictures of the day's sequence) give external structure that advanced planners begin to internalise. Let them "help plan" the bedtime routine: "What book should we read first?" Praise: "You thought about what you wanted before we started — that's planning ahead."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-dev"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Developing</div>If they show no planning behaviour (entirely reactive, no anticipation of routines, no gathering of materials), this is normal before age 3. You are the planner. Make the plan visible and verbal: "First we put on shoes, then we go to the park." Use picture schedules for the daily routine. Consistency IS the plan — the same sequence every day builds the expectation scaffold. Over time, they will begin to anticipate the next step without being told.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>In the daily schedule, use natural waiting moments as practice: "Wait until I say go" before starting a meal, a game, or an outing. Keep waits short (5-10 seconds) and praise the restraint. Transition moments are inhibition moments — "We're going to leave the park in 2 minutes. When I say 'time to go,' we go." If they comply, that IS inhibition in action. Praise: "You stopped playing when it was time — that's hard and you did it."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-dev"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Developing</div>If transitions trigger meltdowns and waiting is impossible, reduce the demand. Give shorter warnings, use physical transition cues (picking up, carrying to the car) alongside verbal ones, and avoid extended waiting periods. Provide a "transition object" — something to hold or carry during the shift from one activity to the next. Don't frame non-compliance as defiance; the inhibitory circuit is simply not yet ready. Patience and consistency are the only intervention.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>Use the planning cadence to exercise memory — "Remember, after lunch we're going to the park, then to buy bread, then home for a bath." Can they recall the sequence an hour later? Routines with 3+ steps where they must remember "what comes next" without prompting are active working memory training. Praise: "You remembered the whole plan — bath, then books, then sleep. That's a lot to hold in your head."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>If they cannot hold even the daily routine sequence in mind, keep the verbal plan to one step at a time: "Now we put on shoes." Complete. "Now we go to the door." Complete. Visual schedules with pictures are essential — they offload memory to the environment. Use the same phrases in the same order daily. The routine itself becomes the memory, not their internal recall. Gradually extend from 1-step to 2-step as the routine stabilises.</div>
</div>

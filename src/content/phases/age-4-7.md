<h1>Age 4–7: First Real Virtue Work</h1>
<p class="subtitle">Abstract reasoning is emerging. Peer comparison begins. The first window where deliberate virtue cultivation produces real results.</p>

<div class="note-box">
<h3>Sub-Phases Within This Range</h3>
<p><strong>4–5 years:</strong> School begins. Peer comparison starts ("am I good at this?"). Executive function actively being built through play. Instrument instruction can start. Martial arts can start. Reading readiness varies widely — don't push. Focus: protect play, start the niche search, begin the Socratic dinner ritual.</p>
<p><strong>6–7 years:</strong> Reading fluency arrives (for most). Abstract reasoning emerging. Beast Academy becomes accessible. Chess becomes real. Longer hikes feasible. This is when the framework's deliberate virtue work starts producing visible results. Focus: deepen the niche commitment, extend reading sessions, introduce first real responsibilities.</p>
</div>

<div class="priority-box">
<h3>Priorities — Your 20 Hours/Week</h3>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">1</span><strong>Bedtime reading in English.</strong> Extended sessions — 30–45 minutes. Chapter books begin. This is language, bonding, content, and imagination in one activity.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">2</span><strong>Niche mastery activity launch.</strong> Identify the domain showing genuine pull (not compliance). Instrument, chess, martial art, or whatever produces flow. Resource it aggressively: best teacher available.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">3</span><strong>Dinner table Socratic ritual.</strong> Pick a character from whatever they're consuming. "What does [character] want? Why? Is that reasonable?" 10 minutes, every few days.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">4</span><strong>Weekend physical challenge.</strong> Hiking 3–5 km, martial arts, swimming, cycling. Build physical confidence and hardship tolerance together.</div>
</div>

<div class="target-grid">

<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">2</span><h4>Agency & Ethical Reasoning</h4><p>Natural consequences operate without rescue — no jacket means cold. Post-mortem together, analytically.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">3</span><h4>Self-Awareness & Self-Regulation</h4><p>Encourage self-description: "I'm the kind of person who..." Zones of regulation and recognizing physiological signals.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">4</span><h4>Deep Curiosity</h4><p>Protect and feed whatever they hyperfocus on — the obsession IS the curriculum. Museums as regular practice.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">5</span><h4>First-Principles</h4><p>Socratic at dinner: "Why do you think that?" "What if the opposite were true?" Start with story characters, generalize.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">6</span><h4>Communication Mastery</h4><p>Storytelling games at dinner, explain-it-back after any film, debate character decisions. Writing stories begins.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">7</span><h4>Relational Intelligence</h4><p>Team sport begins — coordinating with others toward a shared goal under rules they didn't set.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">8</span><h4>Empathy & Kindness</h4><p>Helping as habit: cook, clean, donate toys. "Other beings need things, and you can provide some."</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">9</span><h4>Physical System Mastery</h4><p>Cold-weather outdoor play, long hikes, martial arts with real contact. They carry their own water.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">10</span><h4>Adaptability</h4><p>Open-ended creative play with no right answer. KAPLA and LEGO become primary curriculum vehicles at this age.</p></div>
<div class="target-card"><span class="target-num">11</span><h4>Creative Production</h4><p>Open-ended building, drawing, storytelling, and cooking. The "Make" phase of the engagement cycle begins here.</p></div>
</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>The niche mastery activity can be solitary (instrument practice, chess puzzles, drawing) rather than group-based. Reduce extracurricular scheduling — one activity per day maximum. Protect solo reading/building time as sacred, not as "doing nothing."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Team martial arts, group music classes, collaborative building projects. The dinner Socratic ritual may work better as a family debate format. Playdates are learning environments — use them.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Martial arts selection matters — avoid aggressive dojos. Jiu-Jitsu (ground-based, technical) over Muay Thai (striking). Hiking difficulty should ramp slowly. Cold exposure is VOLUNTARY — model it enthusiastically but never force. "I'm going to splash cold water on my face — want to try?"</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>Push harder on physical challenges. Longer hikes, colder conditions, more demanding martial arts. They need more intensity to reach the productive discomfort zone.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Don't fight the breadth before age 7. Let them sample widely: 6 months of chess, then 6 months of drawing, then instruments. The long-term commitment can wait. Track what produces the deepest engagement — that's the signal for specialization.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>This child is your dream student. Feed the obsession mercilessly. If it's dinosaurs for 18 months straight, go to paleontology museums, buy university textbooks with pictures, watch documentaries. Don't redirect — deepen.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>This child is ready for age 8-10 communication work early. Introduce essay-structure thinking ("beginning, middle, end — now tell me why"), simple debate formats at dinner, and written storytelling with revision. Push L2 Spanish toward narrative construction — not just vocabulary but "tell me a story in Spanish." Cross-reference the age 8-10 phase's presentation and debate exercises. Praise the effort of structuring an argument, not the vocabulary used.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-dev"><div class="t-label">Communication: Developing</div>Scaffold oral expression before demanding written output. Use sentence starters: "I think... because..." Model complete sentences and let them echo the structure. Story dictation (they narrate, you write) removes the motor bottleneck so language development isn't gated by handwriting speed. No time pressure on verbal responses — processing time is not a deficit.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>Accelerate to age 8-10 problem-solving activities: Beast Academy at the earliest recommended age, KAPLA challenges with specific structural constraints ("build a bridge that holds this book"), and introduce Minecraft Creative with design briefs rather than free play. Simple coding logic (Cubetto sequences with 8+ steps) is accessible now. Praise the strategy they chose, not the speed of the solution.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>Break multi-step problems into visible single steps. Demonstrate the full solution once, then let them attempt it with the model still visible. KAPLA works well because errors are immediately physical — the tower falls, and that's the feedback, not an adult's correction. Reduce variables: simpler LEGO sets with fewer pieces, puzzles with larger pieces, one-step coding sequences before multi-step.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-gm-adv"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Advanced</div>This child is ready for structured sport specialisation earlier. Introduce martial arts with technique emphasis (not just play-fighting), extend hike distances to 5-7 km, and consider climbing wall progression from bouldering to top-rope by age 6. Cross-reference the age 8-10 phase's multi-day hiking and competitive sport entries. Praise persistence through physical difficulty, not natural athleticism.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-dev"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Developing</div>Focus on fundamental movement patterns before sport-specific skills: running, jumping, throwing, catching, balancing. Obstacle courses at home or in parks build coordination without the pressure of team performance. Swimming instruction should prioritise water confidence over stroke technique. Shorten hike distances and increase rest stops — the goal is completing the walk, not the distance.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>Introduce precision tasks from the age 8-10 phase early: origami with tighter folds, drawing from observation (not imagination), and handwriting with attention to letter formation. KAPLA tower challenges requiring careful placement at height train steadiness under consequence. Woodworking with real tools (supervised) can start at 5 rather than 6-7. Praise the care taken in placement, not the final product.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Larger manipulatives first: Duplo before LEGO, thick crayons before pencils, tearing paper before cutting with scissors. Playdough and clay strengthen hand muscles that handwriting requires. Don't push pencil grip correction aggressively — the motor patterns are still forming. Demonstrate, then let them attempt at their own pace with the model visible.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-social-adv"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Advanced</div>This child grasps theory of mind early — they can reason about what others believe and feel. Lean into the age 8-10 phase's social intelligence work: "What was that argument really about?" and group dynamics observation after team sport. Introduce perspective-taking exercises with increasing complexity: "Why did your friend react that way? What did she think was happening?" Praise the act of considering another's viewpoint, not the social outcome.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-dev"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Developing</div>Explicit coaching on social scripts: how to join a game already in progress, how to ask for a turn, how to say no. Role-play social scenarios at home before they encounter them live. Parallel play alongside peers is still valuable — don't force cooperative play before they're ready. Debrief social friction gently afterward: "What happened? What did you want? What did they want?"</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>This child can wait their turn and resist impulses earlier than peers. Challenge them with multi-rule board games (Ticket to Ride: First Journey, Catan Junior) where they must hold a strategy across several turns while resisting reactive moves. Introduce the concept of "wait and watch before acting" during team sport — the player who reads the field before passing develops faster. Praise the decision to pause, not just the outcome of waiting.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-dev"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Developing</div>Simplify the inhibitory demand: one rule at a time, clearly stated before the activity begins. Use visual cues — a "stop" hand signal, a red card on the table — as external brakes until internal ones develop. Games like Red Light/Green Light and Simon Says build the stop-go circuit through play. Don't interpret impulsive behaviour as defiance; the brake pedal is still being wired.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>Introduce multi-rule games where rules change mid-play: card games with rotating wild cards, building challenges where the material switches halfway. This child can handle "now do it a different way" without frustration. Cross-reference the age 8-10 phase's subject-switching demands. Cognitive flexibility games (Sort It Out, Blink) are accessible and genuinely challenging. Praise willingness to abandon a strategy that isn't working.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-dev"><div class="t-label">Shift: Developing</div>Transitions are the friction point. Give advance warning before any change: "In five minutes we're stopping LEGO and starting dinner." Use visual schedules so the sequence of activities is predictable. Avoid surprise rule changes in games — consistency builds the security from which flexibility eventually grows. When they get stuck on one approach, gently model an alternative rather than insisting they switch.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-adv"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Advanced</div>This child recovers from frustration quickly and can re-engage after setbacks. Push the difficulty ceiling higher — harder puzzles, longer practice sessions, more challenging physical tasks — because their emotional regulation can handle productive struggle. Introduce the concept of "frustration as signal that you're learning" explicitly. Begin simple breathing techniques (blow out the candle, smell the flower) as conscious tools, not just emergency measures. Praise the recovery, not the absence of frustration.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-dev"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Developing</div>Reduce task difficulty so that frustration doesn't exceed their current capacity to regulate. Co-regulate first: sit with them, name the feeling ("You're frustrated because the tower fell"), and model calm. Provide a physical reset option — squeezing a stress ball, jumping ten times, getting a drink of water — before expecting them to return to the task. The goal is completing the cycle (upset → calm → re-engage), not preventing the upset.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>This child can hold multi-step instructions. Give three-part directions ("Get your shoes, put on your jacket, and meet me at the door") and increase to four. Cooking recipes with 4-5 steps, building from verbal instructions without looking at the picture, and memory-based card games (Concentration with 20+ pairs) are all appropriate challenges. Cross-reference the age 8-10 phase's multi-step math and research work. Praise the effort of holding information in mind, not just the correct execution.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>One instruction at a time, delivered at their eye level, with a pause for processing. Use visual checklists — pictures of each step in a routine (morning: toilet, brush teeth, get dressed, eat breakfast) posted where they can see them. Externalise memory demands: write it down, draw it, put a physical reminder in their path. Reduce the number of items to remember, not the complexity of each item.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-plan-adv"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Advanced</div>This child naturally anticipates steps. Give them ownership of simple project planning: "You want to build a castle — what do you need? What order should you do it in? What might go wrong?" KAPLA and LEGO builds with a drawn plan before building begins introduce the plan-execute-evaluate cycle. Cross-reference the age 8-10 phase's project planning and time management work. Praise the quality of the plan, not just the finished product.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-dev"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Developing</div>Provide the plan externally: a visual sequence of steps, a pre-sorted set of materials, a template to follow. Model planning aloud ("First I need to... then I'll... and finally...") so the internal monologue has a template. Don't expect them to plan before doing — let them start, then pause to reflect: "What's the next step?" Planning is a learned skill, not an innate trait, and it develops later than parents expect.</div>

<h2 id="s-communication" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">record_voice_over</span>Communication & Expression</h2>

<h3>Language — Written & Oral</h3>
<div class="music-track language-track">
<div>
<h4>Written Track</h4>
<p>Daily journal, even if it's a drawing with one sentence. The habit matters more than the quality. Letters to grandparents (in English and Spanish) — writing with a real recipient teaches that writing is communication, not assignment. Story dictation for ambitious narratives: they tell, you transcribe, they illustrate. By 6-7, they write their own short stories. Extend journaling to full stories early, and introduce simple poetry writing by age 6, not just listening. Debate and negotiation exercises can be surprisingly complex at this age — let them argue both sides of a position.</p>
<p>The Yoto Player becomes a writing tool too: after listening to a story, "What happened? Tell me in three sentences." This bridges listening comprehension to written expression.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h4>Oral &amp; Performance Track</h4>
<p><strong>Show-and-tell at home.</strong> Weekly: pick an object, stand in front of the family, explain what it is and why it matters. Insist on standing, facing the audience, and speaking loudly enough to be heard. No reading from notes — they speak from knowledge. This is the first formal public speaking exercise.</p>
<p><strong>Storytelling.</strong> Rory's Story Cubes and dinner storytelling games. "Tell us a story about a dragon who was afraid of fire." Improvised oral narrative builds real-time composition skill that writing cannot train.</p>
<p><strong>Poetry exposure.</strong> Read Shel Silverstein and A.A. Milne aloud together. Not forced memorization — appreciation. "Listen to how that sounds. Why is it funny? What makes it stick?" Poetry is language at its most compressed and musical.</p>
</div>
</div>
<p><strong>L3 exposure window opens.</strong> Age 6-8 is optimal for introducing a third language through immersion (summer program, au pair, media). Not classroom instruction — exposure first. See Philosophy tab for multilingual architecture. French is highest-utility from Madrid; the specific choice follows the child's interest and available immersion.</p>
<p><strong>⚡ Window alert:</strong> Native accent acquisition narrows after age 7-10 (Hartshorne et al., 2018). The bilingual foundation built in these years determines accent quality for life. L3 exposure window opens at age 6-8 — summer immersion, au pair, or media exposure in the target language.</p>

<h3>Negotiation & Sales</h3>
<p><strong>Structured requests.</strong> "I want X" evolves to "I want X because Y, and I can offer Z." Every request is a negotiation. Frame it that way from age 4.</p>
<p><strong>Show-and-tell as selling.</strong> Weekly show-and-tell at home isn't just public speaking — it's the first sales pitch. "Convince us this object is interesting." They learn to read the audience and adjust.</p>
<p><strong>Trading and bartering.</strong> Toy swaps with friends, negotiating dessert choices, deciding who gets to pick the movie. Every micro-negotiation builds the muscle. Don't resolve their conflicts for them — coach from the side.</p>

<h3>Digital Creation</h3>
<p>Not screen time — creation time. A child who uses a tablet to watch is a consumer. A child who uses it to create is a producer.</p>
<p><strong>Photography.</strong> Give them a camera (old phone, kid camera). "Take a picture of something beautiful. Now something funny. Now something that makes you curious." Composition begins here.</p>
<p><strong>Stop-motion animation.</strong> LEGO figures + a phone + Stop Motion Studio app. They're directing films by age 6.</p>

<h3>Visual & Spatial</h3>
<p>Drawing as observation, not "art class." Draw what you see: the plant, the house, your shoe. This trains the eye-hand-mind loop that underpins all spatial thinking. Simple maps: draw our apartment, draw the route to school. Graph reading: weather charts, their own height-tracking chart. LEGO and Brio at this age are spatial reasoning curricula disguised as play.</p>

<h3>Language Milestones</h3>
<p><strong>L1+L2:</strong> Both languages at conversational fluency by 5-6. Reading readiness in the dominant language (Spanish, via school) by 5-6; English reading follows 6-12 months later. By 7: can read simple chapter books in the dominant language, picture books in the minority language. Accent in both languages should be native or near-native if OPOL has been consistent.</p>
<p><strong>L3 window opens:</strong> Age 6-8 is the introduction point for L3 (French or other). Private tutor or immersion, not classroom. The goal is conversational exposure, not fluency yet.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Written expression will be the stronger channel. Lean into journaling, letter-writing, and story dictation over oral performance. Show-and-tell can start as family-only audience — one or two people, not a crowd.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Oral performance is their natural strength — storytelling games, show-and-tell, dinner debates. Channel the verbal energy into structured formats: "You have one minute to convince us." Let them narrate stop-motion films and podcast-style recordings.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>Communication enters through making. Stop-motion animation, photography, map-drawing, and comic strips are all communication — don't privilege text over visual narrative. Let them illustrate stories rather than write them, then caption the illustrations.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Public speaking can trigger genuine anxiety at this age. Start show-and-tell with just one parent as audience and scale up slowly. Never force oral performance in front of unfamiliar adults. Written and visual expression may be safer first channels.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>Push toward narrative construction in both languages: "Tell me a story with a beginning, a problem, and an ending." Introduce reading readiness activities from the age 8-10 phase — simple book arguments ("I liked this book because...") and structured retellings. L2 Spanish can move beyond vocabulary to full sentences and simple stories. Praise the complexity of the narrative structure attempted, not the correctness of grammar.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-dev"><div class="t-label">Communication: Developing</div>Prioritise receptive language (listening comprehension) before demanding expressive output. Read aloud extensively — the Yoto Player and bedtime reading are doing the heavy lifting. For oral expression, accept single-word or short-phrase responses and expand them: child says "dog," you respond "Yes, a big brown dog running fast." Don't correct pronunciation mid-conversation; model the correct form naturally in your response.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>In communication contexts, this child can wait to be called on and resist interrupting. Formalise turn-taking in dinner conversations with a physical "talking object" they must hold to speak. Introduce the "think before you speak" pause: count to three silently before answering a question. These exercises transfer directly to classroom readiness. Praise the discipline of waiting, not just the quality of the contribution.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-dev"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Developing</div>Blurting out answers and interrupting are developmentally normal at 4-5. Use gentle redirection ("I want to hear that — wait until Maria finishes") rather than punishment. Keep audience-facing activities very short (1-2 minutes for show-and-tell) so the inhibitory demand doesn't exceed capacity. Visual turn-taking cues (a special hat, a microphone toy) externalise the rule.</div>

<h2 id="s-music" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">music_note</span>Music</h2>
<p><strong>START FORMAL INSTRUCTION.</strong> This is the launch window.</p>
<p><strong>⚡ Window alert:</strong> Music ear training is optimal before age 7. The neural circuits for pitch discrimination and tonal memory are shaped during this sensitive period. Starting at 4-5 is ideal; starting at 8 is workable but harder; starting at 12 requires significantly more effort for less return.</p>
<div class="music-track">
<div>
<h4>Classical / Reading Track</h4>
<p>Instrument selection: <strong>Piano</strong> is the best first instrument (visual layout of theory, both hands independent, harmonic). Alternative: violin (Suzuki method) or ukulele.</p>
<p>Staff notation from day one. Scales, simple pieces, sight-reading exercises. Weekly lesson with a teacher who demands precision.</p>
<p><strong>Private instruction is the default.</strong> 1:1 with the best teacher available — not group conservatory class. A performing practitioner who demonstrates, not just explains. No budget limit on the primary instructor. Group lessons are supplementary for ensemble/social skills.</p>
<p>Daily practice: 15–20 minutes, same time each day, non-negotiable.</p>
</div>
<div>
<h4>Jazz / Ear / Improv Track</h4>
<p>Parallel from the start: ear training — play a note, they sing it back. Play a 3-note phrase, they repeat it.</p>
<p>Call-and-response games. "I play this, you play something back." No wrong answers — build comfort with musical spontaneity.</p>
<p>Clap rhythms, sing melodies by ear, make up songs together.</p>
</div>
</div>
<p><strong>Live music exposure:</strong> Take them to performances. Classical concerts (children's matinees), jazz clubs (afternoon sessions), any live music. Madrid has excellent options: Auditorio Nacional children's programs, jazz at Cafe Central.</p>

<p><strong>Film scores as appreciation.</strong> <a href="#universe-star-wars">John Williams (Star Wars themes)</a>, <a href="#universe-ghibli">Joe Hisaishi (Ghibli soundtracks)</a>. Recognize leitmotifs: "That's Darth Vader's theme — why does it sound menacing?" Connects music to narrative universes they already love.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Private 1:1 lessons are already the default and will suit them best. Avoid group recitals early — build confidence through mastery first, performance later. Practice is solitary by nature; this child won't resist it.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Add ensemble or group music classes alongside private instruction. Duets with a parent or friend, family jam sessions, call-and-response games with siblings. They need a social dimension to sustain motivation through the tedious early months.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Teacher temperament is critical — find someone patient and encouraging, not demanding. Loud instruments or aggressive correction can create lasting aversion. Start with quieter instruments (ukulele, piano at low volume) and let them build tolerance for their own mistakes gradually.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>They may find early practice boring before they're competent enough for it to be rewarding. Keep sessions short and varied. Drums or percussion can channel physical energy into rhythm. Add movement — marching, dancing — to keep engagement high during the fundamentals grind.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>One instrument, pursued seriously. They'll want to play the same piece fifty times until it's perfect — let them. Resist the temptation to broaden too early. Deep mastery of a single instrument at this age builds the neural architecture for all future music learning.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Let them try multiple instruments before committing. Six months of piano, then ukulele, then recorder is fine at ages 4-5. By age 6-7, gently narrow to one primary instrument with ear training as the constant thread. The exploration phase builds musical vocabulary even if no single instrument sticks yet.</div>

<h2 id="s-mind-body" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">fitness_center</span>Mind & Body</h2>

<h3>Physical System</h3>
<p>"Exercise makes you smarter" — stated plainly, because it's true. Running or active play before focused work (reading, music practice) measurably improves attention. Morning outdoor time before school sets the neurochemical stage for the day. This isn't health advice; it's cognitive optimization.</p>
<p><strong>Sleep is THE intervention.</strong> 10-13 hours. No screens 1 hour before bed. Dark room, cool temperature. A well-rested child who does nothing else from this framework outperforms a sleep-deprived child who does everything. This is not a suggestion — it's the single most evidence-backed claim in the entire framework (Walker, 2017).</p>

<h3>Self-Awareness & Emotional Intelligence</h3>
<p><strong>Critical window.</strong> Name-it-to-tame-it: naming an emotion literally reduces amygdala activation (Lieberman et al., 2007). Teach zones of regulation — color-coded energy levels (blue = low/sad, green = calm/ready, yellow = anxious/excited, red = angry/explosive). Practice recognizing physiological signals: "My stomach feels tight when I'm nervous. My face gets hot when I'm angry."</p>
<p>Post-meltdown debrief (only when fully calm, never during): "What happened in your body before you got upset? What could we try next time?" This builds metacognitive awareness of emotional states — the foundation for all later self-regulation.</p>
<p><strong>Self-knowledge begins.</strong> "I'm the kind of person who..." — encourage self-description. "You're really good at X. What do you think you're good at?" Not performance praise — genuine observation prompting self-reflection. "You figured that out by yourself — how did you do it?" builds awareness of their own thinking process.</p>
<p><strong>Joy as signal.</strong> Watch what produces genuine flow — sustained engagement, loss of time sense, intrinsic motivation. These activities are the signal for niche mastery direction. If an activity never produces flow after consistent exposure, question whether it's the right fit. Protect flow states ruthlessly: when they're absorbed, don't interrupt for meals, transitions, or "better" activities.</p>

<p><strong>Climbing.</strong> Indoor bouldering from age 5+. Every route is a spatial puzzle solved with the body. Problem-solving as physical activity. Low barrier to entry, scales infinitely. Complements martial arts — different movement vocabulary.</p>

<h3>Dance & Movement</h3>
<p><strong>Creative movement class (1x/week, instructor-led).</strong> Not ballet yet — creative movement: responding to different tempos, moving through space, mirroring partners, group choreography. Builds body awareness and rhythm without technical rigidity. Requires an instructor who understands developmental movement, not just choreography.</p>
<p><strong>Flamenco palmas progression.</strong> Clapping increasingly complex rhythmic patterns. Madrid has children's flamenco workshops (instructor-led for formal class). Even basic palmas at home (parent-guided) trains polyrhythmic awareness at a level no other Western dance form touches. The 12-beat compas is one of the most complex rhythmic structures in Western music — exposure now makes it intuitive later.</p>
<p><strong>Freestyle dance at home (parent-guided).</strong> Regular family dance sessions: put on music, everyone dances. No instruction, no correction. The habit must be established before the self-consciousness window (11-13). Music plays, bodies move. This is non-negotiable.</p>
<p><strong>Beat synchronisation games (parent-guided).</strong> Clap to the beat. March to different tempos. Drum circle with pots and spoons. These are rhythm training disguised as play.</p>
<p><strong>Ballet foundations begin at 6-7 (instructor-required).</strong> Pre-ballet or ballet basics class, 1x/week. Posture, alignment, basic positions (1st through 5th), plies, tendus, port de bras. Not performance — grammar. Ballet teaches the vocabulary of movement: where your body is in space, how to hold a line, how to transfer weight precisely. Requires a qualified ballet instructor (RAD or Vaganova trained). Bad ballet habits are worse than no ballet — alignment errors compound and cause injury. Same principle as the framework's "Private Instruction Default" for music: the best available teacher, never compromise.</p>

<h3>Gymnastics & Acrobatics</h3>
<p><strong>Structured gymnastics class, 1-2x/week (instructor-required).</strong> Foundational tumbling: forward rolls, backward rolls, cartwheels, handstands (wall-assisted progressing to freestanding). A qualified gymnastics coach prevents the injury patterns that self-taught tumbling produces — this is the same principle as martial arts instruction. The technique must be correct from the start.</p>
<p><strong>Supplementary at home (parent-guided):</strong> handstand practice against a wall, forward rolls on soft surfaces, flexibility stretching 3x/week.</p>
<p><strong>Flexibility — this is the golden window.</strong> Splits progression, bridges, shoulder flexibility. Flexibility comes easiest before puberty and gets dramatically harder after. Invest now or pay 10x the effort later. Daily stretching (10 minutes) is the minimum. A child who can do the splits at 7 can maintain them for life with modest upkeep; a teenager starting from scratch may never get there.</p>
<p><strong>Balance beam (low), vault basics, bar work</strong> (hanging, swinging, pull-overs). These develop the vestibular confidence and upper-body strength that later acrobatics require.</p>
<p><strong>Trampoline</strong> as a supplementary tool if available: aerial awareness, spatial orientation while airborne, and core control. A garden trampoline with safety net builds air sense cheaply.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Emotional regulation work is especially critical. They feel everything at higher amplitude — meltdowns will be bigger and recovery longer. Zones of regulation give them a vocabulary for what's happening inside. Never dismiss intensity: "I can see this is really big for you" before any problem-solving.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>Emotional awareness needs explicit teaching — they may not naturally notice internal signals. Use concrete physical anchors: "Where in your body do you feel that?" They need more help building the interoception habit because the signals are quieter, not because they don't exist.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>Flow states will be long and intense. A depth-seeking child absorbed in building or reading for two hours is not "stuck" — they're in their optimal mode. Protect these blocks aggressively. Schedule physical activity and emotional check-ins around the flow, not through it.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Flow may look different — shorter bursts across multiple activities rather than one long immersion. Don't mistake variety-seeking for lack of focus. Track which activities produce the brightest engagement across the sampling, and note the common thread (it's often a skill type, not a domain).</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-gm-adv"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Advanced</div>Physical confidence is ahead of schedule — use it. Extend martial arts to include sparring elements, push climbing routes to harder grades, and introduce cycling on roads (supervised) earlier than planned. The age 8-10 phase's multi-day hikes and competitive sport entries become viable from age 6-7. Praise the willingness to attempt harder routes, not the natural coordination.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-dev"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Developing</div>Focus on movement confidence before sport-specific skill. Playground time is gross motor therapy disguised as play — climbing, swinging, hanging, jumping. Swimming should prioritise comfort in water over stroke technique. Martial arts classes that emphasise games and movement patterns over formal technique are the right fit. Don't compare their physical timeline to peers; motor development has a wide normal range at this age.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>Handwriting readiness is ahead — introduce letter formation with pencil control exercises (mazes, dot-to-dot, tracing). Origami folds at the simple-to-intermediate level, bead threading with small beads, and LEGO Technic with small pin connections are all appropriate. The precision required for instrument playing (piano fingering, violin bow hold) will come more easily. Praise careful execution, not speed.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Strengthen the hand muscles before demanding precision: playdough, clay, tearing paper, squeezing sponges. Use thicker writing implements (chunky crayons, triangular pencils) and larger drawing surfaces (chalkboard, easel). KAPLA is ideal because the tolerance for imprecision is built into the material — slight misalignment and the tower falls, providing natural feedback without adult correction.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-adv"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Advanced</div>This child can name and modulate emotions earlier than peers. Introduce more sophisticated emotional vocabulary ("frustrated" not just "angry," "disappointed" not just "sad") and begin connecting emotions to physiological states explicitly. The zones of regulation work can progress to self-monitoring: "What zone are you in right now?" without prompting. Praise the self-awareness, not the emotional state itself.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-dev"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Developing</div>Co-regulation is the primary mechanism — your calm is their calm. During meltdowns, reduce sensory input (quiet voice, dim lights, fewer words) and offer physical comfort. The post-meltdown debrief must wait until they are fully regulated, which may be 30+ minutes later. Keep a consistent calming routine (specific corner, specific blanket, specific breathing exercise) so the pathway becomes automatic.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>Multi-step classroom instructions are manageable. Practice at home with increasingly complex sequences: cooking recipes with 3-4 steps from memory, Simon-style memory games, and "I went to the market and bought..." verbal chains. The morning routine can become self-managed from a memorised sequence rather than visual checklist. Praise holding instructions in mind across a delay, not just immediate recall.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>Classroom instruction-following will be a challenge. Provide external supports: visual schedules at home, picture-based task sequences, and physical placement cues (shoes by the door, backpack on the hook). Repeat instructions at most twice, then guide physically. Working memory develops substantially between ages 5-7 — support now builds the scaffold for the leap ahead.</div>

<h2 id="s-social" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">people</span>Social & Relational</h2>

<h3>Team Sport</h3>
<p>Team sport begins at age 4. This is the primary vehicle for Targets 11-12 (Relational Intelligence, Empathy & Kindness). Not for competition — for the experience of coordinating with others toward a shared goal under rules.</p>
<p><strong>Options:</strong> Football (most accessible in Madrid), basketball, rugby, hockey. The specific sport matters less than: (1) it's genuinely team-based, (2) there's a coach who develops character not just skill, (3) the child plays with children they didn't choose.</p>
<p><strong>What to watch for:</strong> How do they handle a teammate's mistake? Do they encourage or blame? How do they respond to being benched? Can they follow a captain who isn't their friend? The emotional learning happens in these moments, not in the goals scored.</p>
<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Smaller team sports (basketball, futsal) may be less overwhelming than 11-a-side football. One team sport session per week is sufficient — don't overload social energy. Recovery time after matches is real, not avoidance.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Team sport is their natural habitat. They'll want more — let them. This is where their social energy becomes a strength. Multiple team activities are fine if the schedule allows.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Aggressive coaching styles are harmful. Find a coach who develops, not dominates. Contact sports may need gradual introduction. Watch for overwhelm after losses — they may need longer recovery than peers.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>They may not notice a teammate's frustration or a coach's subtle correction. Debrief after practice: "Did you see how Miguel reacted when you took the ball? What do you think he felt?" Explicitly teach social signals that high-sensitivity children pick up automatically.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-social-adv"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Advanced</div>This child reads social situations accurately and cooperates naturally. Push toward leadership roles in team contexts: "Can you help the new kid learn the rules?" Introduce age 8-10 social intelligence concepts — group dynamics observation, active listening practice, and perspective-taking beyond the immediate ("How do you think the coach felt when the team didn't listen?"). Praise the act of noticing others' needs, not just their own social success.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-dev"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Developing</div>Social skills are learned, not innate. Practice specific scenarios at home through role-play: "Let's practice asking to join a game." Provide explicit scripts for common situations (sharing, turn-taking, entering a group). Smaller group settings (2-3 children) are more manageable than large team environments. After social events, debrief one specific interaction rather than the whole experience.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>In team sport, this child can wait for the right moment to pass or shoot rather than reacting impulsively. Introduce strategic waiting games: "Hold the ball until someone is open" in football, "Wait for the right card" in board games. This transfers to social contexts — learning that the first response isn't always the best one. Praise the choice to wait when waiting was harder than acting.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-dev"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Developing</div>Team sport may surface impulsive behaviour that looks like poor sportsmanship but is actually immature inhibition. Choose coaches who understand developmental differences. Simplify game rules so there are fewer impulses to inhibit simultaneously. Post-game, focus on one specific moment: "Remember when you grabbed the ball from your teammate? Next time, try asking first." One concrete behavioural target per session.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>This child adapts to changing social rules and new group dynamics quickly. Challenge them with environments where the norms differ — different coach's style, a pickup game with unfamiliar children, a new activity where they're the beginner. Cognitive flexibility in social contexts is a direct predictor of friendship quality. Praise the ability to adjust their approach when the situation changed.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-dev"><div class="t-label">Shift: Developing</div>New social environments are harder. Prepare them before transitions: "Today's practice will be at a different field with some children you don't know." Maintain consistent social anchors — the same friend at the new activity, the same parent watching from the sideline. Rigid insistence on "how we always do it" is a flexibility signal, not defiance. Model adaptability explicitly: "This is different from usual, but we can try it this way."</div>

<h3>Social Intelligence</h3>
<p>Reading facial expressions — practice with pictures, then real situations. Conflict resolution script: "Use your words. What do you want? What does she want? How can you both get some of what you want?" This three-question framework is the seed of negotiation theory. Model active listening: repeat back what they said before responding. Social intelligence can also be taught through narrative: "What was that argument really about?" Post-game debriefs and conflict analysis resonate strongly. Team sport is often the ideal social vehicle — children bond through shared physical activity as naturally as through conversation. Let the sport do the social teaching; don't over-verbalize the lessons.</p>
<p><strong>First negotiations.</strong> "I want X" evolves to "I want X because Y." Structure requests: what do you want, why, and what can you offer in return? Turn-taking in disagreements. "What does the other person want? Can you both get something?" Every conflict is a negotiation — frame it that way from the start.</p>

<h3>Service & Kindness</h3>
<p>Helping as habit, not event. Help cook, help clean, help carry groceries for the elderly neighbor. Donate toys they've outgrown — let them choose which ones and hand them over personally. Visit an animal shelter. The principle: other beings need things, and you can provide some of those things. This is ethics made physical.</p>

<h3>Social World & Network</h3>
<p><strong>First institutional affiliations.</strong> The martial arts dojo, the music school, the chess club — these are network decisions as much as skill decisions. Choose institutions where the other families value something seriously.</p>
<p><strong>Peer selection:</strong> One or two children who are observably ahead of them in some domain. Not to create competition — to normalize high performance as something peers do.</p>
<p><strong>Madrid-specific:</strong> Chess clubs (Club de Ajedrez Madrid), British Council youth programs, music conservatory preparation programs, international school extracurriculars.</p>

<h3>Social Independence</h3>

<h4>Stories as Curriculum</h4>
<p>"Horton Hears a Who" (Dr. Seuss) — one person was right when everyone else was wrong. "A person's a person no matter how small." "Ferdinand" (Munro Leaf) — the bull who would rather smell flowers than fight. Being different from your peers is fine. "Swimmy" (Leo Lionni) — cooperation AND individual vision. "The Emperor's New Clothes" (Hans Christian Andersen) — the child who said what was true when everyone else was pretending. These are not just stories. They are case studies in social independence, told at the right developmental level.</p>

<h4>Practices</h4>
<p><strong>Celebrate unpopular choices explicitly.</strong> When the child makes a choice different from their peers — different game, different food, different activity — name it positively: "You decided differently from everyone else. That takes courage."</p>
<p><strong>Practice saying "no" and "I don't want to" as complete sentences.</strong> Role-play: "Your friend wants to play X but you want to play Y. What do you say?" The ability to refuse without guilt is a foundational life skill. Don't undermine it by teaching compliance as the default.</p>
<p><strong>One visible domain of difference.</strong> The niche mastery commitment already creates this — a child who plays chess seriously at 6 or practices violin daily is already different from peers. Name it positively: "You're the kind of person who goes deep into things. That's unusual and excellent."</p>
<p><strong>Never force social conformity for your own comfort.</strong> Do not say "just go along with it" about anything that doesn't involve safety. If the child wants to wear the unusual hat, bring the book to the party, or order something different from everyone else — let them. These are low-stakes practice runs for high-stakes independence later.</p>
<p><strong>Comfort challenges (age 6–7).</strong> Wear an unusual hat to the park. Bring a book to a party. Order something different from everyone else at a restaurant. Share something you made, not something you bought. Low-stakes practice at being visibly different. Debrief after: "How did it feel? Did anything bad happen?"</p>

<h2 id="s-building" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">construction</span>Building & Making</h2>

<h3>Coding</h3>
<p>Logic games and sequencing. Robot Turtles (board game), Cubetto, or Bee-Bot for physical programming. Unplugged coding: "Give me step-by-step instructions to make a sandwich" — they discover that computers (and people following instructions literally) need precision. Pattern recognition games. "What comes next?" sequences.</p>

<h3>Entrepreneurship & Projects</h3>
<p><strong>Build:</strong> Physical construction — KAPLA, LEGO without instructions, woodworking with real tools at 6–7 (supervised, real saw and hammer, not plastic). The pride of making something real and functional.</p>
<p><strong>Monetize:</strong> Moonjar system — save, spend, share. Weekly allowance of €5–10, real decisions with real consequences. "You spent it all on candy. That means no money for the toy next week. That's what happened." No bailouts.</p>

<h3>Games & Digital Play</h3>
<p><strong><a href="#universe-minecraft">Minecraft</a> Creative mode only.</strong> No survival, no enemies — pure spatial construction and systems exploration. This is digital KAPLA blocks. Shared with parent, not solo unsupervised.</p>
<p><strong>Also permitted:</strong> Monument Valley, Thinkrolls, age-appropriate puzzle games. Total screen time capped and budgeted.</p>
<p><strong>The rule:</strong> Games are a tool, not a default activity. They're chosen deliberately, not fallen into from boredom.</p>

<p><strong>Origami.</strong> Start with simple folds (crane, boat, frog). Geometry made physical — angles, symmetry, spatial reasoning through paper. Inexpensive, portable, meditative. Progresses to modular origami (polyhedra) by age 7.</p>
<p>Coding logic games (sequencing, instructions) click quickly at this age. Supplement physical building with narration: "Tell me what you're building and why." Written instructions for their creations bridge building to communication. Extend KAPLA to woodworking early. Minecraft Creative mode produces genuinely impressive structures. Introduce real tools (hammer, saw, supervised) by age 6. 3D puzzles and mechanical kits add complexity.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Solo building is their sweet spot. KAPLA, LEGO, and Minecraft Creative are ideal — long, focused, independent construction sessions. Don't force collaborative building projects. Pair programming (Robot Turtles, Cubetto) works best 1:1 with a parent, not in a group.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>One building medium, mastered deeply. If it's LEGO, go from Creative box to Technic to Architecture series. If Minecraft, let them build entire cities over months. Don't rotate materials — let them exhaust one system's possibilities before introducing another.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Rotate building media: KAPLA one month, LEGO the next, then clay, then woodworking. Each medium teaches different spatial constraints. The variety itself is the curriculum — learning that the same design intent requires different techniques in different materials.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>Introduce design constraints: "Build a bridge with only 50 KAPLA blocks that holds this cup." Move from free building to building with specifications — this is engineering thinking. Coding logic (Cubetto, Robot Turtles) can advance to Scratch Jr. by age 5-6, ahead of the typical age 8 Scratch introduction. Minecraft Creative mode benefits from project briefs: "Build a house with three rooms and a garden." Praise the approach to the problem, not the elegance of the solution.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>Free play before structured building. Let them explore materials without a goal first — KAPLA towers that fall, LEGO constructions that don't look like anything. The problem-solving instinct develops from play, not instruction. When they're stuck, demonstrate one step and hand back control rather than completing the task. Coding games should use the simplest mode: 2-3 step sequences in Cubetto before longer chains.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>Precision building is ready: LEGO Technic small-pin assemblies, detailed origami (crane, modular cube), and woodworking with real tools (hand saw, hammer, supervised). Minecraft Creative mode develops spatial precision through block-by-block placement. Cross-reference the age 8-10 phase's miniature painting and electronics work — both may be accessible from age 6-7. Praise the precision of placement, not the speed of construction.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Larger building materials reduce frustration: Duplo, large KAPLA blocks, big LEGO bricks. Avoid tasks where fine motor imprecision causes visible failure (small LEGO Technic pins, detailed origami) until hand strength and coordination develop. Playdough construction (rolling, shaping, cutting) builds the hand muscles that precision tasks require. Let the material itself set the difficulty — they'll self-select toward smaller pieces as dexterity improves.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-plan-adv"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Advanced</div>Before building, draw the plan. "What are you going to make? Sketch it first." This child can anticipate material needs ("I'll need the long blocks for the base") and sequence steps. Introduce project timelines for multi-session builds: "Today we'll do the foundation, tomorrow the walls." This is age 8-10 project planning in embryonic form. Praise the planning process, not just the finished build.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-dev"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Developing</div>Pre-sort materials before the build begins — lay out what they'll need so they don't have to plan material selection and construction simultaneously. Provide step-by-step visual guides (LEGO instruction booklets are perfect training wheels for planning). After building, ask "What would you do differently next time?" — retrospective planning is easier than prospective planning and builds the same muscle.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>This child can hold a design in mind while building. Give verbal building instructions without visual reference: "Make a tower that's five blocks wide and ten blocks tall, with a door on the left side." Multi-step coding sequences (6-8 steps in Cubetto or Scratch Jr.) are manageable. Cooking recipes from memory after one read-through become accessible. Praise holding the plan in mind across steps, not just the final result.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>Keep the instructions visible throughout the build. A picture of the target, a step-by-step guide on the table, or a parent repeating the next step as needed. Don't test memory during creative tasks — the cognitive load of building plus remembering exceeds capacity. Coding sequences should be laid out physically (Cubetto blocks on the board) so the full program is visible, not held in mind.</div>

<h2 id="s-material-culture" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">handyman</span>Material Culture & Craft</h2>

<h3>Observe & Name</h3>
<p><strong>Monthly field trips to see how things are made.</strong> Bakery (watch bread being shaped and baked), cobbler or shoe repair shop, tailor or seamstress, ceramics workshop, woodworking shop, blacksmith if accessible. Madrid has all of these. The goal: see that things are made by people, using materials, with skill.</p>
<p><strong>Weekly object conversation.</strong> Pick any household object. "What is this made of? How do you think someone made it? What would happen if we broke it — could we fix it?" No lectures — questions. Follow their curiosity.</p>
<p><strong>Cooking progression.</strong> By age 7, they cook one simple meal per week with real knives (supervised), real heat, real ingredients. Not baking kits — actual food. Scrambled eggs. Pasta with sauce. A salad with dressing they mixed. Name every ingredient's origin: "These tomatoes grew in a garden. This olive oil came from pressing olives."</p>
<p><strong>Sewing basics.</strong> Thread a needle. Sew a button by age 7. Simple running stitch. Make a small pouch from fabric scraps. This is not gendered — it is material competence. Every human should know how to attach a button.</p>
<p><strong>Gardening.</strong> Grow one food plant from seed to plate per year. Lettuce, tomatoes, herbs. The full cycle: soil, seed, water, sun, wait, harvest, eat. This connects food to its source directly.</p>
<p><strong>Nature materials collection.</strong> Maintain a naturalist's collection box: rocks, shells, feathers, bark, seed pods, dried flowers. Name the materials. Compare textures, weights, colours. "This is granite — feel how heavy it is. This is pumice — feel how light. Both are stone. Why the difference?"</p>

<h3>Energy & Systems Awareness</h3>
<p>"Where does the light come from when you flip the switch?" "Where does the hot water come from?" "What makes the car go?" Not answers yet — questions. Let them wonder and speculate before explaining. The goal is curiosity about the invisible systems that surround them.</p>
<p><strong>Visits:</strong> Construction site (machines, materials, process — watch from a safe distance). Car wash (mechanical systems visible and fascinating). Fire station (how the truck works). Post office (where letters go). These are not field trips for entertainment — they are exposures to how the world functions.</p>

<h3>Philosophical Seed</h3>
<p><strong>"Things are made by people."</strong> Every manufactured object was designed and built by a human being (or a machine that a human built). This combats the magical-thinking consumer mindset where objects simply "exist in shops." When you see a chair, someone cut the wood, shaped the legs, joined them, sanded the surface, and finished it. Name this consistently.</p>
<p><strong>Wabi-sabi exposure.</strong> "This old wooden spoon has marks from years of cooking. That's what makes it beautiful." "This teddy bear is worn because you've loved it so much. The wear IS the value." Begin building comfort with imperfection, age, and use as forms of beauty.</p>
<p><strong>Stories:</strong> "Ox-Cart Man" (Donald Hall) — the cycle of making, selling, and starting over. A family makes everything they need, sells the surplus, and begins again. "Extra Yarn" (Mac Barnett) — generosity and craft. "The Lorax" (Dr. Seuss) — resource extraction and consequences.</p>

<h2 id="s-knowledge" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">lightbulb</span>Knowledge & Thinking</h2>

<h3>Ethics & Moral Reasoning</h3>
<p>Fairness is the entry point. "Is that fair? Why or why not?" These conversations arise naturally — sharing toys, game rules, sibling disputes. Use them. Simple dilemma thinking: "If you found a toy at the park, what should you do? What if no one was watching?" The goal is not correct answers but the habit of ethical reasoning. Ethics and history discussions can go deeper and longer than you expect at this age — extend the "is that fair?" questions into genuine back-and-forth Socratic dialogue. Introduce simple written reflection: "Draw or write what you think about this question."</p>

<h3>History</h3>
<p>History as story at this age. "Who built this castle? When? Why?" Historical picture books and timeline awareness — "a long, long time ago, before cars, before electricity." Usborne history encyclopedias for browsing. The goal is temporal awareness: things were different before, and specific people made specific choices that changed things.</p>

<h3>Nature & Ecology</h3>
<p>Nature journal: draw and describe one thing observed outdoors each week. Ecosystem questions: "What does the ant eat? What eats the ant? What would happen if there were no ants?" Seasonal observation across a full year — tracking the same tree or garden patch through all four seasons teaches systems thinking before you name it.</p>

<h3>Teaching & Explanation</h3>
<p>Not formal yet, but the instinct begins: "Can you show your little brother how to do that?" When they explain something, resist correcting — let them practice the act of explanation. By 6-7: "You learned how this works. Now explain it to me like I don't know anything." This is proto-Feynman technique.</p>

<h3>Cognitive Models</h3>
<p>Begin naming biases informally. When they make a snap judgment, gently surface it: "You decided that really fast — what if you thought about it a bit longer?" This plants the seed of System 1 vs. System 2 thinking without the terminology. The parent should read Kahneman's <em>Thinking, Fast and Slow</em> if not already done — understanding your own cognitive biases is prerequisite to helping them notice theirs.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>Knowledge enters through the hands and eyes for highly focused children. Nature journaling (draw the ant, draw the ecosystem) is a primary vehicle. History through building: construct a castle, a Roman road, a medieval market from blocks. Maps and timelines as physical objects they create, not pages they read. When they fixate on a knowledge domain — dinosaurs, space, ancient Egypt — go all in. University-level picture books, documentaries, museum deep dives. One topic explored to exhaustion teaches more about learning itself than ten topics surveyed.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>The DK Eyewitness series and Nat Geo Kids subscription are perfect — broad, visually rich, topic-hopping. Let them graze across history, science, and nature without forcing depth. The connections they make between disparate topics are their version of deep thinking.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>Ethical dilemmas and history questions can be made genuinely harder: "What should you do if your friend asks you to lie to help them?" Push for multi-step reasoning, not just a quick answer. Beast Academy's math challenges and nature journal predictions ("What will happen to this plant if we move it to shade?") train the hypothesis-test-reflect cycle early. Praise the reasoning chain, not the correct answer.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>Simplify ethical dilemmas to binary choices first ("Is it fair or unfair?") before adding nuance. In nature observation, pair the question with the observable answer: "What does the ant eat? Let's watch and find out." The gap between question and answer should be short enough to maintain engagement. Concrete, physical demonstrations before abstract reasoning — show, then discuss.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>Cross-domain thinking is accessible: "How is the ant colony like a family? How is it different?" These children can shift between perspectives (history character vs. modern child, villain vs. hero) without losing track of either. The "What if the opposite were true?" Inversion model lands quickly. Praise the willingness to entertain an uncomfortable perspective.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-dev"><div class="t-label">Shift: Developing</div>Stick with one perspective or topic per conversation rather than asking them to switch mid-discussion. If exploring "Why did the character do that?" — stay with that character for the full conversation before introducing another viewpoint. Transition between knowledge domains (from history to nature to ethics) needs a bridge: "Speaking of animals, in history people also used animals for..." rather than an abrupt topic change.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>The Socratic dinner conversations can become more complex: hold a question across multiple exchanges, remember what was said five minutes ago, and build on previous arguments. "You said earlier that the villain was wrong — but now you're saying his reason was good. Can both be true?" This metacognitive demand requires strong working memory. Praise holding and connecting ideas across a conversation.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>Keep Socratic questions short and self-contained — each question should be answerable from what's immediately visible or just discussed, not from something said ten minutes ago. Recap before asking a new question: "So we said the character wanted X. Now, why do you think he chose that?" The recap does the memory work so they can focus on the reasoning.</div>

<h2 id="s-culture" data-tier="recommended"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">travel_explore</span>Culture & Inner Life</h2>

<h3>Cultural Travel</h3>
<p>Europe by car and train — deliberate. Paris, Rome, London, Berlin, Amsterdam. One international trip per year minimum. Before the trip: "Where are we going? Show me on the map. What language do they speak? What do they eat?" After: "What was different from Madrid? What was the same?" These debrief questions build comparative cultural thinking.</p>
<p>First non-European destination by age 7 if possible — Morocco, Turkey, or similar. The cognitive shock of a genuinely different culture is worth more than ten European trips. Madrid itself is a cultural education: use it. Retiro, the Rastro, neighborhood diversity, the rhythm of a European capital.</p>

<h3>Meaning-Making & Spirituality</h3>
<p>Bible stories and Greek myths as narrative — the same way they consume Avatar. Noah's Ark, David and Goliath, Odysseus, Theseus and the Minotaur. These are humanity's oldest stories; treat them as rich narrative, not doctrine.</p>
<p>Questions about death, God, existence: engage seriously. "Some people believe X. Other people believe Y. What do you think?" Model intellectual humility about the biggest questions.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Cultural travel can overwhelm — new smells, crowds, unfamiliar food, language they don't understand. Prepare them in advance with pictures and stories. Build in quiet recovery time during trips. Existential questions ("Why do people die?") will arrive earlier and hit harder. Engage fully; don't deflect.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>One destination, explored thoroughly. A week in Rome visiting the same forum three times beats a whirlwind tour of four cities. Let them become the family expert on one culture's mythology, food, or history before moving to the next.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Maximize the number of distinct cultural exposures. Short visits to many places, tasting many cuisines, hearing many languages. The comparative instinct ("In Morocco they do X, but in London they do Y") is their natural mode of cultural learning.</div>

<h2 id="s-planning" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">calendar_month</span>Planning & Milestones</h2>

<h3>Institution Decisions</h3>
<p><strong>⚡ Primary school (age 4-5).</strong> Determines peer group for 6+ years. This compounds. Look for: academic rigor without crushing play, bilingual or English-medium, families who take something seriously. In Madrid: British Council, International College Spain, Hastings, King's College.</p>
<p><strong>⚡ Niche mastery teacher (age 4-5).</strong> The TEACHER matters more than the activity. Best available private instructor, 1:1. A practitioner who performs at a high level, not just teaches. This is the single highest-leverage resource investment at this age. No budget limit.</p>
<p><strong>⚡ Team sport club (age 6-8).</strong> Social calibration window. Coach who develops character, not just talent. Madrid: football junior leagues, basketball, rugby. The club culture becomes the child's social default.</p>

<h3>Summer Intensives</h3>
<p>Summer is for deep dives that don't fit the regular schedule. 2–3 week immersions in a domain they're already drawn to. These are not resumes — they're flow.</p>
<div class="card-grid">
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Nature Immersion Week</div><p>A week at a mountain campo or nature retreat. Daily hikes, outdoor cooking, bug observation, stream exploration. No tech except maybe camera for nature photography. The goal: genuine comfort with wild places.</p></div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Intensive Swim Camp</div><p>2 weeks focused on water competence. Beyond school swimming — real distance, open water, survival skills. Builds physical confidence and hardship tolerance together.</p></div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Art & Building Camp</div><p>A week of serious construction or art instruction. Woodworking intensive, pottery wheel, large-scale LEGO architecture, mural painting. Something they produce that they're proud of.</p></div>
</div>

<h3>Weekly Cadence</h3>
<div class="card-grid">
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Parent Evenings (2 hours)</div>
<p>Arrive → 15 min free play / connection → dinner (Socratic conversation 2–3x/week) → instrument practice (15–20 min, parent present) → reading aloud 30–45 min → bed.</p>
</div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Weekends</div>
<p>Saturday: Morning physical activity (hike, martial arts, cycling) → afternoon museum/cultural outing OR deep play at home → evening: family film night (<a href="#universe-avatar">Avatar</a> episode, <a href="#universe-ghibli">Ghibli</a>, chosen content).</p>
<p>Sunday: One structured activity (chess, music, sport) + extended unstructured time. Follow their lead for at least 2 hours — genuinely, not performatively.</p>
</div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Interna Weekdays</div>
<p>School. After school: outdoor play (1+ hour), snack, Spanish reading, practical life activity (cooking, cleaning with them), free play. Instrument practice if not done in evening. Key: protect unstructured outdoor time.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Cap structured social activities at one per day. Build the weekly cadence with generous solo time between group engagements. Weekend mornings should default to quiet — hike or reading, not playdates. The schedule itself is the intervention.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>They can handle a denser schedule than you'd expect. Back-to-back social activities on weekends are fine if they're energizing, not draining. Use their social drive to fill summer intensives with group-based options — camps, team sport clinics, group art workshops.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Transitions between activities are the friction point. Build buffer time between scheduled blocks — 15 minutes of nothing between martial arts and dinner, for example. Avoid scheduling high-stimulation activities (team sport, live performance) on the same day. Their nervous system needs recovery time you can't see.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Summer intensives should rotate domains year to year: nature camp one summer, art the next, swim the next. During the school year, semester-based commitments work better than year-long ones for elective activities. Keep one anchor commitment (instrument or martial art) constant while rotating the rest.</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-plan-adv"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Advanced</div>This child can help plan the weekly cadence itself: "What do you want to do this weekend? What order makes sense?" Introduce simple time awareness — "Practice takes 20 minutes, dinner takes 30 minutes, how long until we can play?" Cross-reference the age 8-10 phase's project planning and time management. Praise the act of thinking ahead, not just executing a given schedule.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-dev"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Developing</div>The weekly cadence should be externally managed and visually displayed — a picture schedule on the wall showing the day's sequence. Reduce decision points: "It's music time" rather than "What should we do now?" Routines are the scaffolding for planning; once the routine is automatic, they have cognitive space to plan variations. Don't expect them to anticipate tomorrow's needs today — that's an age 8-10 skill.</div>

<h1>Age 0–1: Neural Foundations</h1>
<p class="subtitle">The prefrontal cortex is offline. You are wiring the substrate on which everything else will be built.</p>

<div class="priority-box">
<h3>Priorities — Your 20 Hours/Week</h3>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">1</span><strong>Responsive caregiving.</strong> Respond promptly to distress. This does not spoil infants — the research is unambiguous. Reliability teaches "my actions produce results."</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">2</span><strong>Language saturation.</strong> Talk constantly. Narrate everything. Read aloud daily. English from you; Spanish from everyone else. Mirror their sounds, expand babbling into words, and maintain constant conversational exchange even before comprehension — quantity of spoken input is the single strongest predictor of language trajectory.</div>
<div class="priority-item"><span class="priority-num">3</span><strong>Sensory richness.</strong> High-contrast visuals, varied textures, music, outdoor exposure. The brain is building perceptual capacity. Emphasize physical manipulation: reaching, grasping, batting at mobiles, transferring objects hand to hand. More tummy time, more textured surfaces — infants learn through movement before language.</div>
</div>

<h2 id="s-dev"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">neurology</span>Developmental Reality</h2>
<p>You are not instilling virtues at this age. What you are doing is wiring the neural substrate. Secure attachment predicts more downstream outcomes than any activity or product.</p>
<p><strong>The three real levers:</strong> (1) Responsive caregiving → secure attachment → emotional regulation → all virtue development. (2) Language saturation → vocabulary → reading → academic outcomes. (3) Sensory richness → neural connectivity → perceptual and cognitive capacity.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Reduce sensory intensity. One stimulus at a time. Quieter environments. Slower transitions. Watch for overstimulation cues (gaze aversion, arching) and respond immediately.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>May need more intense stimulation to engage. Bolder contrasts, more physical play, varied textures. Don't mistake calm for contentment — they may simply need more input.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>After social exposure (visitors, outings), allow extended quiet one-on-one time to recover. Watch for withdrawal cues — turning away, fussing in groups — and don't push through them.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Seeks stimulation from faces and voices. More face-to-face interaction, more visitors, more outings. Solitary floor time may produce fussiness — stay nearby and narrate even when not directly engaging.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>When they fixate on a single object or stimulus, protect that attention ruthlessly. Don't rotate toys on a schedule — let them exhaust interest naturally. Sustained staring IS deep processing at this age.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>Rotate stimuli more frequently. If they lose interest quickly, that's not a deficit — it's a broader scanning pattern. Offer variety: different textures, sounds, and visual inputs across the day.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>If babbling emerges early (before 6 months) or first words appear before 10 months, increase language complexity — use fuller sentences, introduce less common vocabulary during narration. Pull conversational turn-taking games from the 1-3 phase. Praise the effort: "You're really trying to tell me something" rather than "you're so clever."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-dev"><div class="t-label">Communication: Developing</div>If vocalisation is limited or babbling is delayed, increase face-to-face vocal play — exaggerate mouth movements, use slow repetitive sounds, and reward any sound with immediate mirroring. Pair every object interaction with its name. More singing, more rhythmic speech. Consult paediatrician if no babbling by 9 months.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>If they demonstrate early object permanence (searching for hidden toys before 8 months) or rapid cause-and-effect understanding, introduce multi-step challenges: hide an object under one of two cloths, use containers that require a sequence to open. Praise the process: "You kept looking — you figured it out."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>Simplify cause-and-effect toys — use those with immediate, obvious feedback (push button → sound). For object permanence, start with partial hiding (object visibly sticking out) before full concealment. More repetition of the same puzzle rather than introducing new ones. Let them succeed often to build confidence.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-adv"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Advanced</div>If rolling, sitting, or crawling arrive early, provide more physical challenge: obstacle courses with cushions, inclined surfaces to crawl up, furniture arranged for safe pulling-to-stand. Don't restrict movement to "safe" flat surfaces — graduated challenge builds coordination. Praise effort: "You climbed all the way up" not "you're so strong."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-dev"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Developing</div>More tummy time with engaging toys placed just out of reach. Use rolled towels under the chest to support early tummy time if they resist it. Supported sitting practice with Boppy pillow. Don't skip stages — if not yet rolling, focus there before expecting sitting. Physical play on varied surfaces (grass, sand, carpet) builds different motor patterns.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>If pincer grip emerges early (before 9 months) or they transfer objects hand-to-hand ahead of schedule, introduce smaller objects (supervised), stacking rings, and containers to drop objects into. Pull simple shape-sorter play from the 1-3 phase. Praise precision: "You picked that up so carefully."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Offer larger, easier-to-grasp objects. Soft balls, thick rings, fabric toys with varied textures. Hand-over-hand guidance for reaching and grasping — place the object in their hand, then gradually increase distance. More batting-at-mobile play to build reaching accuracy before expecting grasping precision.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-adv"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Advanced</div>If social smile is early and robust, stranger awareness develops on schedule, and they actively seek interaction, increase social complexity — more varied faces (not just parents), mirror play, simple social games like peek-a-boo with variations. Praise social initiation: "You waved at her — she liked that."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-dev"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Developing</div>If social smile is delayed or they show limited interest in faces, increase face-to-face time with exaggerated expressions. Hold them at face-distance (20-30cm for newborns). More skin-to-skin contact. Slow, predictable social routines (same peek-a-boo sequence) before introducing variety. Consult paediatrician if no social smile by 3 months.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-adv"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Advanced</div>If they self-soothe earlier than expected (thumb-sucking, orienting to comfort objects) and recover from distress relatively quickly, you can begin slightly longer wait times before intervening — 15-20 seconds of fussing before pickup. This is not neglect; it's space for their emerging regulation. Narrate: "You calmed yourself down — that was hard work."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-dev"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Developing</div>If distress escalates rapidly and recovery is slow, respond faster — not slower. Shorten the lag between distress signal and co-regulation response. Consistent, immediate soothing builds the neural infrastructure for later self-regulation. Use the same calming sequence every time (pick up, hold close, slow voice, gentle rocking) so the routine itself becomes regulatory.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>Inhibitory control is barely emergent before 12 months — even advanced infants cannot truly inhibit. If they show early signs of pausing before grabbing (around 10-11 months), gently reinforce with "wait... okay, now take it." This plants the earliest seed. Praise the pause: "You waited."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-dev"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Developing</div>No meaningful inhibitory control is expected at this age. If they grab everything immediately with no pause, that is entirely normal. Do not attempt to train "waiting" before 12 months — the prefrontal circuitry is not yet wired. Focus instead on consistent, calm redirection when they reach for unsafe objects.</div>

<h2 id="s-communication" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">record_voice_over</span>Communication & Expression</h2>

<h3>Language — Written & Oral</h3>
<p>OPOL active: you speak English exclusively. All books you read are English. The interna reads Spanish books during the day. Singing in both languages. No correction — just immersion.</p>
<p><strong>L3 note:</strong> L3 exposure begins at age 6-8. At this stage, focus on deep L1+L2 bilingual foundation. See Philosophy tab for multilingual architecture.</p>
<p><strong>⚡ Window alert:</strong> Native accent acquisition narrows after age 7-10. The OPOL foundation built now compounds every year. This is not a nice-to-have — it's a closing window.</p>
<p>Pre-writing begins with grasping. Fat crayons, chunky markers on large paper. The motor patterns that will become handwriting start here — the pincer grip, the controlled movement, the cause-and-effect of "I move my hand, a mark appears." No letters, no instruction. Just access to marking tools and surfaces.</p>
<p>Oral expression at this stage is entirely receptive and imitative. Read aloud with expression — vary your pitch, pace, and volume. The infant is absorbing prosody (the music of speech) before they understand words. Narrate with theatrical energy when appropriate: "The BALL went BOUNCING down the HILL!" This is not baby talk — it's vocal modeling. They are learning that the voice is an instrument with range. Read more, narrate more, sing more — reward every sound with a responsive sound. Pair language with physical action: name objects as they grasp them, narrate movement as it happens. "You're reaching for the ball" binds word to sensation. Pointing games and touch-the-picture books connect physical exploration to language.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>If early vocalisations are varied and frequent, lean into conversational turn-taking — pause after you speak and wait for their response sound. Introduce more complex sentence structures in your narration and begin simple "choice" prompts ("Do you want the ball?") even before they can answer. Pull interactive book reading (pointing, naming) from the 1-3 phase. Praise engagement: "You're telling me all about it."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-dev"><div class="t-label">Communication: Developing</div>Slow down your speech. Use shorter phrases with more repetition. Exaggerate intonation — the sing-song quality of parentese is not condescension, it's acoustic highlighting that helps the developing auditory system parse speech boundaries. More tactile pairing: touch the object while naming it. More rhyme and rhythm in your narration. If no babbling by 9 months, discuss with paediatrician.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>If they grip crayons or markers with early control, provide varied surfaces — large paper taped to the floor, chalk on outdoor surfaces. The motor patterns for pre-writing are building ahead of schedule. Let them scribble freely without guidance on "what to draw." Praise the act: "You made marks on the paper — look at that."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Use thicker, softer marking tools — jumbo crayons or finger paints instead of markers. Guide their hand gently over the surface to demonstrate that movement creates marks. Vertical surfaces (paper on the wall) can be easier than horizontal for early mark-making. Don't rush toward precision — the experience of cause-and-effect (hand moves → mark appears) is the lesson.</div>

<h2 id="s-music" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">music_note</span>Music</h2>
<p>Ambient exposure: play diverse genres throughout the day. Classical, jazz, bossa nova, flamenco — variety of rhythm, timbre, harmony. Sing to them. Rhythm shakers from 6 months. The brain is building the auditory architecture that all later music learning will use.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Keep volume low. Sudden percussion or brass may trigger distress. Start with solo instruments — solo piano, solo guitar, solo cello — before introducing full orchestral or rhythmically complex pieces. Watch for startle responses and adjust.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>They may not visibly react to ambient music. Move to more rhythmic, percussive genres. Hold them and sway or bounce to the beat — they need the physical sensation paired with the sound to register it fully.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>If they vocalise along with music early — humming, babbling rhythmically — sing more call-and-response songs and leave deliberate gaps for them to fill. Musical interaction is a language precursor. Praise the attempt: "You sang with me."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>If they grip and shake a rattle with intentional rhythm before 6 months, introduce egg shakers and small maracas. Holding an instrument and producing sound on purpose is a fine motor milestone. Praise the action: "You're shaking to the music."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Use wrist rattles or ankle bells that produce sound from movement without requiring a grip. This lets them experience "I move → sound happens" before they can hold an instrument deliberately. Graduate to hand-held shakers only when grasping is consistent.</div>

<h2 id="s-mind-body" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">fitness_center</span>Mind & Body</h2>

<h3>Physical System</h3>
<p>Tummy time, floor freedom, outdoor exposure to weather variation. Daily outdoor blanket time regardless of weather.</p>

<h3>Dance & Movement Seed</h3>
<p><strong>Dance with them.</strong> Hold, bounce to the beat, sway together. They feel rhythm through your body before they hear it independently. Play diverse rhythms throughout the day — not just 4/4: Afro-Cuban, Brazilian, flamenco compás, waltz, reggae. The auditory-motor coupling that makes rhythm feel natural is being wired now (Zentner & Eerola, 2010 — infants as young as 5 months show rhythmic movement responses to music). No instruction needed — this is entirely parent-guided. The gymnastics substrate is already here: tummy time, floor freedom, reaching, rolling. These ARE the prerequisites for later tumbling.</p>

<h3>Self-Awareness & Emotional Intelligence</h3>
<p>Co-regulation is the entire curriculum. The infant cannot regulate alone — they borrow your nervous system. When they cry, you remain calm. When they're distressed, you name it: "You're frustrated. That's okay. I'm here." This is not sentiment — it's the neurobiological foundation of self-regulation. Children who experience consistent co-regulation develop stronger prefrontal-amygdala connectivity. The parent's emotional state IS the child's regulation tool.</p>
<p><strong>Kindness modeling begins here.</strong> The parent's emotional tone IS the child's first experience of kindness. Responding to distress with warmth, not irritation. Narrating gently, not correcting harshly. The child's first model of "how people treat each other" is you.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Co-regulation takes longer and requires more patience. They feel everything at higher amplitude. Your calm must be deeper and more sustained. Physical contact (skin-to-skin, gentle rocking) is often more effective than verbal soothing for highly sensitive infants.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-low-react"><div class="t-label">Low Reactivity</div>Emotional cues may be subtle — don't assume absence of distress. They may not cry loudly but still need co-regulation. Watch for quieter signals: stillness, gaze avoidance, subtle body tension. Proactively check in rather than waiting for obvious distress.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-adv"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Advanced</div>If they hit motor milestones early (rolling before 4 months, sitting before 6, crawling before 8), expand the physical environment — more floor space, safe climbing surfaces, outdoor time on uneven terrain. Early walkers need shoes later, not sooner — barefoot on varied surfaces builds proprioception. Praise effort: "You pulled yourself up — that took work."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-gm-dev"><div class="t-label">Gross Motor: Developing</div>More supported tummy time with incentive objects placed just ahead. Use a mirror at floor level during tummy time — their own face is the most motivating visual. Gentle exercises: bicycle legs, supported standing, assisted rolling. Avoid walkers and bouncers that skip developmental stages. Patience — motor development has wide normal variation and is not a race.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-adv"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Advanced</div>If they demonstrate early self-soothing (finding their thumb, orienting to a comfort object, calming without pickup), support this emerging capacity — place the comfort object within reach, give a brief pause before responding to mild fussing. They are building the earliest self-regulation circuitry. Narrate: "You found your bear and felt better."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-emoctl-dev"><div class="t-label">Emotional Control: Developing</div>If emotional escalation is rapid and intense, prioritise speed and consistency in co-regulation. Establish one reliable calming routine (e.g., pick up → chest-to-chest → slow rocking → low humming) and use it identically every time. Predictability in your response is what builds their eventual ability to regulate. Do not wait for self-soothing that isn't yet available — meet them where they are.</div>

<h2 id="s-social" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">people</span>Social & Relational</h2>

<h3>Social Intelligence</h3>
<p>Attachment is the foundation. Secure attachment (responsive, consistent caregiving) produces children who later navigate social complexity with confidence. This is not a "parenting style" — it's the empirically validated prerequisite for all subsequent social development. Respond to bids for attention. Be predictable. Be present.</p>

<h3>Social World & Network</h3>
<p>The network IS your network at this age. Cultivate 3–4 families with children of similar age whose values align with yours. The interna's park playgroup matters — who are those children and families? Parent-and-baby groups in Madrid: look at international community meetups (British Council, InterNations) for English-speaking peer exposure.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Smaller gatherings — one or two families, not large groups. Watch for overstimulation in social settings: fussing, clinging, gaze aversion. Leave before meltdown, not during. Quality of social contact matters more than quantity.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Seek more frequent social exposure. They light up around other faces and voices — lean into that. Busier environments (cafes, markets, parks) are stimulating rather than overwhelming. The interna should prioritize social outings.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-adv"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Advanced</div>If they show early social referencing (looking to you for cues in ambiguous situations), joint attention (following your gaze or point), or initiate social games, increase interactive complexity — simple turn-taking games, waving bye-bye, clapping games with a partner. More varied social partners beyond primary caregivers. Praise social initiation: "You reached for her — you wanted to play."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-social-dev"><div class="t-label">Personal-Social: Developing</div>If social engagement is limited — infrequent eye contact, no social smile by 3 months, or no stranger awareness by 8 months — increase face-to-face interaction time with primary caregivers before expanding the social circle. Use exaggerated facial expressions and slow, predictable interaction routines. Consult paediatrician if milestones are significantly delayed.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>In social settings, an early communicator can be the bridge — narrate what the other baby is doing: "She's reaching for the ball too. You could give it to her." This pairs social awareness with language, building the verbal-social connection early. Praise the narration attempt: "You told her about the toy."</div>

<h2 id="s-building" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">construction</span>Building & Making</h2>
<p>No screens. Sensory play is the building. None of the digital media or structured making tools apply yet — the infant's entire "building" curriculum is tactile exploration, grasping, and cause-and-effect discovery through physical objects.</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Introduce new textures one at a time. Wet, sticky, or gritty sensory play (finger paint, sand, food play) may trigger aversion. Start with dry, smooth textures and graduate slowly. Never force contact — offer and wait.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-adv"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Advanced</div>If grasping and transferring are advanced, introduce containers with lids, simple nesting cups, and objects that require rotation or twisting to manipulate. The progression from batting → grasping → transferring → placing builds toward intentional construction. Praise precision: "You put the cup inside the other cup — you looked carefully."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-fm-dev"><div class="t-label">Fine Motor: Developing</div>Stick with large, easy-to-grasp objects. Soft blocks they can hold with a full-hand grip. Sensory play that doesn't require pincer precision — scooping sand, squeezing soft toys, batting at hanging objects. The progression is reach → contact → grasp → hold. Don't skip to placing or stacking before grasping is reliable.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>If they show early cause-and-effect mastery (deliberately dropping objects to watch them fall, pulling a cloth to retrieve a toy), increase complexity — hide objects under two covers and let them search, or use toys that require a two-step action to activate. Praise persistence: "You tried both — you kept going."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>Simplify to single-step cause-and-effect: push button → light, shake rattle → sound. Use toys with immediate, satisfying feedback. Repeat the same toy interaction many times before introducing variation — mastery of one simple mechanism builds the template for all future problem solving.</div>

<h2 id="s-material-culture" data-tier="recommended"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">handyman</span>Material Culture & Craft</h2>
<p><strong>Sensory substrate.</strong> At this age, material culture is sensory exploration. Include natural materials in play: untreated wood blocks, metal spoons, wool fabric, cotton, ceramic bowls (supervised). Plastic is convenient; real materials teach different weights, temperatures, and textures. This is the substrate for all later material literacy — the child who has felt the difference between wood and metal at 6 months recognises it instinctively at 6 years.</p>
<p><strong>Parent behaviour:</strong> Model appreciation for objects. "This wooden bowl is 20 years old and still beautiful." The child absorbs tone, not content.</p>

<h2 id="s-knowledge" data-tier="core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">lightbulb</span>Knowledge & Thinking</h2>

<h3>Nature & Ecology</h3>
<p>Outdoor time as default, not special occasion. Daily exposure to natural light, wind, temperature variation, natural sounds. Name what you see: trees, birds, rain, clouds. The sensory richness of outdoor environments is qualitatively different from indoor spaces — more variable, less predictable, more stimulating to developing neural systems.</p>


<div class="t-adjust t-ps-adv"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Advanced</div>If they demonstrate early curiosity about how things work — turning objects over, examining from multiple angles, systematically exploring features — provide more complex objects to inspect. Mechanical toys with moving parts, textured balls with different surfaces, objects that open and close. The investigative behaviour itself is the cognitive milestone. Praise the process: "You're looking at every side of it."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ps-dev"><div class="t-label">Problem Solving: Developing</div>Simplify the environment. Fewer objects, more time with each. Draw their attention to interesting features — tap the object, turn it slowly, demonstrate one action and wait. Guided discovery: show them one property of an object, then let them find others. Patience and repetition matter more than variety at this stage.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-comm-adv"><div class="t-label">Communication: Advanced</div>If they respond to names of objects (looking when you say "Where's the ball?"), accelerate labelling. Name plants, animals, weather phenomena during outdoor time with more specificity: "That's a pigeon — see its grey feathers?" Early receptive vocabulary is the scaffold for later knowledge acquisition. Praise attention: "You looked right at it when I said its name."</div>

<h2 id="s-culture" data-tier="recommended"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">travel_explore</span>Culture & Inner Life</h2>

<h3>Cultural Exposure</h3>
<p>Travel is not yet educational — it's environmental. But the habits start now: children who travel early normalize novelty. Short trips within Spain: coast, mountains, other cities. The point is sensory range, not cultural education.</p>

<h3>Wonder & Awe</h3>
<p>At this age, meaning-making is pre-verbal: wonder and awe. The infant staring at light through leaves, mesmerized by rain, transfixed by a candle flame — this is the first encounter with the numinous. Don't rush past these moments. Sit in them. The capacity for wonder is the substrate on which all later spiritual and philosophical development is built. This is Layer 0 of the meaning-making progression (see Philosophy tab).</p>

<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Highly sensitive infants experience wonder more intensely — and overwhelm more quickly. Short museum visits (20 minutes max), smaller spaces, natural environments over built ones. They will absorb more from a quiet garden than a busy market.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-high"><div class="t-label">High Self-Regulation</div>When they fixate on something — leaves, water, a pattern on a wall — stay with it. Don't redirect to the "next thing." These extended moments of absorption are the earliest expression of depth orientation. Time in one place beats variety of places.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-ec-low"><div class="t-label">Low Self-Regulation</div>More variety in outings: different parks, different neighborhoods, different sensory environments. They scan broadly and lose interest in familiar settings faster. Short trips to many places over long stays in one.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>Working memory is barely functional at this age, but if they demonstrate early anticipation — looking toward the door when they hear footsteps, reaching for a hidden toy they saw placed — they are holding information briefly. Reinforce by playing simple anticipation games: "Where did the ball go?" with a short delay before revealing. Praise the search: "You remembered where it went."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-adv"><div class="t-label">Shift: Advanced</div>Cognitive flexibility is minimal before 12 months. If they adapt quickly to changed routines (new bath time, different caregiver) without prolonged distress, their shift capacity is developing well. Gently introduce minor routine variations — a different park, a new song at bedtime — and praise the adaptation: "New place, and you're exploring already."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-shift-dev"><div class="t-label">Shift: Developing</div>If any change in routine or environment causes prolonged distress, keep routines maximally consistent. When changes are necessary, provide extra transition support — bring familiar objects, maintain the same caregiver, keep the sensory profile similar. Flexibility will develop, but it requires a secure, predictable base first.</div>

<h2 id="s-planning" data-tier="foundational"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">calendar_month</span>Planning & Milestones</h2>

<h3>Weekly Cadence</h3>
<div class="card-grid">
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Parent Layer (Evenings + Weekends)</div>
<p>Evening: bath → reading aloud (English) → bedtime routine. Same sequence every night — predictability IS the lesson.</p>
<p>Weekend: outdoor time together (parks, walks), sensory play, floor time with full attention. No phones during floor time.</p>
</div>
<div class="card"><div class="card-title">Interna Layer (Weekdays)</div>
<p>Morning outdoor time regardless of weather. Tummy time / floor play. Spanish reading. Nap architecture (consistent times, dark room, white noise). Afternoon sensory variety. Simple: "Let them explore. Don't rescue too quickly. Go outside every day. Read these books."</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="t-adjust t-introvert"><div class="t-label">Introvert</div>Bias the weekly cadence toward smaller, quieter activities. Fewer outings with more recovery time between. The interna should limit group activities to 2-3 per week max, with quiet solo play filling the gaps.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-extrovert"><div class="t-label">Extrovert</div>Pack the social calendar. Daily outings with other children, more park time, more visitors. An extroverted infant left alone too long becomes irritable — they need faces and voices as fuel.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-high-react"><div class="t-label">High Reactivity</div>Build longer transition buffers into every schedule change. Nap times, outings, bath time — all need gradual wind-down. Predictability matters even more: any deviation from routine can dysregulate for hours. Brief the interna explicitly on signals and pacing.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-plan-adv"><div class="t-label">Plan/Organize: Advanced</div>Plan/Organize is not assessable at this age — the prefrontal circuitry required is not yet online. No action needed. The only relevant precursor is routine predictability, which you are already providing through consistent daily sequences.</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-inhib-adv"><div class="t-label">Inhibit: Advanced</div>If by 10-12 months they show any capacity to pause before reaching (even briefly), reinforce with gentle "wait... now" games during feeding or toy play. This is the barest seed of inhibitory control. Keep it playful and brief — seconds, not minutes. Praise the pause: "You waited."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-adv"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Advanced</div>If they remember the location of a hidden toy after a brief delay, begin simple "find it" games with 2-3 second gaps between hiding and seeking. Multi-location hide-and-seek (two possible hiding spots) stretches emerging working memory. Praise remembering: "You knew where it was — you held it in your mind."</div>
<div class="t-adjust t-wm-dev"><div class="t-label">Working Memory: Developing</div>If they don't search for hidden objects or forget immediately, shorten the delay — hide while they're watching and let them retrieve instantly. The progression is: see it hidden → retrieve immediately → retrieve after 1 second → retrieve after 3 seconds. Don't rush the delay extension. Consistency builds the memory circuit.</div>
</div>

<h3>Content Anchors</h3>
<p><strong>Anchor:</strong> Richard Scarry's "Busy, Busy World" and "What Do People Do All Day?" — systems, categorization, scale. Every page is a dense information environment.</p>
<p><strong>Read aloud:</strong> Anything with rhythm and repetition. The content matters less than the vocal patterns, prosody, and the ritual of reading together.</p>

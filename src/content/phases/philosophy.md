<h1>Philosophy</h1>
<p class="subtitle">Depth, CRAFT, and the architecture of this framework.</p>

<h2 id="s-core"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">star</span>Core Principle: Depth Over Breadth</h2>
<p>The central bet of this framework: studying fewer things deeply produces better thinkers than studying many things superficially. A child who has truly understood one complex system — disassembled it, questioned its assumptions, traced its creation — can transfer that analytical capacity to anything. A child who has been "exposed to" fifty topics has acquired impressions, not understanding.</p>
<p>This is counter to most educational philosophy, which optimizes for coverage. Coverage produces the ability to recognize topics. Depth produces the ability to reason about them. These are not the same thing.</p>

<h2 id="s-craft"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">visibility</span>The CRAFT Method</h2>
<p>CRAFT is the analytical lens applied to every anchor work, adapted by developmental stage.</p>
<table>
<tr><th>Letter</th><th>Lens</th><th>Core Question</th></tr>
<tr><td>C — Creation</td><td>Who made this? Under what constraints? What were they trying to do?</td><td>Every work is a human product with context.</td></tr>
<tr><td>R — Reasoning</td><td>What arguments does the work make? What assumptions does it rest on?</td><td>Every work embeds a worldview.</td></tr>
<tr><td>A — Architecture</td><td>How is it structured? Why these choices and not others?</td><td>Structure is a decision, not an accident.</td></tr>
<tr><td>F — Function</td><td>What does this work do to the reader/viewer? What effect was intended?</td><td>Works act on audiences; understand how.</td></tr>
<tr><td>T — Trace</td><td>What influenced this? What did it influence? Where does it sit in the network?</td><td>Nothing exists in isolation.</td></tr>
</table>

<h3>CRAFT by Age</h3>
<p><strong>Ages 0–3:</strong> Pre-CRAFT. The seed is pointing at things and narrating: "Look — someone drew all of these people. Every single one." This builds the C instinct: things are made by people.</p>
<p><strong>Ages 4–7:</strong> Simplified CRAFT. Focus on F (Function) and C (Creation). "What is this story trying to show you?" "Who made this and why?" Don't name the method — just ask the questions naturally.</p>
<p><strong>Ages 8–10:</strong> Name CRAFT explicitly. Walk through all five lenses with anchor works. Start with the easiest (F — "how did that scene make you feel and why?") and build to the hardest (T — "what influenced this creator?").</p>
<p><strong>Ages 11+:</strong> Full CRAFT as an autonomous analytical habit. The goal is that they apply it unprompted.</p>

<h2 id="s-models-overview"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">pattern</span>Mental Models — Overview</h2>
<p>Mental models are cognitive frameworks — simplified representations of how something works — that allow reasoning about reality without processing everything from scratch. The goal is Munger's latticework: models from multiple disciplines (physics, biology, psychology, economics, history, information theory, game theory, complexity science) that reinforce each other and reveal cross-domain patterns.</p>
<p>The developmental constraint is real. Abstract models require abstract reasoning, which requires prefrontal development not complete until mid-20s. Early models are concrete and experiential; abstract and probabilistic models come later.</p>
<p>See each age tab for the specific models installed at that stage.</p>

<h2 id="s-language"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">public</span>Language Strategy — OPOL in Madrid</h2>
<p><strong>Configuration:</strong> Father speaks English exclusively. Mother, interna, school, social world: Spanish. School has some English instruction.</p>
<p><strong>Principle:</strong> English is the minority language and must be the language of fun, emotional intimacy, and intellectual adventure — not instruction or correction. The content curriculum being English-dominant is a major asset: Avatar, Calvin & Hobbes, Roald Dahl, Harry Potter will all be consumed in English first.</p>
<p><strong>When they reply in Spanish (age 4–5 onset):</strong> Don't correct. Continue in English. Forced production backfires; continued exposure works. The research is unambiguous.</p>
<p><strong>Spanish content track:</strong> Mafalda (ages 7+), Borges (ages 16+), Spanish-language films and music. The interna should have a reading list for daytime Spanish input. See each age tab for specifics.</p>

<h2 id="s-music-phil"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">music_note</span>Music — Dual-Track Philosophy</h2>
<p>Musical instrument mastery is elevated to a first-class concern in this framework, not a supplementary activity. The goal: a child who can both read a classical score with precision AND improvise over jazz changes with expression. These are distinct but complementary cognitive skills — classical develops discipline, reading, structure, and interpretive depth; jazz develops ear, improvisation, real-time composition, and comfort with ambiguity.</p>
<p><strong>Both tracks from the beginning.</strong> Most music education splits these artificially. Classical students can't improvise; jazz students can't sight-read. This framework trains both simultaneously.</p>

<h2 id="s-language-phil"><span class="material-symbols-rounded">record_voice_over</span>Language — Dual-Track Philosophy</h2>
<p>Written expression receives outsized attention in most educational systems. Oral expression — the ability to speak with precision, hold a room, think aloud under pressure, perform a text, improvise an argument — is treated as a personality trait rather than a trainable skill. This is wrong. Oral command is a skill, built through the same deliberate practice as writing.</p>
<p><strong>Both tracks from the beginning.</strong> Writing develops precision, revision, and the ability to think in structured paragraphs. Speaking develops presence, improvisation, and the ability to think in real time. A child trained only in writing freezes at a podium. A child trained only in speaking cannot sustain a complex argument past two paragraphs. This framework trains both simultaneously, the same way it trains classical reading and jazz improvisation in music.</p>
<p>The training vehicles are concrete and every one serves a stated purpose: storytelling and show-and-tell (structuring your own thoughts for an audience), debate and argumentation (thinking adversarially under time pressure), theater and improv (inhabiting perspectives, reading audiences, responding in real time), and rhetoric as a study discipline (understanding why persuasion works). No exercise exists for tradition's sake — if it doesn't build a transferable skill, it doesn't belong.</p>

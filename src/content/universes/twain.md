He chooses damnation over complicity in a system he's been taught is righteous. A Connecticut Yankee applies Twain's satire to technology and progress. His essays are among the sharpest social criticism in English."

  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Huckleberry Finn"
    description: "Huck and Jim on the raft. The novel builds to the most important moral decision in American fiction: Huck has been taught that helping a slave escape is a sin. He writes a letter to turn Jim in, feels 'good and all washed clean of sin for the first time.' Then he thinks about Jim as a person — their friendship, Jim's humanity — tears up the letter, and says: 'All right, then, I'll go to hell.' He chooses damnation over complicity. This is moral courage: doing right when everything you've been taught says it's wrong. Discuss: when have systems told you something was right that you knew was wrong?"
    mode: dim
    goals:

      - "Understand that 'doing the right thing' sometimes means defying every authority you've been taught to respect."
    cautions: "Contains period-accurate racial language. This is not a reason to avoid it — it is a reason to discuss it. The language is part of the moral argument: Twain uses the vocabulary of slavery to expose its cruelty."

Twain's essays are among the sharpest social criticism in English."

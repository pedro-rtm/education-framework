summary: "The political economy of technology — who builds it, who benefits, who is harmed, and whether technological progress is unconditionally good."
whyItBelongs: "The framework has Coding (practical) and AI (technical) but nobody teaching the political economy of technology. How do technologies reshape power? Who decides which technologies are built? Is the precautionary principle wisdom or cowardice? Is technological acceleration unconditionally good? The techno-optimist position (Andreessen, Ridley, Deutsch) and the techno-critic position (Ellul, Postman, Zuboff) are taught as positions in a debate, not as curriculum to absorb. Includes the transhumanism debate: CRISPR, brain-computer interfaces, life extension — the framework names its own humanist position (effort-based, embodied) so the child can evaluate it rather than absorb it unconsciously."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Do artifacts have politics?"
    description: "Winner's 'Do Artifacts Have Politics?' — the essay that asks whether technologies are neutral tools or carry political values in their design. Robert Moses' low bridges over Long Island parkways (too low for buses, keeping poor New Yorkers off the beaches) as the canonical example. Postman's Amusing Ourselves to Death: the argument that television (now social media) reshapes public discourse from argument to entertainment. Applied: 'Who designed your phone's notification system? What were they optimising for? Your wellbeing or their revenue?'"
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Understand that technologies are not neutral — they carry political values in their design."
      - "Identify who benefits and who is harmed by specific technological design choices."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: core
    title: "Technopoly, Superintelligence, and the optimist case"
    description: "Postman's Technopoly: every technology is a Faustian bargain — it gives something and takes something. The question is whether we choose consciously. Bostrom's Superintelligence: what happens when AI exceeds human intelligence? The control problem, instrumental convergence, existential risk. Deutsch's The Beginning of Infinity: the optimist counter — problems are soluble, knowledge grows without limit, pessimism is a failure of imagination. Harari's Homo Deus: the transhumanist future narrative. Sandel's The Case Against Perfection: what's lost when we engineer away struggle? The student should be able to articulate the optimist AND critic positions with evidence."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Articulate the techno-optimist case (Deutsch, Ridley) and the techno-critic case (Postman, Ellul) with equal depth."
      - "Engage with Bostrom's control problem as a genuine existential question, not science fiction."
      - "Evaluate Sandel's question: what is lost when technology removes struggle from human development?"
    projects:
      - "Choose one technology you use daily. Analyse it as a Faustian bargain: what does it give you? What does it take? Who decided the terms?"
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: core
    title: "Surveillance capitalism, transhumanism, and the framework's own position"
    description: "Zuboff's Surveillance Capitalism: tech companies extract behavioral data to predict and modify human behaviour. This is not a conspiracy theory — it is a business model described in SEC filings. Ellul's The Technological Society: the most radical critique — technology is an autonomous system that humans serve rather than control. Ridley's The Rational Optimist and the a16z Techno-Optimist Manifesto: the case that technology solves more problems than it creates and that regulation is net negative. Balaji's The Network State: digital communities as alternative political structures. The transhumanism debate in full: Kurzweil (Singularity Is Near), Fukuyama (Our Posthuman Future). The framework names its own position — humanist, effort-based, embodied — so the student can evaluate it rather than absorb it unconsciously. 'This framework believes that 14 years of gymnastics builds a person who can do hard things. A transhumanist would say that's absurd if technology can give you the capability without the effort. Where do you stand, and why?'"
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Understand surveillance capitalism as a business model, not a conspiracy — and evaluate its implications."
      - "Engage with the transhumanism debate from a position of knowledge, not instinct."
      - "Articulate and defend a personal position on technology's role in human flourishing — grounded in arguments, not vibes."

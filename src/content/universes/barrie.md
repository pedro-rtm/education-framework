summary: "The boy who wouldn't grow up — a text far darker and more ambivalent than its Disney afterlife suggests, and therefore a curriculum piece about the choice to mature."
whyItBelongs: "Peter Pan is a fictional universe that has been sanitised so completely by Disney that the original text surprises adults who return to it. Barrie's Peter is not a straightforward hero. He is beautiful, graceful, and heartless — the text uses that word repeatedly. He cannot remember events from one adventure to the next. He forgets Tinker Bell. Eventually he forgets Wendy. The refusal to grow up is staged by the book as a kind of death-in-amber: you preserve yourself by refusing to change, and what you preserve is a self that cannot love anyone for long. For a curriculum teaching agency and the choice to mature, Peter Pan is a rare asset — a children's book that takes the question seriously. Bonus structure: Peter and Hook are complementary-opposites. Hook is trapped in adult vanity; Peter is trapped in eternal youth. Both are stuck. The only characters who move through time are the Darlings."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-4-7
    tier: recommended
    title: "Peter Pan (read-aloud, selected)"
    description: "Read selected passages aloud — the flight to Neverland, the Lost Boys, the fight with Hook. At this age the fairytale surface is the point: flight, pirates, mermaids, a crocodile with a clock inside. Barrie's narrator voice is unusually close to the reader — he asks questions, teases, interrupts. That voice is a gift for read-aloud. Save the darker thematic layer for age 8-10."
    mode: absorb
    goals:
      - "Experience Neverland as an imagined geography — second star to the right, straight on till morning."
      - "Notice Barrie's narrator voice — he speaks directly to the reader in a way few children's books do."
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: core
    title: "Peter and Wendy (independent reading)"
    description: "The full novel, under the original title. Now the darker layer becomes visible. Peter forgets Tinker Bell. Peter forgets the Lost Boys. By the epilogue he forgets Wendy, then Wendy's daughter Jane, then Jane's daughter Margaret — each generation of Darling girls flies with Peter and is left behind. The book ends with the line: 'and thus it will go on, so long as children are gay and innocent and heartless.' Discuss: is Peter happy? Is the refusal to grow up a triumph or a trap? Barrie does not resolve it. The ambiguity is the point."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Read Peter and Hook as two forms of being stuck — eternal boy and eternal adult, both unable to grow."
      - "Evaluate the book's final word: 'heartless.' Barrie uses it without irony. What does he mean?"
    cautions: "The final chapters are melancholy. Peter's forgetting is genuinely sad. This is a book about the cost of refusing to change; some children feel that cost strongly. Read in conversation."

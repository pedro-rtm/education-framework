label: Poetry
kind: literary-canon
summary: "Language at its most compressed, precise, and musical. The art form where every word must earn its place."
whyItBelongs: "Poetry is the highest-leverage communication mastery content in the framework. A person who reads and writes poetry develops: precision with language (every word matters), musicality (rhythm, meter, sound), emotional compression (saying in 14 lines what prose needs 14 pages for), metaphorical thinking (seeing one thing through another), and memorability (poetry is designed to be remembered). The framework emphasises Communication Mastery (Target 6) across written, oral, and performative media — poetry is where all three converge. A person who can quote Ozymandias, recite Neruda in Spanish, and write a sonnet has a relationship with language that prose alone cannot build."
tags: [books, english, spanish, classics, philosophy]
languages: [english, spanish]
targets: [communication-mastery, creative-production, self-awareness, compassion-kindness, deep-curiosity]
models: [via-negativa, complementary-opposites, maps-not-territory]
intensity: medium
socialValue: bridge
phases:
  - phaseId: age-4-7
    tier: core
    title: "Poetry as Sound and Play"
    description: "Shel Silverstein (Where the Sidewalk Ends, A Light in the Attic): funny, rhythmic, subversive. A.A. Milne (When We Were Very Young, Now We Are Six): melody and whimsy. Dr. Seuss as poet: the rhythm IS the content. Spanish: Gloria Fuertes (the essential Spanish children's poet). Read aloud together — poetry is an oral art first. Memorise short favourites naturally through repetition, not drilling. The goal: poetry = pleasure. Not 'what does the poet mean?' — just 'listen to how that sounds.'"
    mode: absorb
    goals:
      - "Experience poetry as musical, funny, pleasurable."
      - "Memorise a few short poems naturally through repetition."
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: core
    title: "Poetry as Craft"
    description: "Begin noticing HOW poems work: rhyme, meter, repetition, line breaks. Robert Louis Stevenson (A Child's Garden of Verses). Rudyard Kipling's 'If' — introduce but don't force. Lewis Carroll (Jabberwocky): language can mean something even when the words are invented. Spanish: Antonio Machado (selected, simplified). Write their own: acrostics, haiku, limericks, free verse. The rule: write a poem a week for one month. Not good poems — just poems. Quantity before quality. 'Via Negativa applied to language: what can you remove and still say the same thing?'"
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Understand rhyme, meter, and structure as tools, not rules."
      - "Write poems regularly — fluency before quality."
    projects:
      - "Write one poem per week for a month. Any form. Read the best one aloud to the family."
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Poetry as Power"
    description: "Poems that hit hard at this age: Shelley's 'Ozymandias' (power and impermanence — 14 lines that outlasted an empire). Kipling's 'If' (revisited now with more life experience). Tennyson's 'Ulysses' ('To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield'). Maya Angelou's 'Still I Rise.' Langston Hughes' 'Harlem.' Spanish: Federico Garcia Lorca (Romance Sonambulo, selected poems — already referenced in resources). Pablo Neruda (Veinte poemas de amor — selected, age-appropriate). Spoken word and slam poetry: poetry is alive, not a museum piece. Attend a poetry slam if accessible in Madrid."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Memorise 3-5 poems that resonate personally."
      - "Understand that poetry is compressed argument — Ozymandias makes a philosophical claim in 14 lines."
      - "Begin writing poetry that says something real, not just sounds nice."
    projects:
      - "Memorise and perform one poem to an audience."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: recommended
    title: "Poetry as Philosophy"
    description: "Rainer Maria Rilke, Letters to a Young Poet (prose, but about the poetic life — 'You must change your life'). T.S. Eliot, The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock (modernist fragmentation, self-consciousness). W.B. Yeats, The Second Coming ('Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold'). Borges' poetry (alongside his prose — already a framework universe). Spanish: Octavio Paz (selected — already referenced in age 14-16). Neruda deepened: Canto General, Residencia en la Tierra. Emily Dickinson: 'Tell all the truth but tell it slant.' Write: attempt a sonnet (14 lines, iambic pentameter, rhyme scheme). The constraint IS the creative engine."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Read poetry that operates at the level of philosophy."
      - "Write within formal constraints (sonnet, villanelle) and experience how constraint produces creativity."
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: recommended
    title: "Poetry as Identity"
    description: "By 18, they have a personal anthology — poems they carry in memory and draw on. They can quote Ozymandias, 'If,' the Litany Against Fear, the opening of Don Quijote in Spanish, and Borges at will. They have written poems of their own that they are not embarrassed by. Poetry is not a school subject — it is a relationship with language that deepens everything else: argument, empathy, observation, memory. Rumi (selected, Barks translations): 'Out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and rightdoing, there is a field. I'll meet you there.' This is the bridge between Western and Eastern philosophy, expressed in poetry."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Have a personal anthology of memorised poems drawn from multiple languages and traditions."
      - "Write poetry that expresses genuine thought and feeling, not performance."

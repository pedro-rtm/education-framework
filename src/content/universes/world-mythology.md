label: World Mythology
kind: literary-canon
summary: "Greek, Norse, Egyptian — the parallel operating systems humans built to explain the same phenomena. Comparative reading exposes structure."
whyItBelongs: "Myth is pre-scientific cognition preserved. Every culture independently generated stories for death, weather, creation, heroism — the overlaps expose cognitive universals, the differences expose cultural variables. Reading one mythology gives archetypes; reading three comparatively gives the meta-pattern (Campbell's monomyth, Lévi-Strauss's structural oppositions). This universe is the substrate under nearly all later Western literature, fantasy, and game design. It pairs with the homer universe but does not replace it — Homer is a specific epic; this is the taxonomic survey."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-4-7
    tier: core
    title: "D'Aulaires' Book of Greek Myths"
    description: "The gateway edition. Hand-drawn lithographs, one god per spread, warmth and weirdness intact. Read aloud over months — the child builds a mental roster of Olympians before meeting them anywhere else. Foundational cultural literacy."
    mode: absorb
    goals:
      - "Name the twelve Olympians and their domains."
      - "Retell at least five core myths (Persephone, Icarus, Pandora, Orpheus, Heracles)."
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: core
    title: "D'Aulaires' Book of Norse Myths + Egyptian myths (Geraldine McCaughrean)"
    description: "Widen the lens. Norse myth is grimmer, more fatalistic (Ragnarök is baked in — Odin knows he loses). Egyptian myth is cyclical and geographical (the Nile is the story). Comparing three pantheons trains the comparative-structural instinct: why do all three have an underworld? Why do trickster figures (Hermes, Loki, Set) exist everywhere?"
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Identify parallel figures across pantheons (sky-king, trickster, underworld-god)."
      - "Articulate one meaningful difference in how each culture treats death."
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Edith Hamilton's Mythology + Bulfinch"
    description: "Step up to the canonical adult treatments. Hamilton reads Greek myth with a classicist's ear; she quotes the sources (Homer, Hesiod, Ovid) directly. This is the bridge text to the homer universe. Begin to see myth as a library of narrative primitives that Shakespeare, Joyce, and Marvel all draw from."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Trace one mythic motif (descent-and-return, hubris, metamorphosis) across three cultures."
      - "Start identifying myth-sources in later works the child already knows."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: recommended
    title: "Joseph Campbell, Hero with a Thousand Faces (selections) + Ovid's Metamorphoses"
    description: "The structural turn. Campbell argues that all hero myths follow the same template; Lévi-Strauss that myths are machines for mediating cultural contradictions. Read Ovid in a good translation (Ted Hughes's Tales from Ovid is viable) to see Greek myth as literary art, not just story source."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Apply the monomyth template to a modern film and critique where it fits and where it strains."
      - "Form an opinion on whether mythic structures reveal cognitive universals or are imposed by the analyst."

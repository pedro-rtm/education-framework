summary: "Adventure, duality of the self, moral complexity in plain prose; the Scottish writer who could speak to children and to Freud in the same career."
whyItBelongs: "Stevenson is a two-handed writer. With one hand he wrote adventure — Treasure Island essentially invented the modern pirate, Kidnapped is a superb historical thriller, A Child's Garden of Verses is canonical children's poetry. With the other hand he wrote Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde, a 60-page novella that gave English a phrase for the divided self and fed directly into Freud's model of the psyche. The range matters: the same writer who can compose a singable nursery verse can also diagnose the moral split that became 20th-century psychology. For a curriculum that values complementary-opposites, Jekyll/Hyde is the canonical case. For a curriculum that values game theory, Long John Silver — part father-figure, part killer — is one of English literature's great ambiguous villains."

    title: "A Child's Garden of Verses (read-aloud)"
    description: "The poems of childhood experience — the counterpane, the lamplighter, the wind, bed in summer. Stevenson writing for children as an adult remembering exactly how it felt to be one. Read aloud for rhythm; some of these poems have been memorised by English-speaking children for 140 years because the meter makes them stick. Low-stakes entry into Stevenson's voice."
    mode: absorb
    goals:

  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: foundational
    title: "Treasure Island (read-aloud then independent)"
    description: "The template for every pirate story since. Start as read-aloud — the opening chapters at the Admiral Benbow inn are pure atmosphere. Then hand it over for independent reading. Long John Silver is the payoff: he is genuinely kind to Jim and genuinely a murderer, and the book never resolves which is 'real'. The first encounter with a villain who is not simply a villain — training wheels for moral complexity. Pair with the question: can a person be both a friend and a threat?"
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Experience adventure narrative as a complete structure — setup, voyage, betrayal, reversal, return."
      - "Hold Long John Silver's double nature without resolving it — he is the introduction to ambiguity."
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Kidnapped and Dr. Jekyll & Mr. Hyde (selected)"
    description: "Kidnapped — David Balfour's journey through a Scotland of betrayal, Jacobite remnants, and loyalty tested under pressure. Historical thriller with real ethical weight. Then Jekyll and Hyde in selected form — the novella is short but dense. Focus on the premise: a man who has separated his 'good' self from his 'bad' self and discovers the bad self grows stronger the more it is indulged. This is the 19th-century text that fed directly into Freud's id/ego/superego and into every 'dark side' trope since Star Wars."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Use Kidnapped to see how loyalty tests function as character reveals."
      - "Diagnose Jekyll's error: separating the parts of himself did not eliminate them; it gave one of them free rein."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: recommended
    title: "Dr. Jekyll & Mr. Hyde (full, with critical framing)"
    description: "The full novella with critical context. Read alongside a short excerpt from Freud's 'The Ego and the Id' — Stevenson's fiction precedes Freud's psychology by a decade and maps onto it almost perfectly. Discuss: is the self one thing or many? If many, which one is 'you'? Stevenson does not answer; he dramatises the question. This is the entry point to thinking about the self as a system of competing drives rather than a single will."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Read Jekyll and Hyde as a proto-psychoanalytic text — fiction can precede and shape later theory."
      - "Interrogate the premise that the 'self' is a unified thing; Stevenson suggests it is a coalition."

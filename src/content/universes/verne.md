summary: "The inventor of hard science fiction; adventure narratives grounded in actual physics, engineering, and geography, decades before the technologies they predicted existed."
whyItBelongs: "Verne is the writer who made engineering narratable. Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea describes a functional submarine in 1870 — before any real one existed. From the Earth to the Moon calculates a lunar trajectory in 1865 that turned out to be strikingly close to Apollo's. Journey to the Centre of the Earth reasons from geology as it was then understood. This is first-principles-reasoning rendered as adventure: Verne starts with what physics allows and builds a story inside the constraint. For a STEM-adjacent humanities curriculum, Verne is the bridge — he legitimises the fusion of technical thinking and narrative imagination. Critical edition note: avoid 19th-century English abridgements (they cut the science); use Frederick Paul Walter or William Butcher modern translations. The older Mercier Lewis English versions are mutilated and should not be read."

    title: "Verne in full — modern translations (Walter or Butcher)"
    description: "The full novels, in modern English translations that preserve the science. Twenty Thousand Leagues: the Nautilus is described in enough mechanical detail that a reader can reverse-engineer it. Journey to the Centre: the geology is period-accurate — read it as a time capsule of what 1864 knew about the earth. From the Earth to the Moon: the Baltimore Gun Club calculates muzzle velocity, gravitational slingshot, and landing — a century before Apollo. These are the texts that taught Asimov, Bradbury, and Clarke that fiction could be a vehicle for technical thought."
    mode: interrogate

    title: "Verne as ancestor of hard SF — lineage to Asimov, Clarke, Lem, Liu Cixin"
    description: "Trace the lineage. Verne invented the mode where speculative fiction stays within the physics envelope. Wells invented the mode where fiction uses physics as metaphor (the time machine is not engineering, it is ideology). Hard SF from Asimov through Clarke through Lem through Liu Cixin descends from Verne, not Wells. Read one Verne novel alongside one chapter of Clarke's 2001 or Liu's Three-Body Problem and map the inheritance: Verne established that constraint is the parent of invention, and that an engineer can be a protagonist."
    mode: critique

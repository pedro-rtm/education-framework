summary: "Post-colonial literature — the collision between indigenous culture and colonialism, told from inside the culture being destroyed."
whyItBelongs: "The framework has Butler for Afrofuturism and Conrad for European colonial self-examination. Achebe provides what neither can: the African perspective on colonialism, told from inside the culture being colonised. Things Fall Apart is the most widely read African novel — 200 pages about Okonkwo, a leader in Igbo society, whose world is dismantled by British missionaries and colonial administration. Achebe wrote it partly in response to Conrad's Heart of Darkness, which he argued dehumanised Africans even while critiquing colonialism. For a framework that values multiple perspectives, Achebe provides the perspective that European literature systematically excluded."

    description: "Arrow of God: a chief priest caught between his traditional authority and British indirect rule — forced to be an instrument of the system destroying his culture. No Longer at Ease: Okonkwo's grandson, Western-educated, returns to Lagos and is ground down by corruption he was educated to transcend. Together with Things Fall Apart, these three novels span three generations of colonial impact: the society before contact, the moment of collision, and the aftermath. Achebe's genius is that none of his characters are simple — Okonkwo's pride is his downfall, the missionaries include genuinely kind people, the colonial system is evil but the individuals within it are human."

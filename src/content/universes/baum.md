summary: "The first intentionally American fairy tale; a constructed mythology where the characters already possess what they believe they are seeking."
whyItBelongs: "European fairy tales are inherited — Grimm and Perrault codified centuries of oral tradition. Baum did something different: he sat down in 1900 and constructed an American mythology from scratch. That is unusual enough to be worth studying as a case in itself. And Oz is built on one of literature's cleanest inversions: the Scarecrow wants a brain and is the party's strategist; the Tin Woodman wants a heart and is the most tender; the Lion wants courage and consistently does the brave thing. They already have what they seek. The Wizard's final gift is not the thing but the recognition of the thing. For a curriculum that teaches inversion and complementary-opposites, Oz is a load-bearing early text. Bonus: the Wizard himself is a fraud with a PR operation — early material for power-structures analysis."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-4-7
    tier: foundational
    title: "The Wonderful Wizard of Oz (read-aloud)"
    description: "Read the original 1900 text aloud, not a picture-book reduction. The book is more formal and more strange than the 1939 film — the Wicked Witch is green only in the movie; the silver shoes are silver, not ruby; there are additional countries beyond the four MGM showed. Let the child hear a constructed mythology for the first time. The central structure is already legible at this age: four companions each convinced they lack something, each demonstrating they have it, each granted a symbol of what they already had. That is a story about self-knowledge."
    mode: absorb
    goals:

      - "Notice the inversion: the companions each have what they think they lack — the journey is recognition, not acquisition."
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: recommended
    title: "The Oz series (independent reading)"
    description: "Baum wrote fourteen Oz books; together with the continuations by Ruth Plumly Thompson, the series is large enough for independent reading across a couple of years. The Marvelous Land of Oz introduces Ozma and gender-change as a plot device in 1904 — worth noticing. Ozma of Oz introduces Tik-Tok, an early clockwork robot. The Emerald City of Oz contains one of the first utopian economies in American children's literature (no money, work is what people choose). Read them to absorb the sense that a single author can sustain an imagined world across many books — a direct predecessor of Narnia, Middle-Earth, Discworld, Harry Potter."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Follow a multi-book imagined world and feel how consistency of world is itself a creative discipline."
      - "Notice the Emerald City's utopian economy — Baum is doing light political speculation under the surface."

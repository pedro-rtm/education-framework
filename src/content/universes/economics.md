summary: "How value is created, priced, and exchanged — prices as information, markets as coordination mechanisms, and the institutions that make trade possible."
whyItBelongs: "You cannot understand power without understanding markets. You cannot understand markets without understanding prices as information signals. Economics is not about money — it is about how humans coordinate behaviour under scarcity. A child who understands comparative advantage, supply and demand, and market failures can evaluate political claims from first principles."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: recommended
    title: "Trade, exchange, and opportunity cost"
    description: "Trade and exchange. Why do people trade? Because both sides benefit (comparative advantage, without naming it). The Moonjar bank: spend, save, share. 'If you buy this toy, what else could that money be?' (Opportunity cost, already in the model curriculum.)"
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Understand that trade benefits both sides — not just the seller."
      - "Grasp opportunity cost intuitively: choosing one thing means giving up another."
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Supply, demand, and comparative advantage"
    description: "Supply and demand: why do prices change? Simulated stock portfolio: track real stocks, explain every decision. Comparative advantage: why does the plumber hire a cleaner even if they can clean faster? This is one of the most counterintuitive and powerful ideas in economics. Inflation: why does the same thing cost more over time? Why do some nations get rich and others don't? (Acemoglu & Robinson's Why Nations Fail concepts — inclusive vs. extractive institutions.) The child should know that market economies correlate with poverty reduction, but the mechanism — institutions, not markets alone — matters."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Understand supply and demand as the mechanism that sets prices."
      - "Grasp comparative advantage — one of the most counterintuitive ideas in economics."
      - "Explain inflation: why the same thing costs more over time."
      - "Understand that institutions (inclusive vs. extractive) explain economic development better than geography or culture."
    projects:
      - "Simulated stock portfolio: track real stocks for 3 months, explain every buy/sell decision."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: core
    title: "Market failures, incentives, and the knowledge problem"
    description: "Freakonomics (Levitt & Dubner). Hayek's knowledge problem: why prices contain more information than any central planner can process. Market failures: externalities, public goods, information asymmetry. Apply to tech platforms: why are social media companies free? 'If the product is free, you are the product.' Smith's Wealth of Nations (selected chapters). Mises' economic calculation problem: why central planners cannot replicate what prices do. Subjective value theory: value is not intrinsic to objects but exists in the mind of the valuer — the resolution to the diamond-water paradox. Marx as analytical framework (not political program): exploitation, alienation, and commodity fetishism as diagnostic tools for understanding how labour relates to capital. 'You don't have to be a Marxist to find Marx's analysis useful.' Market failures beyond the textbook: healthcare, education, housing — sectors where market logic produces outcomes that even market advocates find absurd. Environmental externalities at planetary scale — the market's systematic failure to price ecological destruction. Rent-seeking versus value creation: when 'capitalism' means extraction rather than production."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Understand Hayek's knowledge problem: prices as distributed information systems."
      - "Identify market failures: externalities, public goods, information asymmetry."
      - "Apply economic reasoning to technology platforms and everyday decisions."
      - "Understand Marx as an analytical framework: exploitation and alienation as diagnostic tools, not political program."
      - "Identify where markets systematically fail: healthcare, housing, education, environment."
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: core
    title: "Keynes vs Hayek and creative destruction"
    description: "Keynes vs Hayek: the central debate of macroeconomics. Behavioral economics challenges rational actor models. Creative destruction (Schumpeter): why do industries die and is that good? The student should be able to read the Financial Times and evaluate economic arguments on their merits. Austrian business cycle theory: credit expansion drives malinvestment, creating booms that must end in busts. Böhm-Bawerk's capital theory and time preference. Piketty's Capital in the Twenty-First Century: r > g — when returns on capital exceed economic growth, wealth concentrates structurally. This is the most important economics book of the 2010s. Sen's capability approach: the question is not GDP per capita but 'what can people actually do and be?' Universal Basic Income: Bregman's Utopia for Realists, Yang's The War on Normal People, the evidence from pilot programmes (Finland, Stockton, Kenya). 'Capitalism' is not one thing: Nordic social democracy vs. Anglo-American laissez-faire vs. Chinese state capitalism vs. Singapore's technocratic model. The student should be able to articulate the case FOR markets (poverty reduction, innovation, information aggregation) AND the critique (inequality, externalities, rent-seeking) with evidence, and form their own grounded position. 'Has capitalism been good?' is the wrong question. 'Which market failures matter most, and what institutions correct them?' is the right one."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Understand the Keynes-Hayek debate as the central tension of macroeconomics."
      - "Evaluate how behavioral economics challenges the rational actor assumption."
      - "Read the Financial Times and evaluate economic arguments on their merits."
      - "Articulate the case for AND against market economies with evidence — and form a grounded personal position."
      - "Understand that 'capitalism' is a spectrum of institutional arrangements, not a single system."

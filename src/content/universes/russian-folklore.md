summary: "Afanasyev, Pushkin's verse tales, and the Slavic folk imagination — Baba Yaga, Firebird, Vasilisa."
whyItBelongs: "The Russian folk corpus offers a moral universe distinct from the Western European one. Baba Yaga is not a Western witch: she is ambivalent — sometimes helper, sometimes devourer, governed by her own rules (greeting, naming, hospitality) rather than by simple evil. The Firebird, the Grey Wolf, Koschei the Deathless, Vasilisa the Wise — these figures operate inside a logic of tests, riddles, and thresholds. The lesson for a child is structural: the moral world need not be binary; power and danger can be negotiated if you know the forms. Pushkin's verse tales (Tsar Saltan, Golden Cockerel, Dead Princess) take the folk material and crystallise it into the most quoted Russian literary form — these are to Russian what nursery rhymes are to English, but with more plot. Afanasyev's full collection (the Russian Grimm) is the scholarly anchor."

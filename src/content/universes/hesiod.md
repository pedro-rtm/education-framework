summary: "The taxonomy of Greek myth — genealogy of the gods and the moral economy of agricultural labour."
whyItBelongs: "Hesiod is the implicit prerequisite to Homer. The Iliad and Odyssey presuppose a world already populated and ordered; Hesiod supplies that order. The Theogony is a cosmogony and divine genealogy — how chaos becomes structure, how power passes from Ouranos to Kronos to Zeus, how the hierarchy settles. Works and Days is the counterweight: a didactic poem to his feckless brother Perses, arguing that justice, honest labour, and knowledge of the agricultural calendar are the only real protections against hunger. Together they give children two complementary lenses — cosmological (how the order of things came to be) and practical-ethical (how to live well inside it). Reading Hesiod after Homer reveals how much Homer assumes; reading him alongside the Old Testament creation narratives reveals the structural commonalities of near-Eastern mythography."

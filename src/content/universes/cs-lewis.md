summary: "The Chronicles of Narnia. Allegorical epic with explicit Christian theology — structurally opposed to the Pullman universe and doubly valuable because of it."
whyItBelongs: "Narnia is a seven-book allegorical epic in which Aslan is Christ, the White Witch is evil-as-cold, and the children are pilgrim souls. It is unashamedly Christian, which is precisely what makes it pedagogically important in a household that also reads His Dark Materials — Pullman wrote Dark Materials explicitly as a rebuttal to Lewis, casting the Church as the antagonist and celebrating the Fall. Reading both is the best training ground for complementary-opposites and inversion that the juvenile canon offers: two allegorical epics, same structural ambitions, diametrically opposed theological stances, both powerful. A child who reads only Lewis gets a theological imprint; a child who reads only Pullman gets its inverse. A child who reads both gets the ability to hold two serious frameworks in tension and judge them. Lewis's prose is also clean and classical — he was an Oxford medievalist; the craft is underneath the story."

  - phaseId: age-4-7
    tier: core
    title: "The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe (read-aloud)"
    description: "The entry book, though second in internal chronology. Lucy steps through the wardrobe into perpetual winter, meets the faun, meets Aslan. The crucifixion-and-resurrection shape of Aslan's death at the Stone Table is unmistakable to adults but reads as pure story to a young child. Publication order (Wardrobe first) is the recommended reading order — The Magician's Nephew as prequel ruins Wardrobe's reveals."

    cautions: "Aslan's death scene is intense — humiliation and killing on the Stone Table. The resurrection arrives in the next chapter, so the redemptive arc closes quickly. Read in one or two sittings, not paused overnight mid-death."

  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: core
    title: "The full Chronicles of Narnia (publication order: 1-7)"
    description: "Read all seven — Wardrobe, Prince Caspian, Voyage of the Dawn Treader, Silver Chair, Horse and His Boy, Magician's Nephew, The Last Battle. Dawn Treader is the strongest mid-book (voyage structure, episodic, Eustace's dragon transformation is a moral masterpiece). The Last Battle is tonally difficult — it is essentially an apocalypse, and its treatment of Susan ('no longer a friend of Narnia') is the most contested passage in children's literature. Read it; discuss it."
    mode: dim
    cautions: "The Last Battle contains apocalypse, death of core characters, and the Susan problem. Discuss openly afterwards."
    goals:

      - "Track Aslan's role across the books — not merely as character but as structural presence."
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Narnia vs. His Dark Materials — structured comparison"
    description: "Direct comparative reading with the pullman universe. Both are allegorical children's epics with cosmological stakes, built-in theologies, and a child protagonist opposing a great power. Lewis's cosmos centres on Aslan/Christ; Pullman's frames the Authority as tyrant and the Fall as liberation. The child reads both and articulates where each frame is most persuasive, where each overreaches. This is foundational philosophical training — not deciding who is right, but learning to hold two serious frameworks in tension."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Articulate the theological and moral framework of each author in the child's own words."
      - "Identify at least two passages in each series where the other author would have written it differently — and why."

    title: "Mere Christianity + The Screwtape Letters + Till We Have Faces"
    description: "Lewis for the adolescent. Mere Christianity is the BBC-broadcast apologetics (argue with it — that is the point). Screwtape Letters is a senior demon's correspondence on how to corrupt a human soul (inversion as literary form). Till We Have Faces is Lewis's Cupid-and-Psyche retelling, his most literarily serious adult novel. Engage critically — Lewis is a clear thinker worth disagreeing with as a peer."
    mode: critique

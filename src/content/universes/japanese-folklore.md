whyItBelongs: "Japanese folklore operates on a principle Western folklore largely lacks: the permeability of the visible and invisible worlds. The kwaidan (ghost story) tradition, the yōkai bestiary, and the mononoke corpus assume that spirits, foxes, tanuki, yurei, and demons inhabit the same space as the living, bound by etiquette rather than by exclusion. The aesthetic category is yūgen — profundity suggested via understatement, what is implied rather than shown. This is the deep structural root of Miyazaki's cinema: Spirited Away, Princess Mononoke, and Totoro are not children's fantasies — they are Miyazaki mining the native folk corpus with cinematic fidelity. Lafcadio Hearn (Patrick Lafcadio Hearn, Greek-Irish-American who became Koizumi Yakumo) is the canonical English gateway; his Kwaidan (1904) remains the Western reader's entry. Ueda Akinari's Ugetsu Monogatari (1776) is the literary masterpiece of the supernatural tale; Yanagita Kunio's Legends of Tōno (1910) is the founding text of Japanese folkloristics."

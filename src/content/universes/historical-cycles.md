  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: core
    title: "Pattern Recognition: Thucydides, Ibn Khaldun, Dalio"
    description: "Three frameworks for understanding civilisational dynamics, each from a different era and tradition. Thucydides (Peloponnesian War, selections): the Thucydides Trap — when a rising power threatens an established power, war is the historical default. Apply to US-China, Spain-England (16th century), Athens-Sparta. Ibn Khaldun (Muqaddimah, selections): the most sophisticated pre-modern model of historical change. Asabiyyah (group cohesion) drives conquest; luxury and complacency erode it within 3-4 generations. Apply to the Arab conquests, the Ottoman rise and decline, the American trajectory. Ray Dalio (Principles for Dealing with the Changing World Order): the debt cycle, reserve currency lifecycle, internal order/disorder, and the rise-and-fall pattern across the Dutch, British, and American empires. The data-driven, quantitative approach to civilisational analysis. Essential cinema: Lawrence of Arabia (imperial ambition and its personal cost), Battle of Algiers (revolution and counter-insurgency as systems — the most important political film ever made)."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Understand three distinct analytical frameworks for civilisational cycles."
      - "Apply the Thucydides Trap to current geopolitics."
      - "Understand Dalio's debt cycle and reserve currency framework."
      - "Read Ibn Khaldun's asabiyyah theory and test it against multiple empires."
    projects:
      - "Using Dalio's framework, assess where the current global order sits in the cycle. Present evidence for and against."
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: core
    title: "Synthesis: Building Your Own Historical Model"
    description: "Gibbon (The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire, selected chapters): the foundational text on civilisational decline. The 20th century as one continuous arc: WWI → Versailles → WWII → Cold War → decolonisation → globalisation → present fractures. Peter Turchin's cliodynamics (Ages of Discord): quantitative history — can we predict civilisational instability using data? The mature question: 'What are the structural forces shaping the next 30 years? What can I see now that will seem obvious in retrospect?' Build an original historical model: combine the frameworks studied, identify the variables that matter most, and make falsifiable predictions."
    mode: critique
    goals:

      - "Distinguish pattern-matching (useful) from historical determinism (dangerous)."
      - "Understand that 'history doesn't repeat but it rhymes' — and be able to identify the rhyme scheme."
    projects:

kind: creator-canon
summary: "A technically brilliant writer whose politics are indefensible; the curriculum's hardest case for holding artistic merit and ideological damage simultaneously."
whyItBelongs: "Kipling is the most complicated author in the framework, and that is precisely why he is in it. On one side: The Jungle Book invented a template of talking-animal fable that most children's literature since has borrowed from; Just So Stories is a masterclass in language-play and etymological imagination; Kim is arguably the finest English-language novel ever written about identity-across-cultures. On the other side: Kipling was a full-throated apologist for British imperialism. 'The White Man's Burden' was not satire — it was written sincerely to urge American colonisation of the Philippines. Both things are true. Neither cancels the other. For a reader who will grow up in a world that demands nuanced judgment of historical figures, Kipling is training: he teaches the reader to hold aesthetic excellence and moral failure in the same hand without collapsing either. This is complementary-opposites applied to the moral evaluation of an artist — a skill the 21st century requires."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-4-7
    tier: recommended
    title: "Just So Stories (read-aloud)"
    description: "The etymological fables — How the Camel Got His Hump, How the Elephant Got His Trunk, The Cat That Walked By Himself. Linguistically the richest of Kipling's children's work: he invents words, plays with rhythm, and shows the pleasure of English as a musical system. Read aloud for the sound. At this age the imperial framing is not yet an issue — the stories are animal fables. Bank the language-love now; the critique will come later."
    mode: absorb
    goals:
      - "Absorb the music and invention of Kipling's language — this is English as play."
      - "Build early vocabulary through unusual words in rhythmic context."
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: core
    title: "The Jungle Book (independent reading)"
    description: "Mowgli in the wolf pack. The Law of the Jungle as a formal code — read it as a constitution for a society. Baloo, Bagheera, and Shere Khan as archetypes that most subsequent talking-animal fiction quotes from. At this age, read for narrative and character; the imperial framing is present (the Jungle Book is partly a parable about British India) but is legible as adventure rather than argument. Pair with Disney only after reading the original — the book is darker, more formal, and more interesting than the film."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Experience Mowgli as a figure who crosses between systems — wolf law, human village, jungle order."
      - "Notice that the animals argue from codified principle; the Jungle Book is about the function of law."
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: recommended
    title: "Kim (first read)"
    description: "Kim O'Hara — Irish orphan in India, disciple of a Tibetan lama, apprentice in the British Great Game of colonial espionage. The novel is a masterclass in identity-across-cultures: Kim is never entirely one thing, and the book treats that plurality as strength. First read for narrative and character — the critique layer can wait. Kim is the best entry point to Kipling's complexity: the text is genuinely multicultural and genuinely imperial at the same time, and that tension is what makes it more interesting than a purely ideological text."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Read Kim as a study of a self that holds multiple cultural allegiances without collapsing."
      - "Notice that the Great Game is spy work for the empire — the adventure serves a colonial project."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: core
    title: "Kipling with critique — Kim re-read, plus 'The White Man's Burden' and Orwell's essay on Kipling"
    description: "Re-read Kim with eyes open. Read 'The White Man's Burden' in full — the poem that Kipling wrote to urge American colonisation of the Philippines. Then read George Orwell's 1942 essay 'Rudyard Kipling' — Orwell, himself anti-imperial, argues that Kipling is a good bad poet, that his politics are indefensible and his craft genuine. The thesis of this phase: Kipling is brilliant and imperialist; both must be held simultaneously. This trains the reader in complementary-opposites applied to moral judgment of historical writers. The habit is transferable — Heidegger and Nazism, Wagner and antisemitism, Dickens and sexism, Picasso and misogyny. Learn to think here and you will have the tool for all of those."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Evaluate the argument of 'The White Man's Burden' — it is not ironic; take it at its word and judge it."
      - "Use Orwell's essay as a model for the critical move: affirm craft, condemn politics, do not collapse one into the other."
    cautions: "This is the curriculum's hardest case. The point is not to cancel Kipling or to rehabilitate him — both are too easy. The point is to hold the contradiction until it becomes a tool."

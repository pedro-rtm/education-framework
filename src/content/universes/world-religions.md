whyItBelongs: "The framework claims to prepare globally literate humans, but a person who doesn't understand the basic architecture of the world's major religions is not globally literate. These traditions shaped history, law, art, ethics, and the daily lives of billions. Understanding them is as fundamental as understanding political systems or economic models. The framework's analytical, non-moralistic register is the right approach: study each tradition as an intellectual system — what it claims about reality, what ethics it derives, what it produces culturally, where it succeeds and where it fails. Not comparative theology — comparative anthropology of meaning-making."

  - phaseId: age-4-7
    tier: recommended
    title: "Stories from traditions"
    description: "Bible stories and Greek myths as narrative — the same way they consume Avatar. Noah's Ark, David and Goliath, the Nativity, Diwali stories, Eid stories. These are humanity's oldest stories; treat them as rich narrative, not doctrine. Visit different places of worship if accessible: a church, a mosque, a synagogue, a temple. 'Different people believe different things about the biggest questions. That's interesting, not threatening.'"
    mode: absorb
    goals:
      - "Encounter religious narratives as stories, alongside other cultural narratives."
      - "Experience the diversity of religious practice through visits and festivals."
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: recommended
    title: "What do people believe and why?"
    description: "Name the major traditions and their core claims. Christianity: one God, Jesus as saviour, afterlife. Islam: one God (Allah), Muhammad as final prophet, Five Pillars. Judaism: one God, chosen people, Torah as law. Hinduism: many expressions of ultimate reality (Brahman), karma, dharma, reincarnation. Buddhism: suffering, impermanence, the path to liberation. 'These aren't just beliefs — they're systems that billions of people use to answer the hardest questions: Why are we here? What happens when we die? How should we live?'"
    mode: absorb
    goals:
      - "Know the basic architecture of 5 major world religions."
      - "Understand that religion answers questions that science doesn't address."
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Structure, history, and the philosophy of religion"
    description: "Deeper: the Five Pillars of Islam (shahada, salat, zakat, sawm, hajj). The Ten Commandments. The Four Noble Truths. The concept of dharma and karma. How did Christianity spread? Why did Islam split into Sunni and Shia? Why are there so many Protestant denominations? Religion as social technology: what functions does it serve (community, moral code, meaning, ritual, identity)? Pascal's Wager and its flaws. The problem of evil. Faith vs. evidence — are they incompatible? The is-ought problem (Hume) applied to religious claims."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Understand the internal logic of each tradition — what would you have to believe for this system to be coherent?"
      - "Analyse religion as a social phenomenon, not just a set of beliefs."
    projects:
      - "Compare how two different religions answer the same question: What happens after death? What makes an action moral? Why does suffering exist?"
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: core
    title: "Comparative analysis and critique"
    description: "Read primary texts: selected Quran passages (with scholarly context), Gospel of Mark (the shortest, earliest Gospel), selected Upanishads, Tao Te Ching (already in Eastern Philosophy universe). Where do the major traditions agree on ethics? (The Golden Rule appears in every one.) Where do they irreconcilably conflict on metaphysics? Why do religions fracture into denominations and sects? (Apply the Incentives model: what incentives produce schism?) Secular humanism as an alternative: can you have ethics without God? Existentialism's answer. The New Atheism critique (Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris) and its limitations."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Read primary religious texts with analytical, not devotional, intent."
      - "Evaluate the New Atheism critique: where is it right? Where does it miss the point?"
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: recommended
    title: "Personal synthesis"
    description: "The question is no longer 'what do religions teach?' but 'what role does meaning-making play in human life, and what is mine?' Nietzsche: 'God is dead' and the vacuum it creates. Dostoevsky: the Grand Inquisitor — would humanity choose freedom or comfort? Viktor Frankl: meaning as the primary human drive, even in Auschwitz. Rumi (bridge between traditions): 'Out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and rightdoing, there is a field.' Build a personal position: religious, spiritual, secular, syncretic — arrived at through reasoning and experience, not inherited default."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Articulate a personal position on meaning, transcendence, and ethics — reasoned, not received."
      - "Understand that the question of meaning is permanent and that the answer may evolve."

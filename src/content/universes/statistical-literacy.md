summary: "How data presentations mislead — truncated axes, cherry-picked ranges, survivorship bias, base rate neglect — and how to read any graph, study, or statistical claim critically."
whyItBelongs: "Kahneman covers how the brain misleads itself. Behavioral Science covers how people manipulate socially. Neither covers how data presentations mislead deliberately — truncated Y-axes, cherry-picked date ranges, correlation presented as causation, survivorship bias in infographics, base rate neglect in headlines. In an era of data journalism, social media graphs, and AI-generated statistics, reading a chart critically is as foundational as reading text critically. A child who can spot a misleading graph at 11 is inoculated against a lifetime of statistical manipulation."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: foundational
    title: "Reading charts, estimation, and Gapminder"
    description: "Reading charts and graphs critically. Estimation games: 'How many people live in Africa? How many in Europe? Are you sure?' Gapminder tools (Hans Rosling's visual data platform) — interactive, mind-blowing, destroys assumptions about the world. 'This graph says crime is increasing — but look at the Y-axis. It starts at 95, not zero. What does the graph look like if you start at zero?' Data collection as play: 'How many red cars vs. blue cars did we see today? Let's make a chart.' The seed: every graph is a choice about what to show and what to hide."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Read any simple chart or graph and identify what it actually shows versus what it appears to show."
      - "Build the estimation reflex: before accepting any number, ask 'does that sound right?'"
      - "Experience data collection firsthand — measuring, counting, charting."
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: foundational
    title: "How to Lie with Statistics and media literacy"
    description: "How to Lie with Statistics (Darrell Huff, 1954) — the classic. Short, devastating, accessible at 11. Teaches: truncated axes, misleading averages (mean vs. median vs. mode), sampling bias, correlation ≠ causation, the semi-attached figure. Base rates: '200% increase in shark attacks!' sounds terrifying until you know the base rate was 1. Survivorship bias: we see the successes, not the failures. Media headline deconstruction: when a headline says 'study finds X', what did the study actually find? How big was the sample? Who funded it? What was the effect size?"
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Identify the six most common statistical manipulation techniques in any graph or headline."
      - "Understand base rates: a percentage change means nothing without the baseline."
      - "Deconstruct media headlines: 'study finds' → what study, what sample, what effect size, who funded it?"
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: foundational
    title: "Factfulness, Calling Bullshit, and statistical inference"
    description: "Rosling's Factfulness: the world is better than you think — and here's the data to prove it. Also teaches the ten instincts that distort our worldview (gap, negativity, straight line, fear, size, generalisation, destiny, single perspective, blame, urgency). Bergstrom & West's Calling Bullshit: how to detect misinformation in data presentations — the definitive guide. Statistical inference: p-values (what they mean, what they don't), confidence intervals, the replication crisis. Applied to current events: pandemic data, election polls, economic reporting. Naked Statistics (Wheelan) as accessible introduction to formal statistics."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Read Factfulness and recalibrate: replace instinct-driven worldview with data-driven one."
      - "Apply Calling Bullshit framework to any data presentation: source, sample, method, incentive."
      - "Understand p-values and the replication crisis — most 'statistically significant' findings are weaker than reported."
    projects:
      - "Find three misleading graphs in mainstream media. Remake them honestly. Present the before/after."
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: foundational
    title: "Weapons of Math Destruction, Bayesian thinking, and algorithmic bias"
    description: "O'Neil's Weapons of Math Destruction: how algorithms and statistical models become instruments of oppression — in policing, hiring, insurance, education. Spiegelhalter's The Art of Statistics: the gold standard introduction to modern statistical thinking. Bayesian thinking: updating beliefs based on evidence, not defending positions regardless of evidence. Algorithm bias: how training data encodes historical discrimination. The student can evaluate any data visualisation, any statistical claim, any 'study finds' headline, and any algorithmic decision — not as a statistician but as an informed citizen."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Understand algorithmic bias as historical discrimination encoded in training data."
      - "Apply Bayesian updating: change your mind proportionally to the evidence, not to social pressure."
      - "Evaluate any statistical claim like a scientist: source, method, sample, effect size, replication, incentive."

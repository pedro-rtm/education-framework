summary: "Justice versus mercy as a systems problem — the most important moral dialectic in Western fiction, scaling from musical to masterwork."
whyItBelongs: "Les Misérables fills a gap no other universe covers: institutional justice versus moral justice as a systems problem, not a personal dilemma. Valjean is the case study in redemption against a system designed to prevent it. Javert is the case study in moral rigidity that breaks when confronted with evidence it cannot process. The novel spans revolution, poverty, social systems, and the question of whether mercy is weakness or the highest form of strength. Critically, it scales: the musical works from age 8-10, the adapted novel from 11-13, the full text at 14-16. No other universe in the framework offers this progression from accessible to demanding across the same material."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: recommended
    title: "Les Misérables — the musical"
    description: "The musical (film or stage) as entry point. The emotional arc is accessible: Valjean's transformation, Javert's pursuit, the barricades, Éponine's sacrifice, the Thénardiers' comic villainy. 'Who Am I?' as the song that crystallises identity and moral courage. The musical simplifies the political and philosophical layers but delivers the emotional core intact. After watching: 'Was Javert wrong? He was following the law. Was the law wrong?'"
    mode: absorb
    goals:
      - "Experience the Valjean-Javert moral dialectic through accessible musical narrative."
      - "Begin the question: can a person who broke the law be more moral than the person enforcing it?"
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Les Misérables — adapted novel"
    description: "An adapted/abridged edition (the Marcia Williams illustrated version for younger readers, or a YA adaptation). The political context becomes visible: post-revolutionary France, the June Rebellion, the sewer system as literal and metaphorical underworld. The Thénardiers as capitalism without conscience. Cosette's transformation. Gavroche as the street child who knows more than the revolutionaries. Deeper than the musical but more accessible than the full text."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Understand the political and social systems that produce Valjean's situation — poverty, injustice, rigid law."
      - "See Les Misérables as a systems novel, not just a story of individual redemption."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: core
    title: "Les Misérables — full text"
    description: "The unabridged novel (~1,500 pages). Hugo's digressions — on the Battle of Waterloo, the Paris sewers, the history of French monasteries — are not filler; they are the systems architecture. The novel argues that individual moral courage operates within, and sometimes against, massive institutional systems. Valjean succeeds not because he is strong but because mercy is structurally more robust than rigidity. Javert breaks because his model cannot accommodate mercy. 'He who does not weep does not see.' Read at serial pace."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Engage with Hugo's digressions as systems analysis — Waterloo, sewers, monasteries are not tangents but structural arguments."
      - "Evaluate Hugo's thesis: is mercy structurally more robust than rigidity?"
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: recommended
    title: "Hugo's political vision and critique"
    description: "Les Misérables as political philosophy. Compare with Dickens (institutional critique from a different national tradition). Hugo as political exile — he wrote Les Misérables on Guernsey, in opposition to Napoleon III. The Hunchback of Notre-Dame as architectural preservation argument disguised as novel. Critique: where does Hugo's sentimentality undermine his analysis? Where does his political vision exceed what the novel can carry?"
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Read Hugo as a political philosopher, not just a novelist."
      - "Evaluate where sentimentality strengthens and where it weakens moral argument."

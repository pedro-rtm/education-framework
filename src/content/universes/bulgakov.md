Bulgakov fills the gap between them: surreal, political, structurally audacious, and — critically — funny. The Master and Margarita interweaves three narratives (the Devil in Moscow, a retelling of Pontius Pilate, and the love story of the title characters) into a novel that is simultaneously comedy, tragedy, theological argument, and political satire of Soviet censorship. Bulgakov wrote it knowing it could never be published in his lifetime. It was. Posthumously."

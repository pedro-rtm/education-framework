summary: "Monomania, metaphysical obsession, and the limits of human knowing pitted against indifferent nature; the American novel at its deepest register."
whyItBelongs: "Melville is the 19th-century American writer who pushed the novel past social realism into metaphysics. Moby-Dick is not a book about a whale; it is a book about the failure of every interpretive system a human brain can deploy against a non-human real. Ahab's chart, Ishmael's cetology, Starbuck's prudence — each is a map that fails. For a framework that teaches systems thinking, Melville is the stress-test: what happens when the map doesn't just mislead you but dooms you? Bartleby inverts the same question at the scale of a single office clerk. Billy Budd closes the loop by staging it in law. Three scales of the same problem — the universe, the workplace, the courtroom."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: recommended
    title: "Moby-Dick (abridged) and Bartleby the Scrivener"
    description: "Moby-Dick first in abridged form — the narrative spine without the full cetological digressions. Ahab's pursuit as a monomania case study: a man who has constructed an interpretive frame so total that every event confirms it. Then Bartleby: the clerk who responds to every request with 'I would prefer not to.' Read as the inverse of Ahab — refusal instead of pursuit, withdrawal instead of obsession. Both are studies in what happens when a person stops sharing a world with others."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Diagnose Ahab's monomania as a model that has stopped correcting itself against reality."
      - "Read Bartleby as a study of passive refusal — what is the cost of an unexplained 'no'?"
    cautions: "The despair in Melville is real. For a reader who is already struggling, Bartleby can cut close. Read with company."
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: core
    title: "Moby-Dick (full, Penguin Classics) and Billy Budd"
    description: "The full Moby-Dick — cetology included. The digressions are the point: Ishmael is building a taxonomy of the whale because he, too, needs a map, and the novel is showing that every map is partial. Read the chapter 'The Doubloon' as a masterclass in perspective — each crew member sees a different meaning in the same coin. Then Billy Budd: Melville's final novella, a trial narrative. Captain Vere must hang an innocent man to preserve naval discipline. The law says one thing, conscience says another, and Melville refuses to resolve the tension. This is complementary-opposites at full voltage."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Read 'The Doubloon' chapter as a demonstration that interpretation is perspectival."
      - "Use Billy Budd to interrogate the conflict between rule-following and moral judgment — Vere is right by law and wrong by conscience."
    cautions: "Moby-Dick rewards patience. Do not treat it as a plot novel. Read it the way one reads a philosophical treatise that happens to have a whale in it."

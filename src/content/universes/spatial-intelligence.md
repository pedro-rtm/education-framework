summary: "Maps as power tools, geopolitics as applied geography, and spatial reasoning as a distinct cognitive mode."
whyItBelongs: "Geography determines strategy more than ideology does. Maps are not neutral — they encode power, projection, and perspective. Spatial intelligence is a distinct cognitive mode from verbal or logical reasoning, and GIS is an increasingly critical skill across logistics, urban planning, military, and climate."
tags: [stem, strategy, philosophy]
languages: [english]
targets: [first-principles-reasoning, adaptability, deep-curiosity]

intensity: medium
socialValue: bridge
phases:
  - phaseId: age-1-3
    tier: core
    title: "Puzzles, construction, and spatial play"
    description: "Shape sorters, nesting cups, simple puzzles (4-12 pieces). These are not toys — they are the physical instantiation of spatial logic. Brio World wooden train layouts — the track-building IS spatial reasoning: which piece connects? Does the loop close? What happens if I add a bridge? Block towers with intentional collapse ('how tall before it falls?'). Magna-Tiles from 2.5+ for 3D construction. Grimm's Rainbow for freeform spatial sculpture. Physical maps begin: 'Where are we? Where is the park? Which way is home?' The child who has spent hundreds of hours fitting shapes, closing loops, and balancing structures arrives at age 4 already thinking in three dimensions."
    mode: absorb
    goals:
      - "Build spatial reasoning through physical manipulation — fitting, stacking, connecting, balancing."
      - "Develop systematic problem-solving: try, fail, adjust, retry."
      - "Begin spatial orientation: where am I relative to familiar places?"
  - phaseId: age-4-7
    tier: recommended
    title: "Physical maps and treasure maps"
    description: "Physical maps and globes. 'Where are we? Where is grandma? How far is that?' Treasure maps. Orienteering basics on hikes. Building maps of the neighbourhood from memory."
    mode: absorb
    goals:
      - "Develop spatial awareness: where am I relative to other places?"
      - "Understand that a map is a representation of reality, not reality itself."
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: core
    title: "Map reading and applied geography"
    description: "Map reading on hikes (real topographic maps, not phone GPS). Civilization VI as applied geography. Why do cities form where they do? Rivers, harbours, crossroads. Google Earth exploration."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Read real topographic maps: contour lines, scale, legend, compass bearings."
      - "Understand why settlements form at geographic features: rivers, harbours, crossroads, passes."
    projects:
      - "Navigate a hike using a topographic map and compass — no phone GPS."
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Geopolitics and maps as power"
    description: "Geopolitics introduction. Tim Marshall's Prisoners of Geography. Why does Russia need warm-water ports? Why is the Strait of Malacca the most important waterway on Earth? Maps as power: Mercator projection distorts Africa. Peters projection distorts shape. Every map is a choice."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Understand geopolitics as applied geography: physical terrain shapes political reality."
      - "Recognise that every map is a projection with inherent distortions and choices."
      - "Identify strategic chokepoints: Malacca, Suez, Panama, Bosphorus."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: core
    title: "GIS tools and geographic analysis"
    description: "GIS tools (QGIS, Google Earth Engine). Mackinder's Heartland Theory. Kaplan's Revenge of Geography. Apply to current events: why does geography explain the Ukraine conflict better than ideology? Spatial data analysis: census data, climate data, economic data on maps."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Use GIS tools to visualise and analyse spatial data."
      - "Evaluate geopolitical theories: Mackinder, Spykman, Kaplan — where do they explain and where do they over-determine?"
      - "Apply geographic analysis to current events with more explanatory power than ideology alone."
    projects:
      - "Create a GIS map layer combining geographic, demographic, and economic data for a region."
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: core
    title: "Critique of geographic determinism"
    description: "Critique: when does geographic determinism become reductionist? Braudel's Mediterranean — geography shapes history but doesn't determine it. GIS as professional skill. The child can read any map critically: 'Who made this? What does it emphasise? What does it hide?'"
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Evaluate geographic determinism: powerful lens, not complete explanation."
      - "Read any map critically: who made it, what does it emphasise, what does it hide?"
      - "GIS as a professional-grade analytical skill, not a classroom exercise."

summary: "Anansi, Sundiata, Mwindo, Ifá, and the griot tradition — African narrative beyond the colonial frame."
whyItBelongs: "The African literary canon, as taught in Western schools, collapses into Achebe. This is a pedagogical failure. Africa has a vast pre-colonial narrative corpus, most of it oral: the Anansi trickster cycle (Akan, West Africa, carried to the Caribbean by enslaved people and embedded in Anglophone children's literature); the Epic of Sundiata (13th-century Mali, the foundation story of the Mandé empire, still performed by griots); the Mwindo Epic (Nyanga, Congo); the Ifá divination corpus (Yoruba, a vast body of narrative-ethical verse used in divination, scholarly transcribed by Wande Abimbola). Reading these alongside Achebe corrects the silent assumption that African literature begins with colonial encounter. The meta-lesson is structural: oral traditions are distributed, variant, performance-dependent — which is itself a different theory of authorship and canonicity than the print tradition assumes. Ruth Finnegan's Oral Literature in Africa (1970, revised 2012) is the scholarly synthesis that makes this legible."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-4-7
    tier: recommended
    title: "Anansi the Spider (Gerald McDermott, Verna Aardema retellings)"
    description: "The Anansi trickster cycle from Akan (Ghana) oral tradition — carried to the Caribbean and the American South by enslaved West Africans, ancestor of Brer Rabbit. McDermott's Caldecott-honour picture book Anansi the Spider and Aardema's Why Mosquitoes Buzz in People's Ears are the best entry points. The lesson of the trickster: intelligence beats force; small beats big through cunning."
    mode: absorb
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Sundiata: An Epic of Old Mali (D.T. Niane)"
    description: "The founding epic of the Mandé empire (13th century Mali), transcribed by Niane from the griot Mamadou Kouyaté in the 1960s. It is African medieval epic — kings, prophecy, exile, return — with structural parallels to Odyssey and Mahabharata. The griot's performative voice is preserved in the prose; this is oral tradition captured mid-performance. Age-appropriate and electrifying."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Recognise epic structure across cultures: exile-return, destined king, prophetic birth."
      - "Register the griot as professional memory-keeper — a social role with no Western equivalent."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: recommended
    title: "Mwindo Epic (Nyanga / Congo) + Tutuola's The Palm-Wine Drinkard"
    description: "The Mwindo Epic is a Nyanga heroic narrative (Biebuyck & Mateene edition) — shorter than Sundiata, stranger, more magical. Pair with Tutuola's The Palm-Wine Drinkard (1952) — Yoruba-English hybrid prose, hallucinatory, the foundational Nigerian novel that refuses to obey colonial English. Dylan Thomas called it 'young English' on first encounter. Challenging prose; deeply rewarding."
    mode: interrogate
    cautions: "Tutuola's prose is initially disorienting by design — the grammar is Yoruba-inflected. That is the point, not a bug."
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: recommended
    title: "Ruth Finnegan — Oral Literature in Africa + Wande Abimbola on Ifá"
    description: "The meta-framework. Finnegan's Oral Literature in Africa (open-access via Open Book Publishers) is the canonical scholarly synthesis — how oral traditions work as literature, why the category 'oral literature' is not an oxymoron. Abimbola's Ifá: An Exposition of Ifá Literary Corpus introduces the Yoruba divination corpus — an enormous narrative-ethical system with a sophistication comparable to the Confucian classics. A sophisticated capstone."
    mode: critique

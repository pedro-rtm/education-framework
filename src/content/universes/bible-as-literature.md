label: The Bible as Literature
kind: literary-canon
summary: "The single most cited text in Western literature, read as a literary object rather than a devotional one. Narrative architecture, rhetorical patterns, and the long shadow cast on later writing."
whyItBelongs: "An educated reader cannot parse Dante, Milton, Melville, Dostoevsky, Faulkner, Morrison, Borges, Cormac McCarthy, or Marilynne Robinson without Biblical literacy. Read devotionally, the Bible teaches faith. Read as literature, it teaches how narrative, rhetoric, and metaphor were engineered in the ancient Near East and Hellenistic Mediterranean — and how those engineering choices still organise the sentences of writers who have never picked up the book. Robert Alter's translations of the Hebrew Bible restore the strangeness and terseness that domesticating translations (KJV-descendants included) smooth over. Northrop Frye's The Great Code is the most useful meta-framework. This universe is explicitly non-devotional: the goal is literacy, not belief."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: recommended
    title: "Selected narratives — Genesis, Exodus, David"
    description: "Read selected narratives from Robert Alter's Hebrew Bible translation (if accessible) or a children's illustrated edition with parallel reading later: Creation and Eden, Cain and Abel, Noah, Joseph's full arc (Genesis 37–50 — one of the tightest narratives in the ancient world), Exodus liberation narrative, David-and-Goliath and David-and-Bathsheba together. Frame explicitly: these are stories people have retold for three thousand years. Ask why."
    mode: absorb
    cautions: "Many stories contain violence (Cain, flood, Egypt plagues). Engage as narrative, not as moral instruction. Do not censor the darker ones — the child will encounter them culturally anyway."
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "Wisdom literature — Job, Ecclesiastes, Psalms, Song of Songs"
    description: "The philosophically and poetically densest parts of the Hebrew Bible. Job as the ancient world's clearest statement of the problem of evil — read alongside a classical philosophy treatment. Ecclesiastes as proto-existentialism (Alter's translation renders hevel as 'mere breath' — a better rendering than 'vanity'). Psalms as lyric poetry, with attention to parallelism as the structural device. Song of Songs as erotic poetry — explicit enough to deserve flagging, canonical enough to deserve reading."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Recognise parallelism as the fundamental architecture of Hebrew poetry."
      - "Engage with Ecclesiastes as an existential text without evangelising away its darkness."
    cautions: "Song of Songs is erotic. Job and Ecclesiastes are bleak in a way adult readers often mistake for despair."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: core
    title: "Gospels comparative + Paul as rhetoric"
    description: "Read Mark (shortest, most urgent), Matthew (structured, Jewish-framed), Luke (literary, Hellenistic), John (philosophical, logos-opening) as four different genres treating the same subject. Compare the openings, the passion accounts, the resurrection treatments. Then Paul's letters — Romans and 1 Corinthians 13 especially — as ancient rhetoric. The Greek matters where possible (logos, agape, pistis do not map cleanly to English)."
    mode: critique
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: recommended
    title: "Revelation + Frye's The Great Code"
    description: "Revelation as apocalyptic literature — a genre with conventions, not a prediction document. Read alongside other apocalyptic texts (Daniel, 1 Enoch extracts). Then Northrop Frye's The Great Code: The Bible and Literature, which maps the typological, imagistic, and structural patterns the Bible bequeathed to Western writing. Optional: Erich Auerbach's Mimesis opening chapter on Genesis 22 vs. the Odyssey scar — one of the best single essays on Western narrative technique ever written."
    mode: critique

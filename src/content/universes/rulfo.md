summary: "The inventor of Latin American magical realism — 120 pages of Pedro Páramo and seventeen short stories."
whyItBelongs: "Rulfo wrote almost nothing and changed everything. Pedro Páramo (1955) is 120 pages; El Llano en llamas (1953) is seventeen short stories. That is the whole canonical output. With that, he invented the Latin American novel as García Márquez, Fuentes, and Vargas Llosa would inherit it — García Márquez said he could recite Pedro Páramo backwards and that without it he could not have written One Hundred Years of Solitude. The innovation: polyphonic narration from the dead, time collapsed into simultaneity, a ghost town (Comala) that reveals itself page by page as literally populated by ghosts. El Llano en llamas is the counterpart — spare, violent, rural Mexican stories of the Cristero War aftermath, post-revolution dispossession, drought. The register is understated to the point of austerity. Rulfo teaches that the reader must construct the novel; the author gives fragments. This is a demanding reading experience, appropriate for a serious teenage reader."

    title: "Pedro Páramo"
    description: "120 pages. First reading: disorientation. Second reading (immediately after): the structure clicks. The novel is a polyphonic chorus of the dead — Juan Preciado arrives in Comala to find his father, finds a ghost town, realises slowly that everyone he meets is dead, including eventually himself. Time is simultaneous, not sequential. García Márquez and Borges both identified this as the foundational Latin American novel. Margaret Sayers Peden's English translation is the standard; in Spanish the FCE / RAE critical editions are the authoritative texts."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Read it twice. The first reading produces confusion; the second produces the novel."
      - "Map the structural innovations: narrative from the dead, collapsed temporality, distributed voice."

    cautions: "Casual violence, despair, child death, implied incest. This is not a book for an unready reader; wait for genuine readiness."

summary: "Inventor of the modern short story, the detective tale, and the psychological horror form; master of the unreliable narrator and the architecture of dread."
whyItBelongs: "Poe is three inventions in one writer. He invented the detective story (Dupin — direct ancestor of Holmes, Poirot, every ratiocinating detective since). He codified the modern short story as a unit with a calculated emotional effect (his own essay 'The Philosophy of Composition' lays out the engineering). And he invented psychological horror — not horror from monsters but horror from inside the narrator's own head. For a curriculum that teaches cognitive-biases and unreliable narration, Poe is the laboratory: the narrators in Tell-Tale Heart, The Black Cat, and Ligeia are all insisting on their sanity in ways that prove the opposite. The reader learns to read against the narrator — a foundational critical skill."

  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: recommended
    title: "Selected tales — Tell-Tale Heart, Cask of Amontillado, Masque of the Red Death"
    description: "Entry-level Poe. The Tell-Tale Heart is the cleanest example of the unreliable narrator in English literature — a three-page masterclass in a voice insisting on its own sanity while describing obvious madness. The Cask of Amontillado is a revenge plot executed with cold precision; read as a case study in how grievance becomes obsession. The Masque of the Red Death is plague-as-allegory — Prince Prospero cannot wall out mortality. All three are short enough to read in a sitting and dense enough to discuss for a week."
    mode: dim
    goals:

    cautions: "These are horror stories. They contain murder, premature burial, and psychological disintegration. Read with a parent nearby the first time."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: core
    title: "Full range — Usher, Pit and the Pendulum, Ligeia, and the poems"
    description: "The fuller Poe. The Fall of the House of Usher — the house IS Roderick, crumbling in sympathy with its master's mind. The Pit and the Pendulum — dread engineered stroke by stroke, a story that teaches pacing by demonstration. Ligeia — obsession, grief, and the refusal to let the dead stay dead. Then the poems: The Raven (the sound of 'Nevermore' as a closed feedback loop), Annabel Lee (grief as beauty), Ulalume (mourning as maze). Poe wrote 'The Philosophy of Composition' about The Raven — pair them and see how deliberately the effect was constructed."
    mode: interrogate
    goals:
      - "Read Usher as a study of a mind and its environment collapsing together — a feedback loop rendered in architecture."
      - "Use The Raven alongside Poe's essay on its composition to see horror as engineering, not accident."
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: recommended
    title: "Poe as genre inventor — Dupin, the detective story, and modern short fiction"
    description: "Read The Murders in the Rue Morgue, The Mystery of Marie Rogêt, and The Purloined Letter as a trilogy. Dupin is the prototype: a reasoner who solves by inhabiting the criminal's mind — the method is first-principles reasoning applied to crime. Then read Poe's 'Philosophy of Composition' and 'The Poetic Principle' as critical theory. Finish by tracing Poe's influence: Conan Doyle said Holmes is Dupin's grandson; Baudelaire translated Poe into French and reshaped European literature around him; every modern horror story from Lovecraft to King owes him royalties. Poe is a proof-of-concept that a single writer can invent entire genres."
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Read the Dupin trilogy as the blueprint for every detective story that followed."
      - "Evaluate Poe's own criticism — he is a better theorist of his own work than most of his critics."

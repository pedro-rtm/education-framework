label: Western Philosophy
kind: literary-canon
summary: "From Socrates to Sartre — the tradition of systematic reasoning about truth, ethics, knowledge, and the good life."
whyItBelongs: "The framework references Stoicism, existentialism, utilitarianism, deontology, and virtue ethics across multiple phases but has no unified philosophical universe. Western philosophy provides the intellectual scaffolding for first-principles reasoning (Target 5), ethical reasoning (Target 2), and self-awareness (Target 3). Understanding the tradition — not as history but as a toolkit for living — gives children the vocabulary and frameworks to examine their own assumptions. The framework already borrows heavily from these traditions; this universe makes the borrowing explicit and systematic."
tags: [books, english, philosophy, classics]

intensity: high
socialValue: bridge
phases:
  - phaseId: age-8-10
    tier: recommended
    title: "Philosophy Through Story"
    description: "Not primary texts — philosophy introduced through accessible narrative. The children's adaptation of Plato's cave allegory (picture book versions exist). Aesop's Fables as ethical reasoning: 'What is the moral? Do you agree with it? Can you think of when the opposite would be true?' Sophie's World (Jostein Gaarder) at age 10 — the history of philosophy as a mystery novel. The goal is not to teach philosophy but to establish that people have been asking 'What is real? What is right? How should I live?' for 2,500 years."
    mode: absorb
    goals:
      - "Encounter the idea that 'big questions' have a tradition of rigorous investigation."
      - "Begin asking 'What is the argument? Do I agree?' about ethical claims."
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: core
    title: "The Big Three: Socrates, Plato, Aristotle"
    description: "Socrates: the examined life, the Socratic method (they have been practising it at dinner since age 4 — now they meet the inventor). The Apology: Socrates on trial for asking too many questions. Plato: the Allegory of the Cave, the Theory of Forms — the idea that reality might not be what it appears. Aristotle: virtue ethics ('the good life is one of excellent activity'), the golden mean, logic as formal system. These three established the rules of the game that Western thought still plays."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Understand the Socratic method as a formal technique, not just 'asking why.'"
      - "Engage with the Allegory of the Cave: what are modern 'caves'?"
      - "Understand virtue ethics: 'What would the best version of me do?' as a practical decision tool."
    projects:
      - "Write a Socratic dialogue on a topic you disagree about with a friend or family member."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: core
    title: "Stoics, Existentialists, and the Ethics Toolkit"
    description: "The Stoic practical philosophy (already referenced throughout the framework — now meet the primary sources): Marcus Aurelius, Meditations (selected passages). Seneca, Letters to Lucilius (selected). Epictetus, Discourses (selected). The Stoic core: distinguish what you can control from what you cannot. Focus exclusively on the former. Existentialism: Sartre (bad faith, radical freedom), Camus (the absurd, revolt), Kierkegaard (the individual vs. the crowd). The ethics toolkit formalised: utilitarianism (Mill), deontology (Kant's categorical imperative), virtue ethics (Aristotle), care ethics (Noddings). 'Which framework gives the best answer in this situation?' is the mature question."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Read Stoic primary sources, not summaries."
      - "Apply at least three ethical frameworks to the same dilemma and compare their answers."
      - "Understand that ethical reasoning is not relativism — it is rigorous analysis of competing moral claims."
    projects:
      - "Take one real ethical dilemma you face and analyse it through utilitarian, deontological, and virtue ethics lenses. Where do they agree? Where do they conflict?"
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: core
    title: "Epistemology and the Limits of Reason"
    description: "How do we know what we know? Descartes' radical doubt. Hume's problem of induction. Kant's synthesis. Popper's falsifiability (already in the mental models). Kuhn's paradigm shifts (already introduced). Wittgenstein: 'The limits of my language mean the limits of my world.' Nietzsche: the will to power, the death of God, the revaluation of values — dangerous ideas that must be encountered before they are encountered in less careful contexts. The mature question: 'What are the assumptions I cannot see? What would I have to believe to disagree with myself?'"
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Construct a personal philosophical framework that is internally consistent and honestly acknowledged."
      - "Understand that every system of thought has axioms it cannot prove — including this one."

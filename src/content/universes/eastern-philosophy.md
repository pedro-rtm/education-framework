kind: literary-canon
summary: "Daoism, Buddhism, Confucianism, and Vedantic thought — civilisational systems for understanding reality, ethics, and the self that are fundamentally different from Western frameworks."
whyItBelongs: "The framework's philosophical content is overwhelmingly Western: Stoicism, existentialism, utilitarianism, deontology. A globally literate person needs to understand that Western philosophy is ONE tradition among several, each with different axioms, different methods, and different conclusions about what matters. Daoism teaches complementary opposites and effortless action. Buddhism teaches impermanence and the constructed nature of the self. Confucianism teaches relational ethics and social harmony. These are not exotic supplements — they are alternative operating systems for the mind."

phases:
  - phaseId: age-11-13
    tier: recommended
    title: "Stories and Concepts"
    description: "Introduction through narrative and concept, not primary texts. The Tao of Pooh (Benjamin Hoff) as a gentle entry to Daoist principles through Winnie the Pooh. Buddhist stories: the parable of the poisoned arrow, the blind men and the elephant, the empty cup. Confucian concepts: respect for elders, the examined life through relationships. 'Eastern philosophy asks different questions than Western philosophy — not 'What is true?' but 'How should I live in harmony with what is?'"
    mode: absorb
    goals:
      - "Encounter a philosophical tradition with fundamentally different axioms."
      - "Understand yin-yang as the original complementary opposites model."
  - phaseId: age-14-16
    tier: core
    title: "Primary Texts"
    description: "Tao Te Ching (Lao Tzu) — Stephen Mitchell or Ursula Le Guin translation. 81 short chapters. 'The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao.' Wu wei (effortless action, non-forcing) as a complement to the framework's urgency principle. Bhagavad Gita (selected passages) — duty, action without attachment to outcome, the dialogue between Arjuna and Krishna. Buddhist Four Noble Truths and Eightfold Path: suffering exists, it has a cause, it can end, here is the path. Analects of Confucius (selections): 'What you do not wish for yourself, do not do to others' — the Golden Rule, 500 years before Christ."
    mode: dim
    goals:
      - "Read primary philosophical texts from non-Western traditions."
      - "Compare: how does Daoist wu wei relate to Stoic acceptance? How does Buddhist impermanence relate to existentialist absurdity? How does Confucian relational ethics differ from Kantian duty?"
      - "Understand that the framework's own principles are culturally situated, not universal truths."
    projects:
      - "Write a comparison: pick one life situation and analyse it through Stoic, Daoist, and Buddhist lenses. Where do they agree? Where do they diverge?"
  - phaseId: age-17-18
    tier: recommended
    title: "Integration and Critique"
    description: "Zhuangzi (selected stories) — the butterfly dream, the useless tree, Cook Ding's ox. The most playful and subversive of the Daoist texts. Heart Sutra (Buddhism): 'Form is emptiness, emptiness is form.' Diamond Sutra. Zen koans as anti-logical reasoning tools. The question is no longer 'what does Eastern philosophy teach?' but 'which insights from which traditions do I integrate into my own framework?'"
    mode: critique
    goals:
      - "Build a personal philosophical synthesis that draws from both Western and Eastern traditions."
      - "Recognise that no single philosophical tradition has a monopoly on truth."

summary: "The two great Sanskrit epics, combined length roughly ten times Homer. Dharma, kinship, catastrophic war, and the ethical density that underlies a civilisational literature."
whyItBelongs: "The Mahabharata is the longest poem in the world — roughly 100,000 verses. Its ethical density is unmatched: virtually every major character is wrong at some point, virtually every right action has a hidden cost, and the climactic eighteen-day war at Kurukshetra is won by the 'good' side only after sustained moral compromise. The Ramayana is tauter, more fairy-tale-structured — Rama's exile, Sita's abduction, the war with Ravana — but operates in the same dharmic register, where duty, desire, and consequence entangle. Together they are to the Indian subcontinent what Homer plus the Hebrew Bible are to the West. The Bhagavad Gita, chapter 6 of the Mahabharata, is one of the most widely read philosophical dialogues in human history. Pedagogical risk: these epics contain enormous violence — Kurukshetra alone. Pedagogical reward: the child gets a second complete worldview to triangulate against the Greek and Biblical ones."

    description: "Carole Satyamurti's Mahabharata: A Modern Retelling — a complete verse rendering, roughly 900 pages, the best literary English version available. Arshia Sattar's Valmiki's Ramayana translation is the scholarly standard. Begin the Bhagavad Gita here: Stephen Mitchell's or Eknath Easwaran's translation. The Gita is Arjuna on the battlefield refusing to fight his relatives and Krishna's response — the philosophical heart of the whole tradition."

      - "Identify the moments where the 'good' side commits moral compromise."
